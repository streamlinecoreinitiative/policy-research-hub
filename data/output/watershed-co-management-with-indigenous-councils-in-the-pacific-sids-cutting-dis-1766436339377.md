# Watershed Co-management with Indigenous Councils in Pacific SIDS: Enhancing Environmental Resilience through Integrated Agroforestry-Micro-Hydro Systems

## Executive Summary

This brief focuses on enhancing environmental resilience in lower-income countries by addressing water security, clean energy, and climate adaptation through watershed co-management with indigenous councils in the Pacific Small Island Developing States (SIDS). The project aims to reduce disaster losses for vulnerable groups while promoting sustainable development. By integrating agroforestry practices with micro-hydro systems, communities can improve irrigation efficiency, enhance food security, and mitigate the impacts of climate change.

## Main Sections

### Introduction to Water Scarcity in Lower-Income Countries

Climate change is exacerbating water scarcity in lower-income countries, affecting agriculture, human consumption, and economic stability. Pacific SIDS are particularly vulnerable due to their limited freshwater resources, high population growth rates, and increasing climate-related disasters. Effective watershed co-management with indigenous councils can help mitigate these challenges by promoting sustainable land use practices, improving irrigation efficiency, and enhancing community resilience.

### Agroforestry-Based Micro-Hydro Systems for Climate-Resilient Irrigation

Agroforestry practices integrate trees into agricultural landscapes to improve soil health, biodiversity, and carbon sequestration. When combined with micro-hydro systems, agroforestry can provide a sustainable source of energy for irrigation, reducing reliance on diesel generators and increasing energy efficiency. Pilot projects demonstrate the potential of integrated agroforestry-micro-hydro systems in improving water retention, reducing erosion, and enhancing food security.

### Watershed Co-management with Indigenous Councils

Indigenous councils play a crucial role in watershed co-management, as they possess traditional knowledge about local ecosystems and can effectively engage communities in conservation efforts. By involving indigenous councils in decision-making processes, projects can ensure that management strategies are tailored to the specific needs of each community. This approach also promotes social cohesion, empowers local leaders, and fosters a sense of ownership among community members.
