## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant challenges in accessing safe drinking water and electricity. According to the World Bank Open Data (2024), only 32.4% of the population has access to safely managed drinking water, compared to 73.7% globally [Source: World Bank Open Data]. The region's reliance on traditional energy sources contributes significantly to greenhouse gas emissions, exacerbating climate change impacts such as droughts and floods. Renewable energy consumption remains low at 9.2% (World Bank Open Data, 2021), underscoring the need for increased investment in clean energy technologies.

## Analysis

### Water Security

* Only 32.4% of Sub-Saharan Africa's population has access to safely managed drinking water, compared to 73.7% globally [Source: World Bank Open Data].
* Limited infrastructure and insufficient funding hinder efforts to improve water supply projects.
* Improved water security can help mitigate the impacts of droughts and floods by ensuring a steady supply of clean water during dry periods.

### Clean Energy Access

* Renewable energy consumption in Sub-Saharan Africa remains low at 9.2% (World Bank Open Data, 2021), significantly below the global average of 19.7% (World Bank Open Data, 2020).
* High upfront costs and insufficient grid infrastructure hinder widespread adoption of renewable energy technologies.
* Promoting solar photovoltaic systems for off-grid electricity generation and grid-connected wind turbines can meet growing energy demands sustainably.

### Climate Adaptation

* Sub-Saharan Africa is particularly vulnerable to climate-related shocks such as droughts and floods.
* Improved water security and enhanced access to renewable energy sources reduce reliance on fossil fuels, lowering greenhouse gas emissions and improving overall resilience.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Increase Investment in Water Infrastructure**: Governments should prioritize the construction of new water treatment plants and distribution networks, particularly in rural areas where infrastructure is most lacking. Funding from international development agencies can support these efforts.
2. **Promote Renewable Energy Technologies**: Sub-Saharan Africa should invest in solar photovoltaic systems for off-grid electricity generation and grid-connected wind turbines to meet growing energy demands sustainably. Tax incentives and grants could encourage private sector participation.
3. **Enhance Climate Change Resilience Programs**: Implement early warning systems, drought-resistant crops, and water conservation measures to bolster communities' ability to cope with climate variability.
