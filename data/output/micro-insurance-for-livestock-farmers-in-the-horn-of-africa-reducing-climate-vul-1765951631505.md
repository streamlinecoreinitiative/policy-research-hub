**Micro-insurance for Livestock Farmers in the Horn of Africa: Reducing Climate Vulnerability and Enhancing Food Security**

**Executive Summary**

Climate change is exacerbating droughts and floods in sub-Saharan Africa, severely impacting livestock farmers who rely on their herds for income. This brief outlines a micro-insurance program designed to provide a critical safety net by covering losses from extreme weather events, thus stabilizing livelihoods and ensuring food security. The program's key features include cost-sharing mechanisms, coverage details, and partnerships with local governments, non-profits, and insurance companies.

**Climate Change Impact in the Horn of Africa**

The Horn of Africa is characterized by high climate variability, with frequent droughts and floods affecting livestock farming. Rising temperatures and changing precipitation patterns are projected to worsen these conditions, further threatening the livelihoods of millions of people who depend on agriculture (FAO, 2020). In Ethiopia, Somalia, Eritrea, Djibouti, and Kenya, climate-related shocks have been shown to reduce agricultural productivity by up to 30% (World Bank, 2019).

**Designing a Micro-insurance Program for Livestock Farmers**

A micro-insurance program tailored to the needs of livestock farmers in the Horn of Africa would provide coverage against losses from extreme weather events such as droughts and floods. Key features include:

* **Cost-sharing mechanisms:** Farmers would contribute a small premium, while the government and non-profit organizations would share the remaining costs.
* **Coverage details:** Policies would cover up to 80% of livestock losses due to extreme weather events.
* **Partnerships:** Collaboration with local governments, non-profits, and insurance companies would ensure program sustainability and scalability.
