Executive Summary:

The Central America dry corridor is highly vulnerable to climate change, facing severe droughts and water scarcity that threaten agricultural production and livelihoods. This brief explores the potential of cool roofs as a cost-effective solution for urban heat mitigation in this region.

**Section 1: Climate Change Impacts on Agriculture in Central America**

The Central America dry corridor spans across six countries, including Belize, Costa Rica, El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras, Nicaragua, and Panama. This region is characterized by a tropical savanna climate with high temperatures and low rainfall during the dry season. Climate change has exacerbated these conditions, leading to more frequent and intense droughts that have severe impacts on agriculture.

**Section 2: Cool Roofs as a Climate-Resilient Solution**

Cool roofs are designed to reflect solar radiation rather than absorb it, thereby reducing the temperature of buildings and surrounding areas. Integrating cool roof technology with existing building codes and urban planning strategies can help cities reduce energy consumption, improve air quality, and enhance resilience to climate-related stress.

* Benefits of cool roofs:
	+ Reduced energy consumption
	+ Improved air quality
	+ Enhanced building durability

**Section 3: Policy Recommendations and Future Research Directions**

To support the adoption of cool roof technology in Central America, policymakers can implement the following recommendations:

1. **Develop and enforce building codes**: Incorporate cool roof requirements into national building codes to ensure widespread adoption.
2. **Provide incentives for retrofitting**: Offer financial incentives or tax credits for property owners who install cool roofs on existing buildings.
3. **Integrate urban planning strategies**: Incorporate cool roof technology into urban planning initiatives to reduce the urban heat island effect and improve air quality.
