# Watershed Co-management with Indigenous Councils in the Pacific SIDS: Supporting Adaptation Targets in NDCs

## Executive Summary

Climate change poses significant challenges to the environmental resilience of lower-income countries, particularly in Small Island Developing States (SIDS) like those in the Pacific region. This brief focuses on the importance of watershed co-management with indigenous councils as a key strategy for supporting adaptation targets outlined in Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). By combining micro-insurance schemes and renewable energy-powered irrigation systems, communities can enhance their resilience to climate-related shocks while promoting sustainable water management practices.

## Environmental Resilience in Lower-Income Countries: Challenges and Opportunities

Climate variability significantly impacts agriculture and water security in the Ethiopian Highlands, where this brief's original context was drawn from. In many Pacific SIDS, similar challenges are being faced due to rising temperatures, changing precipitation patterns, and increased frequency of extreme weather events. The consequences include reduced crop yields, decreased food security, and increased poverty rates among rural communities.

### Key Challenges:

*   Climate-related shocks (droughts, floods) impact agricultural productivity
*   Limited access to financial resources for farmers to recover from disasters
*   Inefficient water management practices exacerbate the effects of climate change

## Watershed Co-management with Indigenous Councils: A Solution

Watershed co-management involves collaborative efforts between local communities and indigenous councils to manage natural resources sustainably. This approach can help build resilience in several ways:

### Benefits:

*   Micro-insurance schemes provide financial support during climatic shocks
*   Renewable energy-powered irrigation systems improve water efficiency and reduce operational costs
*   Community-led initiatives foster a sense of ownership and responsibility for environmental conservation

## Case Studies from Pacific SIDS: Lessons Learned

Several countries in the Pacific region have successfully implemented watershed co-management programs, including:

### Examples:

*   Fiji's community-based forest management initiative has improved livelihoods and protected biodiversity
*   Vanuatu's climate-resilient agriculture project has increased crop yields and reduced poverty rates among farmers

## Challenges and Solutions: Scaling Up Climate Resilience Programs

Despite the benefits of watershed co-management, several challenges need to be addressed:

### Common Challenges:

*   Limited resources for program implementation and scaling up
*   Community acceptance and engagement in co-management initiatives
*   Integration with existing policies and programs at national and local levels

## Policy Recommendations: Supporting Adaptation Targets in NDCs

Governments and international organizations can support the expansion of watershed co-management programs by:

### Actions/Recommendations:

1.  Provide technical assistance and capacity-building for indigenous councils and local communities
2.  Offer financial incentives and resources to scale up program implementation
3.  Develop policies and regulations that support community-led initiatives and promote sustainable natural resource management

## Closing Outlook: Enhancing Environmental Resilience in Lower-Income Countries

Watershed co-management with indigenous councils offers a promising solution for enhancing environmental resilience in lower-income countries, particularly in Pacific SIDS. By combining micro-insurance schemes and renewable energy-powered irrigation systems, communities can build their capacity to adapt to climate-related shocks while promoting sustainable water management practices. With the right support from governments and international organizations, these initiatives can be scaled up to achieve significant impacts on environmental resilience and poverty reduction.
