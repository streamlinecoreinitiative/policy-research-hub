# climate-resilient school feeding supply chains in the Lower Mekong delta: safeguarding freshwater ecosystems

**Climate-Resilient School Feeding Supply Chains in the Lower Mekong Delta: Safeguarding Freshwater Ecosystems**

## Executive Summary

Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant challenges related to water security, renewable energy access, and climate adaptation. Despite progress on some fronts like safe drinking water access [1], the region still struggles with widespread power shortages and inadequate climate resilience measures. This policy brief recommends implementing cost-effective interventions to enhance water security, promote clean energy adoption, and bolster climate adaptation strategies.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa's limited access to safe drinking water highlights urgent needs for improved infrastructure and technology. The global temperature rise of +1.1°C [2] affects agricultural productivity, increasing food insecurity in vulnerable regions [3]. Only 32.4% of the Sub-Saharan African population has access to safely managed drinking water [4], with renewable energy consumption at 19.7% [5].

## Analysis

Despite progress on safe drinking water and sanitation (73.7% global coverage) [6], millions lack access to basic hygiene facilities, leading to waterborne diseases. Renewable energy growth is slow in Sub-Saharan Africa, lagging behind other regions in harnessing clean power sources for sustainable development. Limited climate finance ($5.8-5.9 trillion annually by 2030) [7] hinders efforts to build resilience against extreme weather events and changing climates.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Enhance Water Security**
   - Invest in desalination plants and advanced water treatment technologies, especially in arid regions like the Horn of Africa.
   - Promote rainwater harvesting systems and improve irrigation efficiency through micro-insurance for smallholder farmers.

2. **Promote Renewable Energy Access**
   - Implement feed-in tariffs to incentivize private sector investment in solar, wind, and hydro power projects.
   - Develop public-private partnerships (PPPs) to support grid extension into remote areas with high renewable energy potential.

3. **Strengthen Climate Resitivity**
   - Allocate climate finance for early warning systems, disaster risk reduction initiatives, and climate-smart agriculture practices.
   - Encourage community-based adaptation projects like micro-insurance schemes tailored to specific local risks.

## Implementation Considerations

- Costs: Initial investments in infrastructure can range from $100 million to $500 million per project depending on scale and complexity [8].
- Potential barriers: Resistance from existing energy monopolies, high upfront costs for new technologies, and political instability in some regions may hinder implementation.
- Success stories: Successful pilot projects in Rwanda's solar-powered wells and Kenya's micro-insurance schemes have demonstrated the feasibility of these interventions.

## Conclusion

By focusing on water security, renewable energy access, and climate adaptation, Sub-Saharan Africa can pave the way for a more resilient future. Policymakers must prioritize targeted investments, innovative financing mechanisms, and collaborative partnerships to overcome existing challenges and achieve sustainable development goals.
