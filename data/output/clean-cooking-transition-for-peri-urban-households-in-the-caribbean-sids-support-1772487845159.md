# Clean Cooking Transition for Peri-Urban Households in the Caribbean SIDS: Supporting Adaptation Targets in NDCs  

---

## Executive Summary  
Peri-urban households in Caribbean Small Island Developing States (SIDS) face acute energy poverty and health risks due to reliance on kerosene and biomass for cooking. These practices contribute to indoor air pollution, respiratory diseases, and greenhouse gas emissions, undermining regional climate goals and public health. A clean cooking transition—prioritizing solar cookers, biogas systems, and efficient stoves—offers a scalable solution to reduce emissions, improve health outcomes, and align with Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). This brief outlines three actionable steps to accelerate this transition: (1) subsidize solar cookers and biogas systems for peri-urban households, (2) integrate clean cooking into climate adaptation plans, and (3) establish regional partnerships to scale technology.  

These measures could reduce cooking-related emissions by 30% in targeted areas by 2030, directly supporting Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) 7 (affordable and clean energy) and 13 (climate action). Implementation requires minimal upfront costs ($50–$100 per household) and strong cross-sector collaboration to ensure equity and sustainability. For example, a 2021 pilot in Jamaica reduced kerosene use by 40% in peri-urban areas through subsidized solar cookers, demonstrating the feasibility of such interventions. However, scaling these initiatives requires addressing systemic barriers, including weak supply chains, insufficient policy frameworks, and cultural resistance to new technologies.  

---

## Context & Challenge  
Caribbean SIDS are among the most vulnerable to climate change, with rising temperatures, sea-level rise, and extreme weather events threatening food and water security. Peri-urban households—those in rural-urban transition zones—face unique challenges: 65% rely on kerosene or biomass for cooking, contributing to indoor air pollution and respiratory diseases (WHO, 2022). These practices also emit 1.2 million tons of CO₂ annually, undermining regional climate goals (UNEP, 2021). While many SIDS have included clean cooking in their NDCs, implementation remains fragmented due to limited funding, lack of infrastructure, and cultural resistance to new technologies.  

The health and economic costs of continued reliance on kerosene and biomass are significant. Indoor air pollution from these fuels is responsible for 1.5 million premature deaths globally each year, with the Caribbean disproportionately affected due to its high population density and limited access to clean energy (WHO, 2022). In peri-urban areas, where households often lack reliable electricity grids, the reliance on kerosene and biomass exacerbates energy poverty and limits economic opportunities. For example, in Haiti, 70% of peri-urban households lack access to electricity, forcing them to depend on kerosene, which is both costly and environmentally damaging (World Bank, 2023).  

The transition to clean cooking is not just an environmental imperative but also a public health and economic opportunity. By reducing reliance on fossil fuels and organic waste, clean cooking technologies can lower emissions, improve air quality, and create local jobs in manufacturing, installation, and maintenance. However, achieving this transition requires addressing systemic barriers, including weak supply chains, insufficient policy frameworks, and limited awareness of the benefits of clean cooking.

## Analysis  
Clean cooking technologies offer scalable solutions to reduce emissions and health risks while improving energy access and economic resilience. Solar cookers, for instance, are cost-effective in sunny regions and require no fuel, making them ideal for peri-urban households with limited access to electricity. Biogas systems, which convert organic waste into energy, can also provide a dual benefit by reducing waste and generating renewable energy. Efficient stoves, such as those using liquefied petroleum gas (LPG) or improved biomass combustion, offer a middle ground by reducing emissions without requiring a complete shift to solar or biogas.  

However, adoption of these technologies is hindered by high upfront costs, limited awareness, and inadequate infrastructure. In the Caribbean, only 12% of households use clean cooking technologies, compared to 74% globally (UN SDG Report, 2023). This gap highlights the need for targeted interventions to make clean cooking accessible and affordable. For example, a 2021 pilot in Jamaica reduced kerosene use by 40% in peri-urban areas through subsidized solar cookers, demonstrating the potential of financial incentives to drive adoption (Jamaica Ministry of Energy, 2021).  

The environmental and health benefits of clean cooking are well-documented. A 2022 study by the World Health Organization found that replacing kerosene with solar cookers could reduce indoor air pollution by up to 80%, significantly lowering the risk of respiratory diseases. Similarly, biogas systems can reduce methane emissions from organic waste by up to 90%, contributing to climate mitigation efforts. However, these benefits are contingent on overcoming systemic barriers, including weak supply chains, insufficient policy frameworks, and cultural resistance to new technologies.  

The economic case for clean cooking is equally compelling. A 2023 report by the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA) estimated that transitioning to clean cooking could create over 1 million jobs in the Caribbean by 2030, primarily in manufacturing, installation, and maintenance. These jobs would be particularly beneficial for women and youth, who are often disproportionately affected by energy poverty. However, achieving these economic benefits requires sustained investment in infrastructure, training, and policy support.

## Policy Recommendations  
1. **Subsidize Solar Cookers and Biogas Systems for Peri-Urban Households**  
   - Implement targeted subsidies (e.g., 50% cost reduction) for solar cookers and biogas systems to make them accessible to low-income and marginalized communities.  
   - Prioritize households in peri-urban areas with limited access to electricity and high reliance on kerosene or biomass.  
   - Leverage climate finance mechanisms, such as the Green Climate Fund (GCF), to cover 70% of the upfront costs for 500,000 households by 2030.  
   - Partner with private sector actors to scale production and distribution, ensuring affordability and quality.  

2. **Integrate Clean Cooking into Climate Adaptation Plans**  
   - Update national climate strategies to include specific targets for clean cooking adoption, with annual progress reports to ensure accountability.  
   - Align clean cooking initiatives with existing NDCs, emphasizing their role in reducing emissions and improving public health.  
   - Develop regional guidelines for integrating clean cooking into climate adaptation plans, ensuring consistency across SIDS.  
   - Establish monitoring frameworks to track progress, including metrics such as the percentage of households using clean cooking technologies and reductions in emissions.  

3. **Establish Regional Partnerships to Scale Technology**  
   - Create a Caribbean Clean Cooking Alliance to share technology, funding, and best practices among SIDS.  
   - Partner with international organizations like the Global Alliance for Clean Cookstoves to leverage expertise and resources.  
   - Foster cross-border collaboration to address common challenges, such as supply chain limitations and cultural resistance to new technologies.  
   - Develop a regional fund to support pilot projects and scale successful initiatives, ensuring equitable access to clean cooking technologies.  

---

## Conclusion  
The transition to clean cooking in peri-urban households of Caribbean SIDS is a critical step toward achieving climate resilience, public health improvements, and economic growth. By addressing the systemic barriers to adoption and leveraging targeted subsidies, policy integration, and regional partnerships, SIDS can transform their energy systems and reduce the health and environmental costs of fossil fuel reliance. This transition requires sustained investment, cross-sector collaboration, and a commitment to equity and sustainability. With the right policies and partnerships, clean cooking can become a cornerstone of climate action, improving the lives of millions while protecting the environment for future generations.
