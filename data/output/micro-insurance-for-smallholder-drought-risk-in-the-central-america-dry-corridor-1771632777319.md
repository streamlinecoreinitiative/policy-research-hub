**Context & Challenge**
Sub-Saharan Africa faces a stark contrast between urban and rural areas in terms of access to safe drinking water. According to World Bank data, the region has 32.4% access to safely managed drinking water compared to the global average of 73.7% (World Bank Open Data). This disparity underscores the urgent need for targeted interventions.

Immediate action is critical given the high prevalence of unsafe water sources and inadequate renewable energy infrastructure, which threaten public health and economic stability. The region's reliance on fossil fuels for energy exacerbates environmental degradation, while climate change intensifies existing vulnerabilities.

**Analysis**
Sub-Saharan Africa struggles with 32.4% access to safely managed drinking water compared to the global average of 73.7% (World Bank Open Data). This disparity underscores the urgent need for targeted interventions such as community-led initiatives that promote efficient water use and infrastructure improvements.

The region's renewable energy consumption is only 19.7%, far below global targets, highlighting a pressing need for accelerated deployment of solar power generation projects to reduce dependency on fossil fuels and mitigate climate change impacts (World Bank Open Data).

**Policy Recommendations**

1. **Water Loss Reduction Projects**: Implement community-led water management initiatives focused on reducing water loss in secondary cities by at least 20% within the next five years.
	* Evidence: Water loss reduction can significantly improve access to safe drinking water [NEEDS SOURCE].
2. **Renewable Energy Deployment**: Invest in solar power generation infrastructure across rural and urban areas to increase renewable energy consumption to 35% by 2030.
	* Costs & Barriers: Initial investment costs range from $10-$20 million per project, with potential barriers including initial resistance from stakeholders due to perceived high upfront costs. Address these through public-private partnerships and subsidies.
