**Micro-insurance for Smallholder Drought Risk in the Caribbean SIDS: Lowering Emissions from Critical Sectors**

## Executive Summary

In the face of increasing climate-related droughts and water scarcity, smallholder farmers in the Caribbean Small Island Developing States (SIDS) are at risk of crop failure and economic loss. To address this challenge, we recommend implementing micro-insurance programs to mitigate drought risks for these vulnerable communities. By investing $1 billion over 5 years, governments and NGOs can provide critical support to smallholders, enhancing their resilience and reducing greenhouse gas emissions from agriculture.

## Context & Challenge

The Caribbean SIDS are highly susceptible to climate change impacts, including droughts, sea-level rise, and extreme weather events (IPCC, 2019). The consequences are severe: crop failures, reduced yields, and economic losses for smallholders.

## Analysis

The current approach to drought management in the Caribbean SIDS relies heavily on traditional coping mechanisms, such as rainwater harvesting and conservation agriculture. However, these methods have limitations in addressing the scale and frequency of droughts (FAO, 2019). Micro-insurance programs can provide a more effective solution by offering financial protection against crop failures and allowing smallholders to invest in climate-resilient agricultural practices.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Establish micro-insurance programs for smallholder farmers**: Governments and NGOs should work together to develop and implement micro-insurance schemes that cover drought-related risks.
2. **Increase funding for climate-resilient agriculture**: Allocate $200 million annually over 5 years to support smallholders in adopting climate-resilient agricultural practices, such as agroforestry and conservation agriculture.
3. **Enhance early warning systems**: Invest in satellite-based early warning systems to provide timely alerts on droughts and extreme weather events.

## Implementation Considerations

* Allocate $1 billion over 5 years for micro-insurance programs and climate-resilient agriculture initiatives.
* Establish partnerships with insurance companies, NGOs, and smallholder organizations to deliver micro-insurance products.
* Develop a monitoring and evaluation framework to assess the effectiveness of micro-insurance programs.

## Conclusion

The Caribbean SIDS face significant drought-related risks that threaten food security, economic stability, and greenhouse gas emissions. By implementing micro-insurance programs for smallholder farmers, governments and NGOs can provide critical support to these vulnerable communities, enhancing their resilience and reducing emissions from agriculture. We urge policymakers to prioritize this initiative and invest in the future of Caribbean SIDS.
