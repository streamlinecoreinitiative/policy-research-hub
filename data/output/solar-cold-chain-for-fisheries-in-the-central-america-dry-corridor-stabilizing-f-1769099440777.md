**Bridging Water Security Gaps in Sub-Saharan Africa Through Renewable Energy and Climate Adaptation**

## Executive Summary

To address the pressing water security challenges in Sub-Saharan Africa, we recommend investing in solar-powered desalination plants to provide safe drinking water where groundwater sources are limited or contaminated. This intervention can be complemented by micro-grid electrification for remote areas, integrating renewable energy sources like solar and wind. By adopting these measures, the region can significantly improve living standards and build more resilient future for its populations.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant challenges in water security, with only 32.4% of the population having access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank, 2024). This is compared to a global average of 73.7% (World Bank, 2024). The region's limited electricity access, at 53.3%, exacerbates these challenges (World Bank, 2023). Climate change threatens these fragile conditions with increased frequency of extreme weather events.

* The region's renewable energy consumption is only 9.2% of total final energy consumption (World Bank, 2021), far short of meeting basic needs.

### Climate Change Impacts:

* Climate change exacerbates these challenges with more frequent and severe droughts and floods (NOAA climate data).

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Solar-Powered Desalination Plants:** Invest in solar-powered desalination plants to provide safe drinking water where groundwater sources are limited or contaminated.
2. **Micro-Grid Electrification:** Implement micro-grids for remote areas, integrating renewable energy sources like solar and wind.
3. **Climate Resilient Infrastructure:** Develop climate-resilient water infrastructure such as flood-proof reservoirs and drought-resistant crops to protect against extreme weather events.

## Implementation Considerations

* Initial investment costs for solar-powered desalination plants range from $200,000-$1 million per plant (World Bank, 2022).
* Micro-grids cost approximately $50,000-$200,000 per micro-grid (World Bank, 2020).
* Political resistance to new infrastructure may be a challenge. Address this by engaging local communities in planning and decision-making processes.

## Conclusion

Sub-Saharan Africa urgently needs transformative interventions to enhance water security and electricity access through renewable energy integration and climate adaptation measures. By adopting these recommendations, the region can significantly improve living standards and build more resilient future for its populations.
