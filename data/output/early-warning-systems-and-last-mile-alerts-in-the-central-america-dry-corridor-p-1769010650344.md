# early-warning systems and last-mile alerts in the Central America dry corridor: protecting livelihoods under climate stress

**Early-Warning Systems and Last-Mile Alerts in the Central America Dry Corridor: Protecting Livelihoods under Climate Stress**

## Executive Summary

Sub-Saharan Africa is facing significant challenges related to water security, clean energy access, and climate adaptation. With only 32.4% of the population having access to safely managed drinking water [Source: World Bank Open Data (2024)], and a mere 53.3% accessing electricity [Source: World Bank Open Data (2023)], there is an urgent need for targeted interventions that not only improve basic services but also build long-term resilience against climate shocks. This policy brief recommends implementing early warning systems and last-mile alerts in the Central America dry corridor to protect livelihoods under climate stress.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is one of the most vulnerable regions to climate change due to its reliance on natural resources like water and electricity [Source: World Bank Open Data (2024)]. The region's limited infrastructure makes it particularly susceptible to disruptions from droughts, floods, and other extreme weather events. According to the World Bank Open Data (2024), only 73.7% of the world has access to safely managed drinking water, compared to just 32.4% in Sub-Saharan Africa [Source: World Bank Open Data (2024)]. Rising temperatures and changing precipitation patterns threaten existing infrastructure, necessitating urgent adaptation measures.

## Analysis

Despite efforts, progress remains slow. For example, only 53.3% of the population in Sub-Saharan Africa has access to electricity [Source: World Bank Open Data (2023)], compared to 91.6% globally [Source: World Bank Open Data (2023)]. The global population lacking safe water is estimated at 2.2 billion people [Source: UN-Water/WHO (2022)]. Climate change exacerbates these challenges, making it essential to develop targeted interventions that address the root causes of vulnerability.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Implement Early Warning Systems**: Develop early warning systems for droughts and floods using satellite data and IoT sensors.
	* Invest in rainwater harvesting and desalination technologies for remote areas.
	* Implement water pricing policies to encourage conservation and reduce waste.
2. **Enhance Clean Energy Access**: Promote solar energy projects that can be deployed quickly, especially through off-grid systems.
	* Encourage public-private partnerships to finance clean energy initiatives.
3. **Foster Climate Resilience**: Develop climate-resilient infrastructure designs in critical sectors such as agriculture and transportation.

## Implementation Considerations

Practical steps include initiating pilot projects with small grants to test new technologies and business models. Initial investments will vary by project but are expected to yield long-term savings through reduced resource dependency and enhanced resilience. Potential barriers include political resistance, lack of skilled labor, and limited funding.

## Conclusion

The Central America dry corridor is one of the most vulnerable regions to climate change due to its reliance on natural resources like water and electricity. Implementing early warning systems and last-mile alerts can protect livelihoods under climate stress. Governments, NGOs, and development practitioners must work together to implement these recommendations to build a more resilient Sub-Saharan Africa.
