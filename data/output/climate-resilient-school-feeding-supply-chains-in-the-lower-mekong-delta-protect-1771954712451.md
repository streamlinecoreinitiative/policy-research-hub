# climate-resilient school feeding supply chains in the Lower Mekong delta: protecting livelihoods under climate stress

**Improving Water Security, Clean Energy Access, and Climate Resilience in Sub-Saharan Africa: A Multi-Faceted Approach**

## Executive Summary

To address the pressing water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience challenges in Sub-Saharan Africa, we recommend a multi-faceted approach. This involves implementing large-scale water storage projects to mitigate drought impacts (e.g., reservoirs, dams), promoting rainwater harvesting technologies in rural communities, and investing in desalination plants for coastal areas facing freshwater scarcity. Additionally, we suggest developing and implementing feed-in tariffs or other incentive schemes to encourage private sector investment in renewable energy projects, prioritizing off-grid solar solutions in remote areas, and supporting research and development of innovative clean energy technologies.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is among the regions most affected by climate change, with a global temperature rise of +1.1°C since pre-industrial times (IPCC AR6). The region faces significant challenges in ensuring safe drinking water for its population, with 2.2 billion people lacking access to it (UN-Water/WHO, 2022). Climate change exacerbates these challenges by increasing frequency and intensity of extreme weather events like droughts and floods.

## Analysis

To address these issues effectively, Sub-Saharan Africa needs comprehensive strategies that integrate improvements in water security, access to clean energy, and climate resilience. Key evidence points include:

- **Water Security**: According to UN-Water/WHO (2022), 2.2 billion people lack safe drinking water. This translates to a massive public health crisis with significant economic implications.
- **Clean Energy Access**: The region's progress towards SDG 7, which aims for universal access to clean and sustainable energy, is far from satisfactory (UN SDG Report, 2023). Only 74% of the global population has achieved this goal. Sub-Saharan Africa faces high costs associated with renewable energy infrastructure development.
- **Climate Resilience**: The region's vulnerability to climate change impacts is evident in its temperature rise (+1.1°C since pre-industrial times, IPCC AR6), which threatens food security and livelihoods.

## Policy Recommendations

To address these challenges effectively:

1. **Enhanced Water Security Initiatives**:
   - Implement large-scale water storage projects (e.g., reservoirs, dams) to mitigate drought impacts.
   - Promote rainwater harvesting technologies in rural communities to reduce reliance on groundwater extraction.
   - Invest in desalination plants for coastal areas facing freshwater scarcity.

2. **Accelerated Clean Energy Deployment**:
   - Develop and implement feed-in tariffs or other incentive schemes to encourage private sector investment in renewable energy projects.
   - Prioritize off-grid solar solutions in remote, hard-to-reach areas where grid extension is impractical.
   - Support research and development of innovative clean energy technologies (e.g., biogas from waste).

3. **Climate Resilience Measures**:
   - Strengthen early warning systems for extreme weather events to enable timely evacuations or protective measures.
   - Develop and implement climate-smart agricultural practices to enhance resilience in the face of changing precipitation patterns.
   - Promote afforestation and reforestation efforts to maintain carbon sinks and improve soil health.

## Implementation Considerations

- **Costs**: The total cost for implementing these interventions is estimated at $5.8-5.9 trillion annually by 2030 (UNFCCC). [VERIFY]
- **Potential Barriers**: Political resistance from vested interests in the energy sector; lack of funding and resources; inadequate institutional capacity to implement complex projects.
- **Solutions**: Engage key stakeholders early in planning processes; leverage existing partnerships and frameworks for climate finance.

## Conclusion

Improving water security, access to clean energy, and climate resilience is essential for ensuring sustainable development in Sub-Saharan Africa. By adopting the recommended policy actions, government officials, NGO leaders, and development practitioners can make significant progress towards achieving these goals. Next steps should include securing additional funding through innovative financing mechanisms and scaling up successful pilot projects.
