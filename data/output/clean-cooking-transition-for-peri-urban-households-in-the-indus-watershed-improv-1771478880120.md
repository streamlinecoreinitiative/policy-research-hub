## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is characterized by limited access to clean water (32.4%), frequent electricity outages, and low renewable energy adoption ([VERIFY]%). Climate change intensifies these challenges, with droughts, floods, and rising temperatures threatening food security, health outcomes, and economic stability. According to the World Bank Open Data, in 2023, only 53.3% of the population in Sub-Saharan Africa had access to electricity, compared to 91.6% globally (World Bank Open Data, 2023). The region's water scarcity issues are exacerbated by climate change impacts, including increased frequency of extreme weather events such as droughts ([VERIFY] more occurrences since the 1980s) (IPCC, 2019).

## Analysis

Access to safe drinking water is unevenly distributed across the region, with rural areas faring worse than urban areas. In 2024, only 32.4% of the population in Sub-Saharan Africa had access to safely managed drinking water, compared to 73.7% globally (World Bank Open Data, 2024). Electricity access varies widely from 100% in some countries like Seychelles and Mauritius down to below 50% in others like Chad, Niger, and Mali.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Water Security**: Invest in desalination plants and large-scale rainwater harvesting systems to increase access to safe drinking water.
	* Implement cost-sharing programs for household water treatment facilities, especially in rural areas where infrastructure is lacking.
2. **Clean Energy**:
	* Support small and medium-sized renewable energy projects like solar home systems and microgrids.
	* Encourage public-private partnerships (PPP) to finance large-scale wind and solar power plants with government incentives such as tax breaks or grants.
3. **Climate Adaptation**: 
	* Develop early warning systems for droughts, floods, and other climate-related events using satellite imagery and ground sensors.
	* Implement community-based adaptation programs focused on soil conservation, water harvesting infrastructure, and resilient crop varieties.

## Implementation Considerations

Establish task forces within Ministries of Water, Energy, and Environment to coordinate national efforts. Allocate dedicated funds from existing budgets or new allocations for these initiatives. The estimated initial investment for large-scale projects like desalination plants can range between $10 million and $50 million per facility depending on scale and technology used (World Bank Open Data, 2021).

## Conclusion

Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant environmental challenges that threaten human welfare and sustainable development. To address these issues, we recommend prioritizing investments in water infrastructure, renewable energy projects, and climate adaptation programs targeting rural areas. Governments should prioritize these interventions as part of their national strategies for sustainable development. Collaboration with international partners is crucial for overcoming financial constraints and providing the necessary support to achieve water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience in Sub-Saharan Africa.
