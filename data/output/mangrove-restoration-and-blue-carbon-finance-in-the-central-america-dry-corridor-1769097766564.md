**Mangrove Restoration and Blue Carbon Finance in the Central America Dry Corridor: Lowering Emissions from Critical Sectors**

## Executive Summary

This policy brief advocates for integrating mangrove restoration and blue carbon finance into national development plans to reduce greenhouse gas emissions in the Central America dry corridor. By investing $10 million annually over five years, governments can leverage international funding mechanisms and private sector investments to protect critical ecosystems while promoting sustainable livelihoods.

## Context & Challenge

The Central America dry corridor is one of the most vulnerable regions to climate change, with frequent droughts, floods, and heatwaves impacting agriculture, water security, and human health. The region's mangrove forests are crucial for coastal protection, fisheries, and biodiversity conservation but face significant threats from deforestation, overfishing, and pollution. With only 32.4% of the population having access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank, 2024), addressing these challenges requires comprehensive policies that integrate ecosystem-based adaptation measures.

## Analysis

- **Mangrove Forests:** The Central America dry corridor is home to approximately 1 million hectares of mangrove forests, providing critical ecosystem services including coastal protection, fisheries, and carbon sequestration (UNEP, 2020). However, these ecosystems face significant threats from deforestation, overfishing, and pollution.
- **Water Security:** Sub-Saharan Africa struggles with limited access to safe drinking water, exacerbating environmental vulnerabilities and economic disparities. This disparity is particularly acute in rural areas where many rely on contaminated surface water and groundwater (World Bank, 2024).
- **Climate Change Impacts:** The Central America dry corridor experiences frequent droughts, floods, and heatwaves, impacting agriculture, water security, and human health (IPCC, 2019). Addressing these challenges requires robust policies that integrate ecosystem-based adaptation measures.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Mangrove Restoration:**
   - Develop a national mangrove restoration plan with clear targets for forest area recovery and community engagement.
   - Allocate $5 million annually over five years for direct support to local communities, including funding for sustainable livelihoods and ecosystem services.

2. **Blue Carbon Finance:**
   - Establish a blue carbon fund to leverage international funding mechanisms and private sector investments.
   - Develop a framework for certifying and trading blue carbon credits, ensuring transparency and accountability.

3. **Community Engagement and Capacity Building:**
   - Empower local communities through training on sustainable forest management practices and ecosystem-based adaptation measures.
   - Encourage public-private partnerships for financing large-scale projects, leveraging government grants and international development funds.

## Implementation Considerations

- **Costs:** Estimated costs for implementing these interventions range from $10 million to $50 million annually, depending on project scale and complexity. Funding will come from a combination of national budgets, donor contributions, and private sector investments.
- **Barriers:** Potential challenges include political resistance to environmental policies, lack of institutional capacity in rural areas, and high upfront costs for some infrastructure projects.

## Conclusion

Addressing the interconnected issues of water security, clean energy access, and climate adaptation is crucial for fostering resilience in Sub-Saharan Africa. By integrating mangrove restoration and blue carbon finance into national development plans, governments can promote sustainable livelihoods while reducing greenhouse gas emissions from critical sectors. Let us take decisive action now to secure a more sustainable future for all.
