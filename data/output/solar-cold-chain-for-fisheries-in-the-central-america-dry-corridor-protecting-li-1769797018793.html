Solar Cold-Chain for Fisheries in the Central America Dry Corridor: Protecting Livelihoods under Climate Stress

Executive Summary

To address the pressing issue of climate stress on fisheries livelihoods in the Central America dry corridor, this policy brief recommends investing in solar-powered cold-chains for fish storage and transportation. This intervention is crucial due to the region's limited access to electricity (53.3%)[1] and water scarcity (32.4%)[2], which exacerbate the impacts of climate variability on fisheries.

Context & Challenge

The Central America dry corridor, spanning across countries such as Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador, is characterized by frequent droughts and floods[3]. These extreme events severely impact fisheries livelihoods, affecting over 1.5 million people in the region[4]. The lack of access to electricity (53.3%)[1] and safe drinking water (32.4%)[2] further hampers efforts to adapt to climate change.

Analysis

Climate variability has significant implications for fisheries productivity, with droughts affecting fish populations and floods contaminating water sources[5]. In the Central America dry corridor, freshwater withdrawal from surface sources is limited (9.2%)[6], making sustainable water management practices essential. Investing in renewable energy, such as solar power, can reduce carbon emissions and increase energy access.

Policy Recommendations

1. Promote Solar-Powered Cold-Chains: - Invest in solar-powered cold-chains for fish storage and transportation. - Implement climate-resilient infrastructure, including flood-resistant roads and buildings.

  • Improve Water Management:
- Develop drought-resistant crops and irrigation methods. - Expand desalination plants where feasible.
  • Enhance Climate Resilience:
- Build early warning systems for extreme weather events. - Promote community-based disaster preparedness initiatives.

Implementation Considerations

Implementing these recommendations requires careful planning and coordination among various stakeholders, including governments, NGOs, and local communities. Key considerations include:

- Cost: Initial investments in renewable energy infrastructure can be significant but pay off through reduced long-term costs and increased resilience.

  • Barriers: Limited access to finance, technical expertise, and skilled labor may hinder implementation.

By investing in solar-powered cold-chains, improving water management practices, and enhancing climate resilience, countries can build a future where their people thrive despite environmental challenges.
