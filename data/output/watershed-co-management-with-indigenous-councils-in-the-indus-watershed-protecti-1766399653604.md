# Watershed Co-management with Indigenous Councils in the Indus Watershed: Protecting Livelihoods Under Climate Stress

## Executive Summary

Climate change exacerbates water scarcity in lower-income countries, threatening the livelihoods of millions who depend on agriculture and natural resources for survival. The Indus watershed, spanning across several South Asian countries, is particularly vulnerable to climate-related stressors. This brief argues that co-management of watersheds with indigenous councils can provide a sustainable solution to water security, clean energy, and climate adaptation challenges.

## Current Water Management Practices

Climate change impacts agriculture by altering precipitation patterns, leading to erratic rainfall and increased evaporation rates (precipitation variability range: 600-1400 mm). Traditional irrigation systems in the Indus watershed are characterized by high water loss percentages from boreholes and canals (evaporation: 30%, seepage: 5%). These methods are energy-intensive, costly, and unsustainable.
