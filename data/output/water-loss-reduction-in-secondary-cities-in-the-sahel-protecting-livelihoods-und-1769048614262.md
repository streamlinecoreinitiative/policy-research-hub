# water loss reduction in secondary cities in the Sahel: protecting livelihoods under climate stress

**Bridging Water Security, Clean Energy, and Climate Resilience in Sub-Saharan Africa: A Comprehensive Approach**

## Executive Summary

To address the pressing challenges of water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience in Sub-Saharan Africa, we recommend that governments prioritize investing in renewable energy infrastructure, enhancing water management systems, and promoting community-led initiatives. Specifically, we propose:

* Expanding renewable energy infrastructure to increase renewable energy consumption by at least 20% within five years.
* Enhancing water management systems through watershed co-management with indigenous councils for sustainable water resource management.
* Promoting community-led initiatives, such as micro-insurance programs to stabilize food security during droughts.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant challenges in water security and clean energy access. According to the World Bank data (2024), only 32.4% of the population has access to safely managed drinking water [1], compared to a world average of 73.7% [2]. In terms of electricity, less than half of the population has access, with a regional average of 53.3%, well below the global target of 91.6% [3].

The region's climate resilience efforts are also far from adequate, with only 74% of the global population covered by safe drinking water (SDG 6 target) [4]. These numbers underscore the urgent need for targeted interventions to enhance both environmental resilience and socioeconomic development in the region.

## Analysis

Access to safe drinking water and clean energy are critical for health and economic stability in Sub-Saharan Africa. The World Bank reports that only 32.4% of Sub-Saharan African residents have access to safely managed drinking water, while 53.3% rely on unreliable electricity sources [1]. Inadequate water security and limited renewable energy exacerbate climate vulnerabilities, including droughts and extreme weather events.

Recent studies show that Sub-Saharan Africa's climate resilience efforts are far from adequate, with only 74% of the global population covered by safe drinking water (SDG 6 target) [4]. These numbers underscore the urgent need for targeted interventions to enhance both environmental resilience and socioeconomic development in the region.

## Policy Recommendations

To address these challenges, policymakers should prioritize:

1. **Expand Renewable Energy Infrastructure**: Invest in solar, wind, and hydroelectric projects to increase renewable energy consumption by at least 20% within five years.
2. **Enhance Water Management Systems**: Implement watershed co-management with indigenous councils for sustainable water resource management. Focus on improving groundwater recharge techniques and expanding rainwater harvesting systems.
3. **Promote Community-led Initiatives**: Support micro-insurance programs to stabilize food security during droughts, particularly in rural communities.

## Implementation Considerations

For successful implementation:

* **Funding**: Secure funding from international development agencies for renewable energy projects and water management initiatives. Allocate resources based on need.
* **Capacity Building**: Train local technicians and community leaders in sustainable water resource management and renewable energy technologies to ensure long-term sustainability.
* **Policy Frameworks**: Develop and enforce policies that incentivize private sector investment in clean energy and water infrastructure, such as tax breaks for renewable energy projects.

## Conclusion

Sub-Saharan Africa must urgently address its critical challenges of water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience. By implementing evidence-based recommendations outlined in this brief, governments can safeguard the region's future prosperity and health while contributing to global sustainability goals. The time to act is now.
