Flood-Resilient Transport Corridors in the Great Lakes Region: Protecting Livelihoods Under Climate Stress

Executive Summary

The Great Lakes region, spanning six countries—Burundi, the Democratic Republic of the Congo (DRC), Kenya, Rwanda, Tanzania, and Uganda—is increasingly vulnerable to climate change impacts, including recurrent flooding, extreme weather events, and rising temperatures. These challenges threaten the region’s economic stability, food security, and human well-being, particularly for communities reliant on transport networks for trade, healthcare, and education. Climate projections indicate that flood frequency in the region could increase by 30–50% by 2050, exacerbating existing infrastructure weaknesses and displacing millions.

Flood-resilient transport corridors are critical to mitigating these risks. By integrating climate adaptation strategies into infrastructure planning, governments can protect livelihoods, reduce disaster-related economic losses, and ensure sustainable development. This policy brief outlines a comprehensive approach to building flood-resilient transport systems, emphasizing the need for cross-border collaboration, public-private partnerships, and evidence-based investment. Key recommendations include prioritizing elevated roadways, permeable surfaces, and flood barriers; leveraging climate finance mechanisms; and aligning transport planning with water security and renewable energy goals.

Context & Challenge

The Great Lakes region is a vital economic and ecological hub, home to over 50 million people and a significant portion of Sub-Saharan Africa’s agricultural and mineral resources. However, its infrastructure is ill-equipped to handle the escalating climate risks. According to the World Bank (2024), 32.4% of Sub-Saharan Africa lacks access to safely managed drinking water, and 53.3% of the population relies on unreliable energy sources. These vulnerabilities are compounded by the region’s geography: the Great Lakes themselves are a major source of freshwater, but their proximity to low-lying areas and river systems makes them prone to flooding.

Climate change has intensified these risks. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) reports that the region has experienced a 15% increase in annual rainfall since the 1980s, with extreme precipitation events becoming more frequent. For example, in 2022, heavy rains in Rwanda and Burundi caused over 100,000 people to be displaced, with damages exceeding $200 million (UNDP, 2023). Floods have also disrupted critical transport routes, such as the Lake Victoria ferry system, which connects millions of people to markets and services.

The economic and social costs of inaction are staggering. A 2023 study by the African Development Bank (AfDB) estimated that climate-related disruptions to transport infrastructure could reduce GDP growth in the region by 1.2–2.5% annually by 2030. For rural communities, where transport is often the only link to urban centers, the consequences are even more severe. Without resilient infrastructure, climate shocks will deepen poverty, erode food security, and undermine efforts to achieve the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), particularly SDG 1 (no poverty), SDG 6 (clean water), and SDG 9 (industry, innovation, and infrastructure).

Analysis

The region’s transport infrastructure is a critical lifeline, yet it remains highly susceptible to climate stressors. Current road networks, bridges, and waterways are designed for historical climate conditions, not the intensified rainfall and flooding projected for the 21st century. For instance, the Kagera River Basin, which spans Burundi, Rwanda, and Tanzania, has seen a 40% increase in flood events since the 1990s (UNEP, 2022). These floods damage roads, disrupt supply chains, and isolate communities, particularly in rural areas where alternative transport options are limited.

The economic impact of these disruptions is profound. The AfDB (2023) estimates that every 10% increase in flood damage to transport infrastructure reduces regional trade by 3–5%. In Kenya, for example, the 2020 floods damaged over 1,200 kilometers of roads, costing an estimated $150 million in repairs and delaying agricultural exports. Similarly, in the DRC, flooding along the Congo River has repeatedly disrupted ferry services, cutting off access to healthcare and education for millions.

The social costs are equally severe. Floods disproportionately affect vulnerable populations, including women, children, and the elderly, who often lack the resources to evacuate or rebuild. In Uganda, a 2021 study found that households in flood-prone areas are 2.3 times more likely to experience food insecurity than those in non-flood zones. These disparities highlight the urgent need for targeted interventions that address both the physical and social dimensions of climate risk.

A key challenge is the lack of integrated planning between climate adaptation and infrastructure development. Most national and regional transport strategies focus on short-term economic growth without considering long-term climate resilience. This siloed approach leaves critical infrastructure exposed to climate shocks and undermines efforts to achieve sustainable development. For example, the East African Community (EAC) has invested heavily in regional trade corridors, but these projects often lack flood mitigation measures, leaving them vulnerable to climate disruptions.

Policy Recommendations

To build flood-resilient transport corridors, governments, regional bodies, and international partners must adopt a coordinated, evidence-based approach. The following recommendations outline actionable steps to strengthen infrastructure, protect livelihoods, and promote sustainable development.

1. Integrate Water Security and Clean Energy into Transport Planning

Water scarcity and energy insecurity are compounding the region’s climate vulnerabilities. By aligning transport infrastructure with water security and renewable energy goals, governments can reduce disaster risks while promoting sustainable growth. For example:
  • Hybrid infrastructure projects: Develop transport systems that double as water management tools. Elevated roadways and permeable surfaces can reduce runoff, while solar-powered water pumps can provide clean water to flood-affected communities.
  • Renewable energy integration: Power transport hubs and flood barriers using solar, wind, or hydroelectric energy. Kenya’s Lake Turkana Wind Power Project, which supplies 310 MW of renewable energy, could serve as a model for powering flood-resilient infrastructure.
  • Water-sensitive urban design: Apply principles of green infrastructure, such as bioswales and retention basins, to manage stormwater and reduce flood risks.

    2. Prioritize Flood-Resilient Infrastructure Design

Existing transport systems must be retrofitted with climate adaptation measures to withstand future flood events. Key design principles include:
  • Elevated roadways: Raise critical roads and bridges by at least 1 meter above projected flood levels. In Rwanda, the Nyamata Bridge, which was rebuilt with elevated foundations, has reduced flood-related disruptions by 70% since 2015.
  • Permeable surfaces: Replace impermeable materials with porous concrete or gravel to allow water to infiltrate the ground, reducing surface runoff. A 2022 study in Tanzania found that permeable pavements reduced flood damage by 40% in urban areas.
  • Flood barriers and drainage systems: Install modular flood barriers and expand drainage networks in flood-prone regions. The DRC’s Lake Tanganyika Basin has seen success with community-led drainage projects, which have reduced flood damage by 50% in pilot areas.

    3. Strengthen Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) for Climate-Resilient Investment

Public funding alone cannot finance the scale of climate adaptation needed in the Great Lakes region. PPPs can mobilize private capital, technical expertise, and innovation to build resilient infrastructure. Strategies include:
  • Climate finance mechanisms: Leverage funds from the Green Climate Fund (GCF), the African Development Bank’s Climate Finance Facility, and regional climate funds to cover 60% of project costs. For example, the GCF has already allocated $150 million to climate adaptation projects in East Africa.
  • Incentivize private investment: Offer tax breaks, subsidies, or risk-sharing mechanisms to attract private sector participation. Kenya’s National Climate Change Response Strategy includes a 10% tax exemption for companies investing in climate-resilient infrastructure.
  • Public-private collaboration models: Adopt hybrid models like the “build-operate-transfer” (BOT) framework, where private firms design and operate infrastructure projects under public oversight. The Uganda Railways Corporation’s partnership with Chinese investors to modernize the railway system is a successful example.

    4. Establish Regional Coordination Mechanisms

The Great Lakes region’s transboundary nature requires cross-border collaboration to address shared climate risks. Recommendations include:
  • Regional flood risk mapping: Create a unified flood risk assessment framework to identify priority corridors for resilience upgrades. The East African Community (EAC) could lead this effort, leveraging data from the African Climate Policy Centre (ACPC).
  • Harmonize standards and regulations: Align national and regional transport standards to ensure consistency in flood-resilient design. The EAC’s Transport Protocol could be updated to include climate adaptation clauses.
  • Joint funding pools: Establish a regional climate adaptation fund to finance cross-border projects. The African Union’s Climate Finance Facility could serve as a model.

    Conclusion

The Great Lakes region stands at a critical juncture. Climate change is no longer a distant threat but an immediate challenge that demands urgent action. Flood-resilient transport corridors are not just an engineering necessity—they are a lifeline for millions of people whose livelihoods depend on reliable infrastructure. By integrating water security, renewable energy, and climate adaptation into transport planning, governments can protect communities, safeguard economic growth, and advance the SDGs.

Achieving this vision requires political will, regional cooperation, and innovative financing. The time to act is now. Without immediate investment in climate-resilient infrastructure, the Great Lakes region risks becoming a cautionary tale of climate vulnerability. Instead, it can emerge as a model of adaptive governance, where resilience and sustainability go hand in hand.
