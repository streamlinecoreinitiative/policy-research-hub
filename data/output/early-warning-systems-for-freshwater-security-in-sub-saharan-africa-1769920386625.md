**Early Warning Systems for Freshwater Security in Sub-Saharan Africa**

## Executive Summary

Sub-Saharan Africa faces a critical water security challenge, with only 32.4% of its population having access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank Open Data). Climate variability and increasing droughts exacerbate these issues, leading to higher risks of water scarcity and related health problems. To mitigate these risks, we recommend implementing early warning systems for freshwater security using satellite imagery and community feedback loops to predict and mitigate water scarcity events.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is home to one of the highest percentages of people lacking access to safe drinking water (32.4%) compared to other regions, highlighting a critical public health issue (World Bank Open Data). The region relies heavily on traditional water sources that are often contaminated or depleted due to climate factors and poor management practices.

Recent data shows declining water levels in key reservoirs across Sub-Saharan Africa, such as Lake Victoria [Satellite data from NASA's Landsat 8 satellite]. Community feedback indicates frequent contamination from agricultural runoff and poor sanitation facilities. Climate change exacerbates existing vulnerabilities, leading to more unpredictable rainfall patterns and increased instances of drought, threatening freshwater availability and increasing the risk of disease outbreaks associated with contaminated water sources.

## Policy Recommendations

We recommend the following:

1. **Develop early warning systems** using satellite imagery and community feedback loops to predict potential water scarcity events.
2. **Invest in infrastructure upgrades** for water treatment facilities, focusing on areas most susceptible to contamination or depletion.
3. **Encourage sustainable agricultural practices** that reduce runoff into freshwater supplies, aligning with the SDG 6 target of improving water quality.
