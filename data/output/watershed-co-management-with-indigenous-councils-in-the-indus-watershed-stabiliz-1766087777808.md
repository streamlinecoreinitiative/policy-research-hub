## Executive Summary

The Indus watershed, spanning across several lower-income countries, faces significant challenges to environmental resilience due to climate change and population growth. The Indus basin faces numerous water-related issues, including:

• Reduced river flows due to climate change and over-extraction
• Overexploitation of groundwater resources for agriculture and industry
• Insufficient water supply for domestic use in rural areas (Current electricity access rate in rural areas)

These challenges have severe implications for food security, with droughts affecting crop yields and livestock productivity.

## Watershed Co-management with Indigenous Councils: A Sustainable Solution

Watershed co-management involves collaborative efforts between local communities, governments, and other stakeholders to manage water resources sustainably. Indigenous councils play a crucial role in this process, as they possess traditional knowledge of the watershed's ecology and hydrology. By engaging indigenous councils in decision-making processes, it is possible to:

• Develop context-specific conservation strategies
• Implement micro-hydro turbines for electricity generation and clean water supply
• Enhance community resilience to droughts through sustainable water management practices

## Economic Viability and Financial Sustainability

Micro-hydro systems offer several economic benefits, including:

• Reduced energy costs for households and businesses
• Increased agricultural productivity through reliable water supply
• Job creation and income generation opportunities for local communities

To ensure financial sustainability, it is essential to involve communities in the decision-making process and provide government incentives. Strategies such as:

• Community-led microfinance initiatives
• Government subsidies for initial investment costs
• Long-term maintenance and repair contracts with local businesses can help ensure the economic viability of micro-hydro projects.

## Challenges and Solutions

Potential challenges to implementing micro-hydro systems include:

• Initial investment costs
• Technological limitations (e.g., limited scalability, high upfront costs)
• Lack of awareness among local communities about the benefits of micro-hydro systems

To address these challenges, it is essential to:

• Develop context-specific business models and financial plans
• Provide training and capacity-building programs for local communities and technicians
• Establish partnerships between governments, NGOs, and private sector entities to promote awareness and adoption of micro-hydro technologies.
