# climate-smart irrigation cooperatives in the Caribbean SIDS: safeguarding freshwater ecosystems

**Climate-Smart Irrigation Cooperatives in the Caribbean SIDS: Safeguarding Freshwater Ecosystems**

## Executive Summary

This policy brief highlights the need to address water security, clean energy access, and climate adaptation challenges in Sub-Saharan Africa through innovative solutions. The region faces significant hurdles with only 32.4% of its population having access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank Open Data) [1] and a mere 53.3% accessing electricity (World Bank Open Data) [2]. To bridge these gaps, we recommend the implementation of solar-powered desalination plants, expansion of renewable energy projects, and development of early warning systems for droughts.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is one of the most vulnerable regions to water scarcity and power shortages. The limited access to safe drinking water (32.4% in 2024) [1] and electricity (53.3% in 2023) [2] has severe implications for public health, economic productivity, and social stability (World Bank Open Data). Climate change exacerbates these challenges by intensifying extreme weather events like droughts and floods.

## Analysis

### Water Security

The region's water access rate is alarmingly low compared to global averages (73.7% globally in 2024) [1] (World Bank Open Data). Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant challenges in providing sustainable water supply due to inadequate infrastructure, contaminated sources, and population growth.

### Clean Energy Access

Only about half of the sub-region's population has access to electricity, with renewable energy consumption at a mere 9.2% [VERIFY]. This disparity hinders economic development and exacerbates poverty.

### Climate Adaptation

Climate change is increasing the frequency and intensity of droughts and floods, posing severe threats to water resources and food security.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Invest in Solar-Powered Desalination Plants**: Allocate funds for solar-powered desalination plants that can provide clean drinking water to remote areas. These facilities require significant upfront investment but offer long-term benefits.
2. **Expand Renewable Energy Projects**: Encourage private sector participation through favorable policies and incentives for renewable energy projects, particularly solar and wind power.
3. **Develop Early Warning Systems**: Establish early warning systems for droughts and floods using satellite imagery and weather forecasting technology to help farmers make informed decisions and improve food security.

## Implementation Considerations

* Cost Analysis: Each desalination plant costs approximately $10 million [VERIFY], while renewable energy projects range from $5 to $20 million per unit depending on scale.
* Potential Barriers: High initial investment costs, limited local expertise in solar technology installation, and potential political resistance due to vested interests in traditional power sources.

## Conclusion

To ensure sustainable development and resilience in Sub-Saharan Africa, policymakers must prioritize investments in water security and clean energy. Leveraging innovative technologies like desalination plants and renewable energy projects can significantly improve access to essential resources while mitigating climate risks. Collaboration between governments, international organizations, and the private sector is crucial for realizing these goals.
