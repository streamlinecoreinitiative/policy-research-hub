**Executive Summary**

The Malian Sahel is one of the most vulnerable regions to climate change, facing recurring droughts and water scarcity that severely impact rural communities' livelihoods. This brief argues that a "water-first" approach, integrating indigenous practices with modern climate-resilient technologies, can enhance water security and agricultural productivity in the face of climate change. By combining traditional knowledge with innovative solutions, communities can improve their resilience to climate-related shocks and stresses.

**Indigenous Practices for Sustainable Water Management in the Sahel**

Historically, indigenous practices such as traditional irrigation systems, agroforestry, and soil conservation techniques have been used by local communities to manage water resources sustainably. These practices not only conserve water but also promote soil fertility and biodiversity. For example:

* Traditional irrigation systems use locally available materials to construct canals and reservoirs, reducing evaporation losses and increasing crop yields.
* Agroforestry practices integrate trees into agricultural landscapes, improving soil health and reducing erosion.
* Soil conservation techniques such as terracing and contour farming reduce soil degradation and promote water retention.

**Modern Climate-Resilient Technologies Integration**

To enhance the resilience of local communities to climate-related shocks and stresses, modern technologies can be integrated with indigenous practices. Some examples include:

* Solar-powered water pumps that provide reliable irrigation for crops
* Rainwater harvesting systems that collect and store rainwater for domestic use
* Drought-resistant crop varieties that improve agricultural productivity in water-scarce conditions

**Case Studies of Successful Implementation**

Several projects have successfully implemented a "water-first" approach, integrating indigenous practices with modern climate-resilient technologies. For example:

* The **Sahel Alliance** project in Mali has integrated traditional irrigation systems with solar-powered pumps to increase crop yields and improve water security.
* The **Green Sahel** initiative has promoted agroforestry practices and rainwater harvesting systems among local communities, reducing soil erosion and improving water retention.

**Economic and Social Impacts**

The integration of indigenous practices with modern climate-resilient technologies has improved livelihoods, reduced poverty, and promoted food security among local communities. For example:

* A study by the **World Bank** found that solar-powered irrigation systems can increase crop yields by up to 30% and reduce water costs by up to 50%.
* The **UNDP** reported that rainwater harvesting projects have improved access to clean water for over 10,000 households in Mali.

* Funding constraints: Many projects rely on external funding, which can be unpredictable and unreliable.
* Policy support: Weak policies and regulations often hinder the adoption of modern climate-resilient technologies.
* Community engagement: Effective community engagement is crucial for successful project implementation.
