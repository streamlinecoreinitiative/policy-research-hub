# urban heat mitigation through cool roofs in the Great Lakes region: lowering emissions from critical sectors

**Urban Heat Mitigation through Cool Roofs in the Great Lakes Region: Lowering Emissions from Critical Sectors**

### Executive Summary

Climate change poses significant challenges to urban areas, particularly in regions like the Great Lakes where extreme heat events are becoming more frequent and severe. One effective strategy for mitigating urban heat is the implementation of cool roofs, which can reduce energy consumption and lower emissions from critical sectors such as buildings and transportation.

### Introduction

The Great Lakes region experiences an increase in extreme heat events, with temperatures often exceeding 90°F (32°C) during summer months. These events have significant impacts on public health, energy consumption, and economic stability. The implementation of cool roofs can help mitigate these effects by reducing the urban heat island effect, decreasing energy consumption, and lowering emissions from buildings.

### Benefits of Cool Roofs

*   **Energy Savings:** Cool roofs can reduce energy consumption in buildings by up to 20% [verify], leading to cost savings for building owners and occupants.
*   **Emissions Reductions:** By reducing energy consumption, cool roofs can lower greenhouse gas emissions from the built environment, contributing to climate change mitigation efforts.
*   **Improved Public Health:** Reduced urban heat island effect can lead to improved public health outcomes, particularly in vulnerable populations such as the elderly and young children.

### Feasibility of Cool Roofs in the Great Lakes Region

The Great Lakes region has a temperate climate with moderate temperatures and precipitation levels. This makes it an ideal location for cool roof implementation, which can be achieved through various materials and technologies:

*   **Reflective Coatings:** Applied to existing roofs to reflect solar radiation and reduce heat gain.
*   **Cool Roofing Materials:** Designed to absorb less solar radiation and emit more heat than traditional roofing materials.
*   **Green Roofs:** Vegetated roofs that provide additional insulation and reduce urban heat island effect.

### Policy Recommendations

To support the adoption of cool roofs in the Great Lakes region, the following policy recommendations are proposed:

1.  **Incentives for Building Owners:** Offer financial incentives, such as tax credits or rebates, to building owners who implement cool roofs.
2.  **Building Codes and Standards:** Update building codes and standards to require the use of cool roof materials in new construction projects.
3.  **Public Education and Outreach:** Educate building owners, occupants, and policymakers about the benefits of cool roofs and their feasibility in the Great Lakes region.

### Actions/Recommendations

1.  Conduct a comprehensive analysis of energy consumption and emissions reductions associated with cool roof implementation in the Great Lakes region.
2.  Develop a Cool Roof Implementation Plan for the Great Lakes region, outlining strategies for adoption and scaling up.
3.  Establish a Cool Roof Task Force to coordinate efforts among stakeholders, including building owners, policymakers, and industry experts.

### Closing Outlook

The implementation of cool roofs is a critical step towards mitigating urban heat in the Great Lakes region. By reducing energy consumption and emissions from buildings, cool roofs can contribute to climate change mitigation efforts and improve public health outcomes. With the right policies and incentives in place, the adoption of cool roofs can be accelerated, leading to a more sustainable and resilient built environment.
