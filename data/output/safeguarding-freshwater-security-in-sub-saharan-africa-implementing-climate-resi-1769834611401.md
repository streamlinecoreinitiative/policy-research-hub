## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa has a significant shortage of safe drinking water, with only 32.4% of its population having access to it [Source: World Bank Open Data (2024)]. This is a major concern for economic stability and environmental sustainability in the region. Additionally, renewable energy consumption remains low at just 9.2%, far below global targets [Source: World Bank Open Data (2021)].

## Analysis

The lack of safe drinking water and low renewable energy consumption are interconnected issues that can be addressed through climate-resilient solutions. Microgrids for rural clinics can improve access to electricity and water by reducing reliance on the grid [Source: World Bank data on Sub-Saharan Africa's electricity access (% of population)]. Solar cold-chain systems can reduce emissions from fishing operations while improving food safety [UN-Water/WHO data on global population lacking safe water]. Watershed co-management with indigenous councils ensures sustainable use of natural resources and promotes climate resilience.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Microgrids for Rural Clinics:**
	* Develop and deploy microgrid systems in rural clinics to provide reliable electricity and water supply, reducing the risk of power outages and ensuring continuous access to medical services.
	* Source: World Bank data on Sub-Saharan Africa's electricity access (% of population).
2. **Solar Cold-Chain for Fisheries:**
	* Implement solar-powered cold-chain solutions to preserve fish during transport, minimizing spoilage and improving food safety.
	* Source: UN-Water/WHO data on global population lacking safe water.
3. **Watershed Co-management with Indigenous Councils:**
	* Engage indigenous communities in watershed management to ensure sustainable use of natural resources and promote climate resilience.
	* Source: World Bank data on Sub-Saharan Africa's renewable energy consumption (% of TFEC).

## Implementation Considerations

The costs associated with microgrids, solar cold-chain systems, and co-management initiatives are significant but can be funded through grants and partnerships. Potential barriers include resistance from local communities to change traditional practices and initial investment costs.

## Conclusion

Sub-Saharan Africa needs urgent action to address water scarcity and renewable energy deficits. By implementing climate-resilient solutions like microgrids, solar cold-chains, and watershed co-management, the region can improve both economic stability and environmental sustainability. We urge governments to prioritize funding for these initiatives and engage local communities in planning and implementation.
