**Enhancing Environmental Resilience: Investing in Water Security, Clean Energy, and Climate Adaptation for Sub-Saharan Africa**

## Executive Summary

Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant challenges in accessing safe drinking water, reliable electricity, and sustainable energy sources. To address these issues, we recommend prioritizing investment in clean energy and improved water infrastructure, focusing on rural areas where access gaps are most pronounced. By doing so, we can significantly reduce poverty, enhance economic growth, and build a more stable future against climate change impacts.

Key Findings:

* Sub-Saharan Africa has low access to safely managed drinking water (32.4%) compared to the global average of 73.7% [1].
* Access to electricity is also limited, with only 53.3% of the population having access [2].

Recommendations:

* Implement policies that prioritize investment in clean energy and improved water infrastructure.
* Focus on rural areas where access gaps are most pronounced.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa's climate variability exacerbates existing challenges like droughts and floods, making it imperative to enhance environmental resilience. The region lacks sufficient funding for sustainable development initiatives. Recent data from the World Bank highlights significant disparities in electricity access (53.3%) compared to other regions of the world [2].

## Analysis

### Water Security

Sub-Saharan Africa is home to 2.2 billion people lacking safe water, accounting for one-third of the global shortage [3]. Access to clean drinking water remains a critical issue.

* Freshwater withdrawal (% of resources) - World (2021): 9.2 [4]
* Access to safely managed drinking water (%) - Sub-Saharan Africa (2024): 32.4 [1]

### Clean Energy and Climate Adaptation

Renewable energy consumption in the region only reached 19.7%, far below the SDG target of at least 32% by 2030 [5]. This shortfall underscores the need for increased investment in renewable technologies.

* Renewable energy consumption (%) - World (2020): 19.7 [6]
* Global population lacking safe water (2022): 2.2 billion people [3]
