## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is one of the regions most affected by climate change, with a significant gap in access to safe drinking water and limited electricity access. The region suffers from severe water scarcity, with only 32.4% of the population having access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank Open Data). These deficits exacerbate climate vulnerabilities, making it imperative to address these issues promptly.

## Analysis

The United Nations targets a 74% coverage rate for safely managed water by 2030 under SDG 6. However, progress has been slow, with Sub-Saharan Africa lagging behind the global average significantly (UN SDG Report 2023). For instance, in 2024, only 32.4% of the population had access to safely managed drinking water, compared to a world-wide figure of 73.7% (World Bank Open Data). Climate change is further compounding these challenges, with global temperatures having risen by approximately +1.1°C since pre-industrial levels (IPCC AR6).

## Policy Recommendations

To address the critical issues facing Sub-Saharan Africa, we propose the following policy recommendations:

1. **Expand Renewable Energy Integration**: Invest in solar power plants and microgrids to increase electricity access and reduce reliance on fossil fuels.
	* Data: The World Bank reports that 53.3% of the population lacks access to electricity (World Bank Open Data). By integrating solar power into irrigation systems, this figure can be improved significantly.
2. **Promote Efficient Irrigation Technologies**: Implement drip irrigation systems and solar-powered desalination plants to enhance water use efficiency.
	* Data: The World Bank’s latest data shows Sub-Saharan Africa has 53.3% electricity access (World Bank Open Data).
3. **Early Warning Systems for Climate Change**: Develop and deploy early warning systems (EWS) to alert communities of impending climate events such as floods or droughts.

## Implementation Considerations

Implementing these recommendations requires careful planning and resource allocation. For instance, solar-powered desalination plants can be costly initially but offer long-term benefits in terms of water supply reliability and energy efficiency. Early warning systems need robust funding to ensure they are effective and accessible across various regions.

## Conclusion

Sub-Saharan Africa’s challenges in water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience require immediate attention and concerted effort from governments, NGOs, and development practitioners. By adopting the recommended policies, Sub-Saharan Africa can lay a solid foundation for sustainable development while enhancing its ability to withstand future climatic shocks.
