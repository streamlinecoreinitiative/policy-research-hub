## Executive Summary

Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant challenges related to water security, energy access, and climate resilience. With only 32.4% of the population having access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank Open Data) compared to a global average of [VERIFY]%, and limited renewable energy consumption at 9.2% (World Bank Open Data), Sub-Saharan Africa is disproportionately affected by these issues. To address this, we recommend prioritizing renewable energy projects for water supply, sanitation, and adaptation measures.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is one of the regions most vulnerable to climate change impacts such as droughts, floods, and sea-level rise (World Bank Open Data). Limited access to clean water and electricity hinders economic growth and exacerbates health risks in the region. According to UN-Water/WHO, 2.2 billion people globally lack safe drinking water, with a significant portion residing in Sub-Saharan Africa.

## Analysis

Evidence shows that renewable energy can play a crucial role in addressing both water security and climate adaptation challenges (World Bank Open Data). For instance, solar-powered desalination plants can provide safe drinking water in coastal areas where fresh water is scarce. Wind and solar projects also contribute to reducing carbon emissions, helping the region meet its climate goals.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Encourage government investment**: Governments should allocate grants and subsidies for renewable energy infrastructure focused on water supply (e.g., desalination plants, solar-powered wells).
2. **Develop and implement policies**: Encourage clean energy adoption by local communities through policy incentives.
3. **Promote community-led initiatives**: Support sustainable maintenance and use of new technologies through community-led initiatives.

## Implementation Considerations

Initial setup costs for renewable energy infrastructure can be high but may be offset by long-term savings on water treatment and electricity bills (World Bank Open Data). Addressing challenges such as limited technical expertise, lack of access to finance, and resistance from traditional power providers requires training programs, public-private partnerships, and innovative financing mechanisms.

## Conclusion

Investing in renewable energy for water security and climate adaptation can be a win-win strategy that not only improves living conditions but also supports broader SDG goals. Governments should prioritize this investment as part of their national development plans to ensure sustainable growth and resilience in the face of climate change.
