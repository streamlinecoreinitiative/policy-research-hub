# Enhancing Resilience in Secondary Cities of the Ganges-Brahmaputra Delta: Integrated Water Management and Climate Adaptation Strategies

## Executive Summary

The Ganges-Brahmaputra delta is one of the most densely populated regions, vulnerable to climate change impacts. This brief argues that integrated approaches to water management, clean energy, and climate adaptation are essential for building resilience in secondary cities. We propose a focus on reducing water loss through the adoption of agroforestry-based micro-hydro systems.

## Water Security Challenges in Secondary Cities

### Overview of Climate Change Impacts

The Ganges-Brahmaputra delta is experiencing increased frequency and severity of extreme weather events, including floods and droughts, exacerbated by climate change (1).

### Importance of Resilient Irrigation Methods

Resilient irrigation methods are crucial for ensuring food security and economic stability in secondary cities. However, existing irrigation systems often suffer from high levels of water loss due to evaporation and seepage.

## Current Irrigation Practices and Challenges

### Description of Existing Irrigation Systems

Secondary cities in the Ganges-Brahmaputra delta rely heavily on canal irrigation systems, which are often inefficient and prone to water loss. Boreholes and other groundwater-based systems also contribute to the region's water scarcity challenges (2).

### Analysis of Water Loss Percentages

Water loss from existing irrigation systems is estimated to range between 30-50% due to evaporation and seepage [verify] (3).

Micro-hydro technology involves the use of small-scale hydroelectric systems to generate electricity. This brief argues that micro-hydro systems can reduce reliance on diesel generators and increase energy efficiency.
