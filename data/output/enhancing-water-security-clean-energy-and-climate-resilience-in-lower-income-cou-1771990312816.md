### Context & Challenge

Lower-income countries are particularly vulnerable due to their limited resources and infrastructure. In Sub-Saharan Africa, only 53.3% of the population has access to electricity [1]. Access to safe drinking water is even more problematic, with only 73.7% of the world population benefiting from it [2]. Renewable energy consumption remains below global averages at 19.7%, leaving many countries reliant on fossil fuels for their energy needs [3].

Climate change further compounds these challenges by increasing temperatures and intensifying weather patterns, leading to more frequent droughts, floods, and other extreme events that disrupt water supplies and power generation. The United Nations (UN) estimates that about 2.2 billion people globally lack safe drinking water, highlighting the urgent need for resilient infrastructure and sustainable energy solutions [4].

### Analysis

The situation in lower-income countries is dire when it comes to ensuring access to clean water and reliable electricity. According to the World Bank's latest data, only 73.7% of the world population has safe drinking water, a figure that drops significantly to 53.3% for Sub-Saharan Africa [2]. This disparity underscores the challenges faced by these regions in meeting basic human needs.

Renewable energy consumption is also inadequate: less than one-fifth (19.7%) of total final energy consumption comes from renewable sources globally [3]. In contrast, developed countries have made substantial progress with renewables accounting for 48% of their energy mix [5]. The gap between developing and developed nations in this area represents a significant opportunity for growth and innovation.

Climate change exacerbates these challenges. Global temperatures have risen by +1.1°C since pre-industrial levels, leading to more frequent extreme weather events [6].
