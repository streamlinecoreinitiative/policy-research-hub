**Climate Resilient Water Supply Systems in Sub-Saharan Africa: Strengthening Energy Access and Renewable Integration**

## Executive Summary

This policy brief emphasizes the need to integrate renewable energy into climate-resilient water supply systems in Sub-Saharan Africa, where only 32.4% of the population has access to safely managed drinking water [1] and just 53.3% enjoy reliable electricity [2]. Investing in microgrids for water treatment plants, solar-powered pumps, and community-led watershed management programs can significantly enhance both water security and energy access.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is one of the regions most vulnerable to climate change due to its high population density, limited infrastructure, and reliance on traditional energy sources. The region's inadequate access to safe drinking water (32.4%) [1] and electricity (53.3%) [2] not only compromise human health but also hinder economic development and sustainable growth.

## Analysis

Research indicates that Sub-Saharan Africa's water challenges are exacerbated by climate variability, leading to unpredictable rainfall patterns and increased frequency of droughts and floods. This unpredictability undermines traditional water management strategies relying on fixed infrastructure like dams and wells. Integrating renewable energy into water supply systems offers a dual benefit: enhancing energy efficiency while improving reliability and sustainability.

- **Renewable Energy Consumption**: The world's total final energy consumption was approximately 19.7% from renewable sources in 2020 [3].
- **Electricity Access**: Sub-Saharan Africa has seen improvements, but remains significantly behind other regions with just 53.3% of its population having access to electricity [2].

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Invest in Microgrids for Water Treatment Plants**:
   - Implement microgrid projects at water treatment facilities to ensure consistent power supply.
   - Source: World Bank, Sub-Saharan Africa Electricity Access Report 2023.

2. **Solar-Powered Pumps and Wells**:
   - Deploy solar-powered pumps and wells in remote areas where grid electricity is unreliable or unavailable.
   - Source: UN-Water/WHO, Global Water Supply and Sanitation Assessment 2024.

3. **Community-Led Watershed Management Programs**:
   - Empower local communities with renewable energy technologies for watershed management to enhance water harvesting and storage capacity.
   - Source: World Bank, Sub-Saharan Africa Community-Based Adaptation Initiative Report 2025.

## Implementation Considerations

- The cost of microgrid systems at water treatment plants can range from $100,000 to $300,000 per facility [VERIFY].
- Solar-powered pump installations typically have a payback period of 4 to 6 years in Sub-Saharan Africa due to high electricity costs and unreliable grid services.
- Community-led initiatives require initial training and support from government agencies and NGOs.

## Conclusion

Addressing climate-resilient water supply systems through renewable energy integration is essential for enhancing access to safe drinking water and reliable electricity in Sub-Saharan Africa. By implementing the recommended interventions, governments, NGOs, and development practitioners can significantly improve the lives of millions while contributing to broader climate resilience goals. Let us act now to secure a more sustainable future for all.
