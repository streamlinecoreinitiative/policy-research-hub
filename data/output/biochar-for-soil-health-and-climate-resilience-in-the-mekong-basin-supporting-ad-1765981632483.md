**Climate Change Impacts in the Mekong Basin**

The Mekong River basin is one of the most vulnerable regions to climate change. Rising temperatures are altering precipitation patterns, leading to more frequent droughts and floods (UNEP, 2020). Climate-related stressors have significant implications for agriculture, which accounts for a substantial portion of the region's economy and population. Soil degradation, including salinization, erosion, and nutrient depletion, limits agricultural productivity and exacerbates climate vulnerability.

**Biochar Application Mechanism**

Biochar is a carbon-rich material produced through the pyrolysis of biomass (e.g., agricultural waste). Its application to soil can improve structure, increase water retention, enhance organic matter content, and mitigate acidification (Lehmann et al., 2003). Biochar's benefits for climate-resilient agriculture are multifaceted:

• **Improved Water Retention**: Biochar increases water-holding capacity in soils, reducing the need for irrigation and enhancing crop resilience to droughts.
• **Increased Crop Yields**: Biochar application can increase crop yields by improving soil fertility and structure.
• **Reduced Input Costs**: By retaining nutrients and improving soil health, biochar reduces the need for fertilizers and pesticides.

**Case Studies from the Mekong Region**

Successful projects using biochar in climate-resilient agriculture have been implemented in Vietnam and Cambodia. For example:

• In Vietnam, a project funded by the Ministry of Agriculture used biochar to improve soil fertility and structure, resulting in increased crop yields and reduced input costs (NARO, 2020).
• In Cambodia, a community-led initiative applied biochar to reduce soil salinization and erosion, improving agricultural productivity and livelihoods for smallholder farmers (World Bank, 2019).
