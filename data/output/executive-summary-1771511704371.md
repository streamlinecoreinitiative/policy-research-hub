# Executive Summary

**Climate Resilient Water Security in Sub-Saharan Africa: Strengthening Access and Energy for Sustainable Development**

## Executive Summary

This policy brief highlights the critical need to address water security and energy access in Sub-Saharan Africa, where [VERIFY] 32.4% of the population lacks access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank data) and only [VERIFY] 53.3% have electricity (World Bank data). Climate change exacerbates these issues by increasing extreme weather events, further straining existing infrastructure. ## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant challenges related to access to safe drinking water (32.4%) and electricity (53.3%), with [NEEDS SOURCE] 2.2 billion people lacking safe water globally. These deficiencies not only threaten health but also hinder economic development. Climate change exacerbates these issues by increasing the frequency of extreme weather events, further straining existing infrastructure.

## Analysis

### Water Security

Only [VERIFY] 32.4% of Sub-Saharan Africa's population has access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank data), compared to [SOURCE: World Bank Open Data] 73.7% globally. Climate change leads to more frequent droughts and floods, further diminishing water availability. In arid regions, solar-powered wells can provide sustainable water solutions.

### Energy Access

Despite global progress, only [VERIFY] 53.3% of Sub-Saharan Africa's population has access to electricity (World Bank data), compared to [SOURCE: World Bank Open Data] 91.6% worldwide. This disparity impacts water management; for instance, solar-powered wells can provide sustainable water solutions in arid regions.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Integrate Renewable Energy**: Implement solar-powered well systems in rural areas where conventional infrastructure is inadequate or non-existent.
2. **Microgrids for Rural Clinics**: Deploy microgrids to power clinics, ensuring reliable electricity and reducing dependency on the grid. This can support health outcomes during heatwaves and improve overall community resilience.

## Implementation Considerations

- **Costs**: Initial investment in solar-powered wells and microgrids is substantial but justified by long-term savings and improved public health.
- **Barriers**: Political resistance to changing existing infrastructure, lack of skilled labor for installation and maintenance, and initial cost concerns are potential obstacles. Address these through targeted funding mechanisms and community engagement.

## Conclusion

Addressing water security in Sub-Saharan Africa requires a holistic approach that incorporates renewable energy solutions such as solar-powered wells and microgrids for rural clinics. By doing so, we can enhance sustainable development outcomes while reducing the region's vulnerability to climate change impacts. Next steps include pilot projects followed by policy advocacy and funding mechanisms.
