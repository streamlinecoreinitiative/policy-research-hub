Harnessing Renewable Energy for Water Security in Sub-Saharan Africa: A Pathway to Climate Resilience

Executive Summary

Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant challenges in ensuring water security due to climate change and existing infrastructure gaps. To address this, we recommend integrating renewable energy into national strategies to improve access to safe drinking water and agricultural irrigation. This can be achieved through policy incentives such as tax breaks or grants for solar power installations and desalination plants. By leveraging climate finance mechanisms, including green bonds and development funds, governments can support these initiatives and achieve both environmental sustainability and socioeconomic development goals.

Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is one of the most vulnerable regions in terms of climate change impacts, with frequent droughts and floods threatening water security [IPCC AR6]. Despite progress towards achieving SDG 6 (water and sanitation), only 74% of people have access to safely managed drinking water globally [UNSDG Report 2023] [Source: UN-Water/WHO]. Climate finance needs are particularly acute for developing countries, estimated at $5.8-5.9 trillion annually by 2030 [UNFCCC].
