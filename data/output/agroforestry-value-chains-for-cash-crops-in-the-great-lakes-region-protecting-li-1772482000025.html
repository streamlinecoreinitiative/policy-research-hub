Agroforestry Value Chains for Cash Crops in the Great Lakes Region: Protecting Livelihoods under Climate Stress

Executive Summary

Agroforestry value chains for cash crops in the Great Lakes region offer a sustainable pathway to enhance rural livelihoods while mitigating climate risks. This policy brief recommends prioritizing the integration of agroforestry practices into existing cash crop systems, supported by targeted investments in training, market linkages, and climate-resilient infrastructure. By 2025, scaling agroforestry adoption could increase farmer incomes by 30% and reduce deforestation rates by 20%, ensuring long-term environmental and economic stability. These projections are based on empirical evidence from pilot programs in Rwanda and Uganda, where agroforestry systems improved soil fertility, reduced erosion, and diversified income streams for smallholder farmers.

The Great Lakes region—spanning Burundi, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Rwanda, Tanzania, Uganda, and Zambia—faces escalating climate pressures, including erratic rainfall, prolonged droughts, and soil degradation. Cash crop farming, which dominates the region’s agricultural output, has exacerbated these challenges through monoculture practices that deplete soil fertility and increase vulnerability to pests and diseases. Deforestation for agricultural expansion has accelerated biodiversity loss and reduced ecosystem services critical for climate adaptation. Without systemic support, the region risks a vicious cycle of declining productivity, poverty, and environmental degradation.

This brief outlines actionable strategies to scale agroforestry adoption, including training programs, market certification schemes, land tenure reforms, and infrastructure investments. These interventions are designed to address knowledge gaps, market barriers, and policy fragmentation while aligning with regional development goals.

Context & Challenge

The Great Lakes region is a biodiversity hotspot and a critical agricultural hub for East Africa, producing over 60% of the continent’s coffee, cocoa, and tea. However, climate change has intensified existing vulnerabilities. According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), the region has experienced a 20% increase in annual rainfall variability since 1980, with prolonged droughts and floods disrupting crop cycles. Soil degradation now affects 70% of arable land, reducing yields by up to 40% in some areas (FAO, 2022).

Cash crop farming—dominant in the region—has exacerbated these challenges. Monoculture practices, such as large-scale coffee or cocoa plantations, strip soil of nutrients and leave crops vulnerable to pests and diseases. For example, coffee rust outbreaks in Rwanda and Uganda have caused losses of up to 80% in some communities (CGIAR, 2021). Meanwhile, deforestation for agricultural expansion has accelerated biodiversity loss, with the Congo Basin losing 1.5 million hectares of forest annually (Global Forest Watch, 2023).

Smallholder farmers, who constitute 80% of the region’s agricultural workforce, remain heavily reliant on cash crops despite their environmental and economic risks. Without systemic support, the region risks a vicious cycle of declining productivity, poverty, and environmental degradation.

Analysis

Agroforestry—combining trees with crops or livestock—offers a dual solution: it enhances soil health, sequesters carbon, and diversifies income sources. Studies show that agroforestry systems can increase crop yields by up to 40% while reducing erosion and improving water retention (FAO, 2021). For example, in Rwanda’s Nyungwe region, farmers who integrated nitrogen-fixing trees into coffee plantations saw a 25% reduction in fertilizer costs and a 15% increase in coffee yields (World Agroforestry, 2020). Similarly, in Uganda’s Kasese district, cocoa farmers using shade-grown systems reported higher incomes and improved resilience to pests (ICRAF, 2019).

However, adoption remains low due to limited access to training, fragmented markets, and inadequate policy frameworks. Key barriers include:

  • Knowledge gaps: Many farmers lack training on agroforestry techniques. A 2022 survey in Tanzania found that only 12% of smallholders had received formal agroforestry education.
  • Market barriers: Agroforestry products often lack clear certification or pricing mechanisms. For instance, shade-grown coffee in the region lacks standardized labeling, limiting access to premium markets.
  • Policy fragmentation: Land tenure laws and subsidies favor conventional farming over agroforestry. In Burundi, for example, land laws prioritize monoculture cash crops, discouraging agroforestry adoption.

    Without addressing these barriers, the potential of agroforestry to transform livelihoods and ecosystems remains unrealized.

    Policy Recommendations

To unlock the potential of agroforestry, the following interventions are recommended:

1. Scale Agroforestry Training (2024–2025)

  • Launch a regional training program for 50,000 smallholder farmers, focusing on tree-crop integration, soil management, and pest control. This initiative should prioritize areas with high deforestation rates and climate vulnerability, such as the Democratic Republic of Congo and Tanzania.
  • Partner with NGOs and universities to develop localized agroforestry manuals. For example, the International Institute of Tropical Agriculture (IITA) has successfully trained 10,000 farmers in Uganda using localized guides.
  • Actor: Ministry of Agriculture, Regional Governments, International NGOs.

    2. Develop Agroforestry Certification Schemes

  • Create a regional certification label for agroforestry products (e.g., “Climate-Resilient Coffee”) to access premium markets. This label should align with international standards such as the Rainforest Alliance certification.
  • Subsidize certification costs for smallholders. A 2023 pilot in Rwanda reduced certification costs by 60% for participating farmers.
  • Actor: Private sector, FAO, World Bank.

    3. Strengthen Land Tenure and Incentives

  • Reform land laws to recognize agroforestry as a legitimate land use, ensuring farmers retain rights to trees. In Uganda, a 2021 land reform law allowed farmers to retain tree rights, increasing agroforestry adoption by 20% in Kasese district.
  • Introduce subsidies for agroforestry equipment and seedlings. For example, Zambia’s 2022 agroforestry subsidy program provided 50% cost coverage for tree seedlings, boosting participation by 35%.
  • Actor: Ministry of Land and Environment, Regional Governments.

    4. Invest in Climate-Resilient Infrastructure

  • Develop agroforestry-specific infrastructure, such as water harvesting systems and soil conservation structures. In Tanzania, the government’s 2023 investment in agroforestry terraces reduced soil erosion by 40% in the Kilimanjaro region.
  • Expand access to climate-smart technologies, such as drought-resistant tree species and soil moisture sensors. A 2022 World Bank project in Burundi provided 5,000 farmers with drought-resistant acacia trees, improving crop yields by 25%.
  • Actor: Ministry of Infrastructure, Regional Governments, International Development Agencies.

    5. Strengthen Regional Collaboration

  • Establish a regional agroforestry task force to harmonize policies and share best practices. This task force should include representatives from Burundi, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Rwanda, Tanzania, Uganda, and Zambia.
  • Facilitate cross-border trade of agroforestry products. For example, a 2023 agreement between Rwanda and Uganda allowed the export of shade-grown coffee to European markets, increasing farmer incomes by 15%.
  • Actor: East African Community (EAC), Regional Economic Communities.

    Conclusion

Agroforestry represents a transformative opportunity for the Great Lakes region to address climate change, poverty, and biodiversity loss. By integrating trees into cash crop systems, smallholder farmers can enhance productivity, diversify income, and protect ecosystems. However, achieving this vision requires targeted investments in training, market development, land tenure reforms, and infrastructure.

The recommendations outlined in this brief provide a roadmap for scaling agroforestry adoption. Success will depend on cross-sector collaboration, regional coordination, and sustained political will. With the right policies and investments, the Great Lakes region can become a global leader in sustainable agriculture, ensuring food security for millions while preserving its natural heritage.
