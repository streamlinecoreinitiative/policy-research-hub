# climate-resilient school feeding supply chains in the Horn of Africa: safeguarding freshwater ecosystems

**Strengthening Water Security, Clean Energy, and Climate Adaptation in Sub-Saharan Africa**

## Executive Summary

Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant challenges in water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience. With only 32.4% of the population having access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank Open Data) and less than 50% having access to electricity (World Bank Open Data), there is an urgent need for innovative policy solutions that can scale quickly and effectively. This brief recommends integrating renewable energy into power grids, implementing early warning systems for droughts and floods, and enhancing community-based watershed management.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is characterized by high population growth, limited access to clean water (32.4% of the population has access), and low electricity coverage (53.3%) (World Bank Open Data). Climate change exacerbates existing challenges through increased frequency of droughts and floods. Without immediate intervention, these issues will hinder socio-economic development and increase vulnerability to climate shocks.

## Analysis

### Water Security

*   **Access to safely managed drinking water**: Sub-Saharan Africa lags behind the world average (73.7%) with only 32.4% having access (World Bank Open Data). This disparity affects health, education, and economic productivity.

### Climate Adaptation

*   **Frequency of extreme weather events**: Increased frequency of droughts and floods due to climate change impacts water security and agricultural productivity, leading to food insecurity.
*   **Early Warning Systems**: Implementing early warning systems can mitigate losses from natural disasters by providing timely alerts, thus reducing mortality rates.

## Policy Recommendations

1.  **Integrate Renewable Energy into Power Grids**:
    *   Encourage public-private partnerships to develop solar-powered mini-grids and microgrids in rural areas.
    *   Provide subsidies for renewable energy projects and financial support through grants or low-interest loans.
2.  **Implement Early Warning Systems**:
    *   Invest in satellite-based early warning systems with localized alert dissemination channels, especially for droughts and floods.
    *   Train local communities on how to use these tools effectively to minimize vulnerabilities.
3.  **Enhance Community-Based Watershed Management**:
    *   Empower indigenous councils through capacity building programs and financial incentives to manage water resources sustainably.
    *   Promote community-led initiatives in watershed co-management, which can reduce pollution from agricultural runoff and improve soil health.

## Implementation Considerations

*   **Costs**: Initial investments in renewable energy infrastructure could range from $10 million to $50 million per project. Costs for early warning systems depend on the scale of deployment but are typically between $2 million to $10 million.
*   **Barriers**: Political resistance, lack of technical expertise, and insufficient financial resources may hinder implementation. Addressing these requires strong political will, community engagement, and targeted capacity building programs.

## Conclusion

Sub-Saharan Africa faces urgent challenges in water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience. By integrating renewable energy into power grids, implementing early warning systems for natural disasters, and enhancing community-based watershed management, we can significantly improve these critical areas. Governments must prioritize these interventions within their national development plans to ensure sustainable growth and improved living standards.

**Next Steps**: Advocate for increased funding at both the national and international levels; collaborate with NGOs and private sector partners to accelerate implementation of recommended policies.
