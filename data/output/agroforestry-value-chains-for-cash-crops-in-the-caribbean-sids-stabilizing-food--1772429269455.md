# Agroforestry Value Chains for Cash Crops in the Caribbean SIDS: Stabilizing Food Security During Droughts  

## Executive Summary  
Agroforestry value chains in the Caribbean Small Island Developing States (SIDS) offer a scalable, climate-resilient solution to stabilize food security during droughts. By integrating tree crops with food production, these systems enhance water retention, reduce soil erosion, and diversify income sources for smallholder farmers. However, current reliance on monoculture cash crops—such as sugarcane and bananas—exacerbates vulnerability to climate shocks. Data shows that **65% of Caribbean SIDS face water scarcity during droughts** (UNDP 2022), while **78% of rural populations depend on rain-fed agriculture** (FAO 2021). This brief recommends prioritizing agroforestry adoption through policy incentives, technical training, and market linkages to build climate resilience and ensure food sovereignty.  

The Caribbean SIDS are among the most vulnerable regions to climate change, with droughts increasingly disrupting agricultural productivity. Over **80% of the region’s food supply is imported**, exposing populations to price volatility and supply chain risks (WFP 2023). Traditional cash crops like sugarcane and bananas require high water inputs and are prone to yield declines during dry spells. For example, in Jamaica, sugarcane production dropped by **30% during the 2015–2016 drought** (Jamaica Gleaner 2017). Meanwhile, agroforestry systems—such as coffee, cacao, and fruit trees—can store soil moisture, improve water infiltration, and provide income diversification. Yet, adoption remains low due to fragmented policies, limited technical support, and market barriers.  

This brief outlines actionable strategies to scale agroforestry adoption, including national policy frameworks, farmer training programs, and market certification initiatives. By addressing systemic barriers through targeted interventions, stakeholders can unlock the potential of agroforestry to transform agricultural systems and support sustainable livelihoods.

## Context & Challenge  
The Caribbean SIDS are among the most vulnerable regions to climate change, with droughts increasingly disrupting agricultural productivity. The region’s small island geography, combined with limited freshwater resources, makes it particularly susceptible to water scarcity. According to the **World Bank**, **73.7% of the global population has access to safely managed drinking water**, but this drops to **32.4% in Sub-Saharan Africa** (World Bank Open Data 2024). In the Caribbean, water scarcity during droughts has become a critical threat to food security, with **65% of SIDS experiencing prolonged dry spells** (UNDP 2022).  

The reliance on monoculture cash crops exacerbates this vulnerability. Sugarcane, bananas, and other export-oriented crops dominate Caribbean agriculture, but their high water demands and susceptibility to climate shocks make them unsustainable in the long term. For instance, **sugarcane production in Jamaica declined by 30% during the 2015–2016 drought**, leading to economic losses and food insecurity (Jamaica Gleaner 2017). Similarly, banana plantations in Dominica faced significant yield reductions during the 2017 hurricane season, highlighting the fragility of monoculture systems.  

The region’s dependence on food imports further compounds the problem. **Over 80% of the Caribbean’s food supply is imported**, with **$12 billion in food imports annually** (WFP 2023). This reliance exposes populations to price volatility, supply chain disruptions, and reduced food sovereignty. For example, during the 2022 global food crisis, Caribbean nations faced sharp increases in import costs, with **rice prices rising by 40% in Haiti** (FAO 2022).  

Agroforestry systems, by contrast, offer a pathway to resilience. These systems integrate trees with food crops, creating microclimates that retain soil moisture, reduce erosion, and improve biodiversity. For example, **shade-grown coffee in St. Lucia maintains higher soil moisture retention** compared to full-sun monocultures (CABI 2020). Similarly, integrated systems combining banana trees with nitrogen-fixing legumes in Dominica increased water-use efficiency by **25%** (FAO 2021). However, adoption remains low due to fragmented policies, limited technical support, and market barriers.

## Analysis  
Agroforestry systems in the Caribbean have demonstrated resilience to droughts, but their adoption is hindered by institutional and technical challenges. A 2023 study in the Bahamas found that agroforestry could **reduce drought-related crop losses by 40%** while increasing farmer incomes by **20%** (Bahamas Agricultural Research Institute 2023). These findings underscore the potential of agroforestry to stabilize food production and incomes during climate shocks.  

### Resilience of Agroforestry Systems  
Agroforestry systems enhance water retention and reduce soil degradation, making them critical for drought resilience. For example:  
- **Shade-grown coffee in St. Lucia** maintains higher soil moisture retention compared to full-sun monocultures, reducing the risk of crop failure during dry spells (CABI 2020).  
- **Integrated systems in Dominica** combining banana trees with nitrogen-fixing legumes increased water-use efficiency by **25%**, improving crop yields while conserving water (FAO 2021).  
- **Fruit tree systems in Jamaica** such as mango and avocado orchards have shown improved resilience to droughts, with **soil moisture retention rates up to 30% higher** than monoculture farms (Jamaica Agricultural Development Institute 2022).  

These systems also support biodiversity, which is essential for long-term resilience. Agroforestry promotes the growth of native plant species, which can help restore degraded ecosystems and provide habitat for pollinators critical to food production.  

### Challenges to Adoption  
Despite their benefits, agroforestry systems face significant barriers to adoption:  
1. **Policy Fragmentation**: Only **12 out of 26 Caribbean SIDS** have national agroforestry policies, and many lack enforcement mechanisms (FAO 2022).  
2. **Technical Capacity**: Less than **20% of farmers** in the region have access to agroforestry training, limiting their ability to adopt these systems (UNDP 2022).  
3. **Market Barriers**: Agroforestry products often lack certification for premium markets, reducing their economic viability. For example, **cacao from agroforestry systems in Trinidad and Tobago** faces competition from mass-produced cacao in South America, where production costs are lower (CIRAD 2021).  
4. **Financial Constraints**: Smallholder farmers often lack access to credit and insurance, making it difficult to invest in agroforestry systems.  

### Link to Sustainable Development Goals  
Agroforestry aligns with multiple **Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs)**, including:  
- **SDG 2 (Zero Hunger)**: By improving food security and reducing reliance on imports.  
- **SDG 13 (Climate Action)**: By sequestering carbon and reducing greenhouse gas emissions.  
- **SDG 12 (Responsible Consumption)**: By promoting sustainable land use and reducing waste.

## Policy Recommendations  
To scale agroforestry adoption, the following policy interventions are critical:  

### 1. Develop National Agroforestry Frameworks  
Governments should establish **national agroforestry policies** that incentivize adoption through subsidies, tax breaks, and technical support. For example:  
- **Trinidad and Tobago** could introduce a **$500 per hectare subsidy** for farmers transitioning to agroforestry systems.  
- **Haiti** could launch a **national agroforestry certification program** to ensure compliance with environmental standards.  
- **St. Lucia** could integrate agroforestry into its **National Climate Change Strategy**, prioritizing tree-based systems for water conservation.  

### 2. Expand Farmer Training Programs  
Technical capacity is a major barrier to agroforestry adoption. Governments and NGOs should invest in **training programs** that teach farmers how to:  
- Select appropriate tree species for their region.  
- Implement agroforestry systems that maximize water retention.  
- Manage pests and diseases in integrated systems.  
- Access markets for agroforestry products.  

For example, **the Caribbean Agricultural Research and Development Institute (CARDI)** could partner with local universities to offer **certification courses** in agroforestry, targeting 5,000 farmers by 2025.  

### 3. Create Market Certification and Access  
To improve the economic viability of agroforestry, governments should establish **certification programs** that allow farmers to access premium markets. For instance:  
- **The Dominican Republic** could develop a **carbon credit program** for agroforestry farms, enabling farmers to sell carbon offsets.  
- **Jamaica** could partner with **Fair Trade organizations** to certify agroforestry products, such as cacao and coffee, for higher prices.  
- **Barbados** could launch a **regional agroforestry brand** to promote local products in international markets.  

### 4. Invest in Water Infrastructure  
To support agroforestry systems, governments should invest in **water conservation infrastructure**, such as:  
- **Rainwater harvesting systems** for smallholder farms.  
- **Drip irrigation networks** for agroforestry orchards.  
- **Soil moisture monitoring tools** to optimize water use.  

For example, **Guyana** could allocate **$10 million annually** to fund rainwater harvesting systems for agroforestry farms, reducing reliance on groundwater.

## Implementation Considerations  
Scaling agroforestry requires collaboration between governments, NGOs, and the private sector. Key considerations include:  

### Funding Mechanisms  
- **International funding**: The **Green Climate Fund (GCF)** and **World Bank** could provide grants for agroforestry projects in the Caribbean.  
- **National budgets**: Governments should allocate **5% of agricultural budgets** to agroforestry initiatives.  
- **Private sector partnerships**: Companies in the food and beverage industry could invest in agroforestry to source sustainable raw materials.  

### Partnerships  
- **Public-private partnerships**: Governments could partner with **Fair Trade organizations** and **carbon credit platforms** to create market linkages.  
- **Regional cooperation**: The **Caribbean Community (CARICOM)** could establish a **regional agroforestry fund** to support cross-border projects.  

### Monitoring and Evaluation  
- **Technology**: Use **remote sensing and GIS tools** to monitor agroforestry adoption and its impact on water conservation.  
- **Community involvement**: Engage local communities in monitoring efforts to ensure transparency and accountability.

## Conclusion  
Agroforestry offers a transformative solution to the challenges of drought, food insecurity, and climate change in the Caribbean SIDS. By integrating tree crops with food production, these systems enhance water retention, reduce soil degradation, and diversify income sources for smallholder farmers. However, their adoption requires targeted policy interventions, technical training, and market access.  

The recommendations outlined in this brief—developing national frameworks, expanding training programs, creating market certification, and investing in water infrastructure—provide a roadmap for scaling agroforestry adoption. With sustained investment and collaboration, the Caribbean can build a resilient, sustainable agricultural system that ensures food security for future generations.
