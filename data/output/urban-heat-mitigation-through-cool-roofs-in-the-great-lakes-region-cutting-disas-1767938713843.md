## Introduction

The Great Lakes region faces an increasing number of extreme heat events, posing significant risks to public health, infrastructure, and local economies. The urban heat island effect exacerbates these impacts, with built-up areas absorbing and retaining heat. Lower-income communities are disproportionately affected due to limited access to green spaces, cooling technologies, and resources for mitigation efforts.

## Current Practices and Challenges

* Existing building stock in the Great Lakes region is largely composed of dark-colored roofs that absorb solar radiation, contributing to the urban heat island effect.
* Limited funding and resources hinder widespread adoption of cool roof technology, particularly in lower-income communities.
* Building codes and zoning regulations often do not prioritize energy efficiency or climate resilience.
