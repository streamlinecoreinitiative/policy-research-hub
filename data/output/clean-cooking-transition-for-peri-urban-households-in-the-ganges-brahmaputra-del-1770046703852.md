**Enhancing Water Security, Clean Energy Access, and Climate Resilience in Sub-Saharan Africa: A Multi-faceted Approach**

## Executive Summary

Sub-Saharan African countries face significant challenges in ensuring access to clean water (32.4% of the population lacks safely managed drinking water) and reliable energy sources (53.3% lack electricity access). To address these issues, we recommend investing in water infrastructure, expanding electrification efforts, and promoting the use of renewable energy sources. This approach is crucial for achieving the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) related to water and energy security.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa faces critical challenges such as limited access to clean water and electricity. These issues exacerbate poverty, disease prevalence, and hinder economic development. According to World Bank data, only 32.4% of the population in Sub-Saharan Africa has access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank, 2024). In contrast, 91.6% of the world's population has access to electricity, but this figure drops to 53.3% for Sub-Saharan Africa (World Bank, 2023).

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Invest in Water Infrastructure**: Construct and upgrade water treatment facilities; build rainwater harvesting systems where feasible.
	* Implementation Consideration: Costs vary based on location and scale but can be funded through grants or concessional loans.
2. **Expand Electrification Efforts**: Prioritize rural areas, especially those with high population density. Focus on solar-powered mini-grids to ensure reliability.
	* Implementation Consideration: Governments should collaborate with international partners for financing and technical expertise.
3. **Promote Renewable Energy**: Encourage the use of solar power in households and small businesses; support community-led initiatives.
	* Implementation Consideration: Provide subsidies, tax incentives, and training programs to facilitate adoption.

## Implementation Considerations

The total cost will vary based on scale but could range from $10 million for a village-level project to billions for national-scale programs. Potential barriers include limited financial resources, resistance from traditional energy providers, and lack of technical expertise and infrastructure.
