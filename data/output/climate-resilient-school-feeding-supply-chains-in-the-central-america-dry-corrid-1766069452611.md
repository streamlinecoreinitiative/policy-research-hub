# climate-resilient school feeding supply chains in the Central America dry corridor: protecting livelihoods under climate stress

**Climate-Resilient School Feeding Supply Chains in the Central America Dry Corridor: Protecting Livelihoods under Climate Stress**

**Executive Summary**

The Central America dry corridor is one of the most climate-vulnerable regions worldwide, facing recurring droughts and erratic rainfall patterns that significantly impact agricultural productivity. This brief focuses on micro-insurance mechanisms coupled with renewable energy solutions as a critical intervention to enhance food security and livelihoods in this region. By providing financial support during climatic crises and promoting sustainable agricultural practices, these initiatives can mitigate immediate shocks while building long-term resilience against future climate-induced disruptions.

**Climate Vulnerability in the Central America Dry Corridor**

The Central America dry corridor spans across six countries: Belize, Costa Rica, El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras, Nicaragua. This region is characterized by a tropical savanna climate with pronounced dry and wet seasons. However, recent trends indicate an increase in drought frequency and severity, particularly during the dry season (December to May). The 2018-2020 drought episode resulted in significant crop losses, affecting over 1 million people and causing estimated economic losses of $2.3 billion.

**Micro-insurance Mechanisms for Farmers**

Micro-insurance products designed specifically for farmers provide a financial safety net against climate-related shocks. These mechanisms work by pooling resources from participating farmers to create a fund that can be drawn upon during climatic crises, such as droughts or floods. The key benefits of micro-insurance include:

• **Financial support**: Immediate cash disbursements to farmers affected by climatic events
• **Risk reduction**: Reduced financial vulnerability for farmers and their households
• **Increased resilience**: Long-term adaptation to climate variability through improved agricultural practices

**Renewable Energy Solutions as a Supporting Factor**

The integration of renewable energy solutions, such as solar-powered irrigation systems, can significantly improve agricultural productivity by reducing costs associated with traditional energy sources. This approach also enhances water management and reduces greenhouse gas emissions. Key benefits include:

• **Energy efficiency**: Reduced energy consumption and lower operational costs
• **Water conservation**: Improved water management through efficient irrigation systems
• **Renewable energy adoption**: Increased deployment of solar-powered solutions, contributing to a low-carbon economy

**Case Studies from Existing Programs**

Several micro-insurance programs have been implemented in the Central America dry corridor with notable successes:

• The **World Bank's Weather Risk Management Facility (WRMF)** has provided financial support to over 100,000 farmers in Honduras and Nicaragua.
• The **Guatemalan Microfinance Association (AMF)** has launched a climate-resilient agriculture program offering micro-insurance products to smallholder farmers.

**Challenges and Policy Recommendations**

Despite the potential benefits of micro-insurance and renewable energy solutions, several challenges need to be addressed:

• **Limited awareness**: Many farmers are unaware of these initiatives or lack access to information
• **High transaction costs**: Administrative fees associated with micro-insurance can be prohibitively expensive for smallholder farmers
• **Regulatory hurdles**: Existing policies may not support the widespread adoption of micro-insurance and renewable energy solutions

**Actions/Recommendations**

1.  Conduct awareness campaigns to educate farmers about micro-insurance and renewable energy solutions.
2.  Implement policy reforms to reduce transaction costs associated with micro-insurance.
3.  Develop and disseminate case studies highlighting successful implementations of micro-insurance and renewable energy solutions.

**Closing Outlook**

The Central America dry corridor faces significant climate-related challenges that threaten the livelihoods of millions of people. By implementing micro-insurance mechanisms coupled with renewable energy solutions, we can enhance food security and build resilience against future climate-induced disruptions. It is essential to address the identified challenges through policy reforms, awareness campaigns, and capacity-building initiatives to ensure the widespread adoption of these critical interventions.
