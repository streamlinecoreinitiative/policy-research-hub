# climate-smart irrigation cooperatives in the Great Lakes region: supporting adaptation targets in NDCs

**Enhancing Water Security, Clean Energy, and Climate Adaptation in Sub-Saharan Africa: Feasible Interventions for Sustainable Development**

## Executive Summary

This policy brief highlights critical gaps in water security, clean energy access, and climate adaptation in Sub-Saharan Africa. Despite global efforts towards sustainable development goals (SDGs), the region lags behind the rest of the world. We recommend investing in solar-powered wells to improve access to safe drinking water, promoting renewable energy sources like hydroelectricity to increase electricity access, and implementing microgrids for rural clinics to reduce reliance on the grid. These measures are cost-effective, scalable, and can be implemented with supportive policies.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is one of the most water-stressed regions globally, with only 32.4% of its population having access to safely managed drinking water [1]. Energy scarcity exacerbates this issue; just 53.3% of the region's population has access to electricity [2]. Climate change poses additional challenges, including more frequent droughts and floods. These conditions highlight a pressing need for sustainable water security solutions and clean energy infrastructure.

## Analysis

The World Bank data shows that Sub-Saharan Africa lags behind other regions in both water security and renewable energy consumption. For example, 73.7% of the world has access to safely managed drinking water [1], compared to only 32.4% in Sub-Saharan Africa [1]. Similarly, while 91.6% of the world's population has access to electricity [2], this drops significantly to just 53.3% in Sub-Saharan Africa [2]. Renewable energy consumption is also lower here, at 9.2% [3], as opposed to 19.7% globally [4].

These statistics underscore the urgency of implementing sustainable water security and climate adaptation measures. Interventions like solar-powered wells can provide clean drinking water where it's most needed. Expanding renewable energy sources such as hydroelectricity offers a viable solution for electricity access, particularly in rural areas. Additionally, microgrids can be installed in clinics to reduce reliance on the grid, improving reliability and reducing emissions.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Invest in Solar-Powered Wells**: Implement solar-powered water pumps in underserved regions where traditional boreholes are ineffective or non-existent. This initiative would improve access to safe drinking water.
2. **Promote Renewable Energy Sources**: Invest in hydroelectric projects to increase the region's renewable energy consumption and reduce reliance on fossil fuels. Focus on cost-effective, scalable solutions like small-scale dams.
3. **Microgrids for Rural Clinics**: Develop microgrid systems that power clinics and health centers, ensuring reliable electricity for essential services. This would enhance healthcare outcomes while reducing carbon emissions.

## Implementation Considerations

- **Costs**: Initial investments in solar-powered wells can range from $10,000 to $50,000 per well [5], with maintenance costs around 20% of initial investment annually.
- **Barriers**: Political instability and limited funding could hinder implementation. Collaboration between governments, NGOs, and private sector entities is crucial for overcoming these challenges.

## Conclusion

Sub-Saharan Africa needs immediate action on water security, clean energy, and climate adaptation to meet SDG targets. By implementing feasible interventions such as solar-powered wells, renewable energy projects, and microgrids, the region can achieve sustainable development goals while improving resilience against future shocks. Collaboration among stakeholders is essential for successful implementation.

**Call to Action**: Governments, NGOs, and international organizations must prioritize these interventions in their policies and budgets to create a more resilient Sub-Saharan Africa.
