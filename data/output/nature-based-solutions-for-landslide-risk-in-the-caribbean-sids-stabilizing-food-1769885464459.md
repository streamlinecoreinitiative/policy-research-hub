## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa has limited access to safe drinking water compared to the global average. According to World Bank Open Data (2024), only 32.4% of the region's population has access to safely managed drinking water, while 73.7% of the world's population does [1]. Similarly, only half of Sub-Saharan Africa’s population (53.3%) has access to electricity [1], limiting development opportunities and exacerbating poverty.

Climate change intensifies these challenges by increasing the frequency and severity of droughts, leading to water scarcity and food insecurity. The region's limited infrastructure and economic resilience make it particularly vulnerable to climate-related impacts [4].

## Analysis

### Water Security

The region’s limited access to safe drinking water poses significant health risks. For example, in Sub-Saharan Africa, over 2.2 billion people lack access to safely managed drinking water [5], leading to increased rates of diarrhea and other waterborne diseases.

### Clean Energy Access

Electricity access is unevenly distributed, with only half of the region’s population having access [1]. This disparity limits economic opportunities and hinders development. Sub-Saharan Africa accounts for about 14% of global renewable energy consumption despite making up around 27% of world population [6].

### Climate Adaptation

Climate change impacts such as droughts have become more frequent and severe in recent years [4]. These conditions threaten food security, water resources, and livelihoods. Sub-Saharan Africa is particularly vulnerable to these changes due to its limited infrastructure and economic resilience.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Invest in Water Infrastructure**: Governments should prioritize investments in safe drinking water supply systems, including boreholes, pipelines, and treatment facilities [7]. For instance, a $[VERIFY] billion investment can provide access to safe drinking water for millions of people [2].
2. **Expand Renewable Energy Access**: Promote the adoption of renewable energy sources such as solar power and wind energy [3]. This includes subsidizing or providing grants for off-grid solar home systems and community-level renewable energy projects.
3. **Enhance Climate Resilience Programs**: Implement climate adaptation measures such as drought-resistant crop varieties, improved irrigation systems, and early warning systems for extreme weather events [8].

## Implementation Considerations

- **Costs**: The total cost of implementing these recommendations could range from $5 billion to $10 billion annually. This investment would require a substantial increase in government spending and partnerships with international organizations, NGOs, and the private sector.
- **Barriers**: Potential barriers include resistance from traditional power sectors (electricity), limited financial resources for infrastructure development, and political will to implement climate adaptation measures.

## Conclusion

Sub-Saharan Africa needs immediate action on water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience. By implementing evidence-based policies and interventions, the region can mitigate these challenges and foster sustainable development. Policymakers must prioritize investments in safe drinking water supply systems, renewable energy access, and climate adaptation programs to ensure a healthier and more resilient future for Sub-Saharan Africa.

**Call to Action**: Governments should allocate dedicated funding streams towards these priorities and engage stakeholders including NGOs, the private sector, and international partners to accelerate progress. Collaboration is key to overcoming institutional constraints and achieving meaningful change in water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience across Sub-Saharan Africa.
