# water loss reduction in secondary cities in the Central America dry corridor: raising resilience finance for communities

**Water Loss Reduction in Secondary Cities in the Central America Dry Corridor: Raising Resilience Finance for Communities**

**Executive Summary**

The Central America dry corridor is one of the most vulnerable regions to climate change, characterized by frequent droughts and water scarcity. Secondary cities in this region face significant challenges in providing water security for their residents, exacerbating existing social and economic inequalities. This brief highlights the importance of environmental resilience in lower-income countries, focusing on water security, clean energy, and climate adaptation. We propose a comprehensive approach to raise resilience finance for communities in secondary cities, leveraging micro-insurance schemes to mitigate financial risks associated with water scarcity.

**Climate Variability and Water Security in Secondary Cities**

The Central America dry corridor spans across six countries, including Guatemala, Honduras, El Salvador, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, and Panama. Climate change projections indicate a 20-30% decrease in rainfall by 2050, exacerbating existing water scarcity issues (UNEP, 2020). Secondary cities, which are often characterized by rapid urbanization and inadequate infrastructure, struggle to provide reliable access to clean water for their residents. Water losses due to leaks, theft, or inefficient systems can reach up to 30% of total production, further straining already limited resources (World Bank, 2019).

**Current Challenges Faced by Secondary Cities**

Secondary cities in the Central America dry corridor face significant financial and institutional challenges in addressing water security issues. Limited access to finance, inadequate infrastructure, and lack of technical capacity hinder efforts to implement efficient water management systems. Existing micro-insurance schemes are often tailored for smallholder farmers or rural communities, leaving a gap in coverage for urban residents (African Development Bank, 2018).

**Micro-Insurance Schemes for Water Security**

To address the financial risks associated with water scarcity, we propose the development of micro-insurance schemes specifically designed for secondary cities. These schemes can provide protection against losses due to droughts, floods, or other climate-related events that impact water availability. By pooling resources and sharing risks, communities can better manage financial vulnerabilities and invest in resilience-building measures (WFP, 2020).

**Case Studies and Economic Impact Analysis**

Successful micro-insurance programs have been implemented in various regions, including the Sahel region of Africa. For example, a study by the African Development Bank found that farmers who participated in micro-insurance schemes experienced an average crop yield increase of 15% (African Development Bank, 2018). Estimated cost savings for farmers due to reduced financial losses from climate-related events range from $100 to $500 per household (World Bank, 2019).

**Policy Recommendations and Actions**

To raise resilience finance for communities in secondary cities, we recommend the following policy measures:

1. **Establish a regional micro-insurance fund**: Pool resources from governments, donors, and private sector entities to support the development of micro-insurance schemes.
2. **Develop tailored insurance products**: Design insurance products that address specific water security risks faced by secondary cities.
3. **Strengthen institutional capacity**: Provide technical assistance and training to local authorities to enhance their ability to manage and implement micro-insurance programs.

**Closing Outlook**

The Central America dry corridor is one of the most vulnerable regions to climate change, with secondary cities facing significant challenges in providing water security for their residents. By leveraging micro-insurance schemes to mitigate financial risks associated with water scarcity, we can raise resilience finance for communities and enhance their ability to adapt to climate-related events. This brief highlights the importance of environmental resilience in lower-income countries and provides a roadmap for policymakers, donors, and private sector entities to support the development of micro-insurance programs.
