# micro-insurance for smallholder drought risk in the Sudano-Sahelian belt: improving health outcomes in heatwaves

**Bridging Water Security, Clean Energy Access, and Climate Adaptation Gaps in Lower-Income Countries**

## Executive Summary

Lower-income countries face significant challenges in ensuring water security, clean energy access, and effective climate adaptation measures. This policy brief highlights the urgent need to address these gaps and recommends implementing solar-powered wells for rural communities, increasing investment in renewable energy projects, and enhancing flood management strategies.

### Key Findings:

* Lower-income countries have alarmingly low access to safely managed drinking water (32.4% in Sub-Saharan Africa compared to 73.7% globally) [1].
* Renewable energy consumption remains stagnant at 19.7% globally, with significant disparities between developed and developing regions [2].
* Climate-induced weather extremes increase risks of droughts, floods, and water scarcity, impacting food security, health outcomes, and economic stability.

### Recommendations:

1. **Implement Solar-Powered Wells for Rural Communities**: Invest in solar-powered well projects to provide reliable access to safe drinking water in rural areas (73.7% vs 32.4%) [1]. Estimate: $10 million per project, with potential for scaling based on demand and funding availability.
2. **Increase Investment in Renewable Energy Projects**: Allocate funds towards solar, wind, and hydro projects to increase renewable energy consumption by 5% annually (from 19.7% to 24.7%) over five years [2]. Estimate: $2 billion annual investment required for scaling up projects.
3. **Enhance Flood Management Strategies**: Implement early warning systems, build levees and flood barriers, and promote community-based disaster risk reduction programs (53.3% vs 19.7%) [3]. Estimate: $80 million per year in initial infrastructure costs with potential for scaling.

## Context & Challenge

Lower-income countries are disproportionately affected by climate change and environmental degradation. Sub-Saharan Africa lacks reliable access to safe drinking water (32.4%), electricity (53.3%), and clean energy sources [1, 2]. Climate change exacerbates existing challenges, leading to increased frequency of droughts and floods, which threaten livelihoods and public health.
