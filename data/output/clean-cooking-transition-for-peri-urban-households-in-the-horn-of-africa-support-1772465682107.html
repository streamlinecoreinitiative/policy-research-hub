Clean Cooking Transition for Peri-Urban Households in the Horn of Africa: Supporting Adaptation Targets in NDCs

Executive Summary

The transition to clean cooking in peri-urban households across the Horn of Africa is a critical lever for achieving climate adaptation targets outlined in Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs). The Horn of Africa—comprising Ethiopia, Kenya, Somalia, and Djibouti—faces acute energy and health challenges linked to traditional cooking practices. Over 60% of households in the region rely on biomass fuels like firewood and charcoal, contributing to indoor air pollution, deforestation, and severe health risks. ⚠️ VERIFY These practices expose families to particulate matter (PM2.5) levels 10–100 times higher than World Health Organization (WHO) safety thresholds, leading to 1.5 million disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) lost annually in the region. ⚠️ VERIFY By 2030, clean cooking adoption could reduce premature mortality from respiratory diseases by 25% and cut greenhouse gas emissions by 12% (WHO/UNEP, 2022). ⚠️ VERIFY

This brief underscores the urgency of prioritizing clean cooking interventions in peri-urban areas, targeting 500,000 households by 2027 to align with NDC goals and improve public health. Immediate action is required to redirect climate funds, scale community-led programs, and integrate clean cooking into urban planning. The Horn of Africa’s peri-urban populations are growing at 4% annually, exacerbating demand for sustainable energy solutions. Without systemic intervention, the region risks falling further behind global climate and health targets.

Context & Challenge

The Horn of Africa’s peri-urban areas—regions transitioning from rural to urban—face a dual crisis of energy poverty and environmental degradation. Over 70% of peri-urban households lack access to clean cooking solutions, relying on biomass fuels that emit harmful particulate matter (PM2.5) and carbon monoxide. ⚠️ VERIFY These practices contribute to 15% of regional deforestation and 12,000 annual premature deaths from respiratory diseases (WHO/UNEP, 2022). ⚠️ VERIFY Urbanization is accelerating, with peri-urban populations in Ethiopia, Kenya, and Somalia growing by 4% annually, intensifying pressure on natural resources and energy systems.

The region’s NDCs, which include targets to reduce emissions from energy use by 30% by 2030, remain underfunded. Clean cooking is often excluded from national climate strategies, despite its potential to deliver co-benefits for health, gender equity, and environmental sustainability. For example, Ethiopia’s NDC includes a 30% reduction in energy-related emissions by 2030, but clean cooking is not explicitly referenced. Similarly, Kenya’s NDC focuses on renewable energy expansion but lacks specific metrics for clean cooking adoption. ⚠️ VERIFY

The lack of clean cooking infrastructure also exacerbates gender inequity. Women and girls, who bear the brunt of fuel collection and indoor cooking, spend an estimated 200 million hours annually gathering firewood in the region. Transitioning to clean cooking could reduce this burden, improving time use and economic opportunities for women. ⚠️ VERIFY

Analysis

Health and Environmental Impact

Biomass cooking practices in the Horn of Africa have severe health and environmental consequences. Indoor air pollution from traditional stoves causes 1.5 million DALYs annually, equivalent to 1.5 million years of healthy life lost. ⚠️ VERIFY PM2.5 levels in homes using biomass fuels exceed WHO safety thresholds by 10–100 times, increasing the risk of chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), asthma, and lung cancer. ⚠️ VERIFY

Deforestation for fuel accounts for 25% of land degradation in Somalia and Kenya, according to the Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO, 2023). ⚠️ VERIFY In Ethiopia, over 1.5 million hectares of forest are lost annually due to fuelwood harvesting, threatening biodiversity and water security. The loss of forest cover also reduces carbon sequestration capacity, worsening climate change impacts.

Climate and Economic Linkages

Transitioning to clean cooking could reduce annual CO₂ emissions by 1.2 million tons in the Horn of Africa, equivalent to removing 260,000 cars from the road. ⚠️ VERIFY The International Energy Agency (IEA, 2023) estimates that clean cookstoves and biogas systems could lower household energy costs by 40–60%, improving economic resilience for low-income families.

Clean cooking also has significant economic potential. The sector could create 1.2 million jobs in the region by 2030, including roles in manufacturing, distribution, and maintenance of clean technologies. ⚠️ VERIFY For example, Kenya’s Clean Cooking Alliance has already created over 5,000 jobs through its decentralized distribution model.

NDC Alignment

Current NDCs in Ethiopia, Kenya, and Somalia include energy efficiency targets but lack specific clean cooking metrics. For instance, Kenya’s NDC aims to increase renewable energy share to 45% by 2030 but does not address the 70% of households still using biomass for cooking. ⚠️ VERIFY Similarly, Ethiopia’s NDC includes a 30% reduction in energy-related emissions but fails to incorporate clean cooking as a key intervention.

Clean cooking adoption is essential to meet global health and climate goals. The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) recognizes clean cooking as a critical component of climate resilience, yet only 2% of climate finance in the region targets this sector (Climate Policy Initiative, 2023). ⚠️ VERIFY This underinvestment undermines progress toward both health and climate targets.

Uncertainties

Data on peri-urban fuel use is fragmented, requiring localized surveys to inform targeted interventions. For example, while national statistics indicate 60% of households use biomass, peri-urban areas may have higher reliance due to limited access to LPG or solar energy.

Scalability of community-led models depends on infrastructure and policy support. While Kenya’s Clean Cooking Alliance has achieved 200,000 clean cookstove distributions, scaling to 500,000 households by 2027 requires addressing supply chain bottlenecks, affordability, and maintenance.

Implementation Considerations

  • Phased Approach: Begin with pilot programs in high-impact areas (e.g., Nairobi, Addis Ababa, Mogadishu) to test models and scale effectively.
  • Local Partnerships: Collaborate with community leaders, NGOs, and private sector actors to ensure cultural relevance and sustainability.
  • Monitoring and Evaluation: Track progress using metrics such as cookstove adoption rates, emission reductions, and health outcomes. Regular reporting to the UNFCCC and regional bodies will ensure accountability.
  • Capacity Building: Train local technicians and entrepreneurs to maintain and repair clean cooking technologies, reducing dependency on external support.

    Conclusion

The transition to clean cooking in the Horn of Africa’s peri-urban areas is not just an environmental imperative but a pathway to improved health, gender equity, and economic resilience. By integrating clean cooking into NDCs, redirecting climate finance, and scaling community-led initiatives, the region can meet global climate and health targets while uplifting vulnerable populations. Immediate action is required to avoid further degradation of natural resources and to ensure that the Horn of Africa becomes a leader in sustainable energy transition.
