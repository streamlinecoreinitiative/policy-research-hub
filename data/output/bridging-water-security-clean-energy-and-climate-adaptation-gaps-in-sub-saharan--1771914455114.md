## Executive Summary

To bridge the water security, clean energy, and climate adaptation gaps in Sub-Saharan Africa, we recommend implementing national water management plans focused on improving access to safe drinking water. This can be achieved by investing in solar-powered desalination plants for coastal areas where freshwater is scarce. Additionally, expanding microgrids and off-grid renewable energy solutions in rural communities can increase electricity access, while developing early-warning systems for climate-induced disasters will enhance community resilience.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa faces a triple challenge of water scarcity, energy poverty, and climate-induced vulnerabilities (World Bank Open Data). The region is grappling with severe water shortages and extreme weather events, exacerbating existing infrastructure vulnerabilities. With an estimated 2.2 billion people lacking safe drinking water ([NEEDS SOURCE]), Sub-Saharan Africa's population faces significant health risks. Similarly, the limited access to electricity hinders economic development and climate adaptation efforts.

## Analysis

Evidence shows that improving access to clean water can significantly reduce diseases like cholera and typhoid, leading to better health outcomes (UN-Water/WHO). For instance, a study found that ensuring safe drinking water could prevent millions of deaths annually. In terms of renewable energy, Sub-Saharan Africa holds substantial potential with less than 20% currently being used ([NEEDS SOURCE]). However, significant investments are needed to harness this resource effectively and promote climate resilience.
