# water loss reduction in secondary cities in the Pacific SIDS: protecting livelihoods under climate stress

**Water Loss Reduction in Secondary Cities in the Pacific SIDS: Protecting Livelihoods Under Climate Stress**

Executive Summary
-----------------

Climate change exacerbates water scarcity issues in secondary cities of the Pacific Small Island Developing States (SIDS), affecting agriculture and livelihoods of smallholder farmers. Traditional irrigation methods are energy-intensive and costly, while agroforestry-based micro-hydro systems offer a sustainable solution for climate-resilient irrigation.

### Introduction

Climate change has severe impacts on the Pacific SIDS, including increased frequency and severity of droughts and heatwaves (Source: IPCC). Traditional irrigation methods, such as boreholes and canals, are inefficient and costly. Agroforestry practices have been shown to improve soil health and water retention capacity, while micro-hydro systems provide renewable energy for irrigation.

### Integrated Systems Implementation

The project proposes integrating agroforestry with micro-hydro systems in pilot projects to demonstrate improved water security and reduced dependency on external energy sources (Section 5). A cost-benefit analysis is needed to assess the economic viability of these combined solutions.

### Policy Recommendations

To support adoption, government subsidies or low-interest loans are suggested to cover initial investments and infrastructure development costs. Funding is also proposed for community-based irrigation system improvements and joint decision-making committees involving government officials, farmers, and environmental experts.

### Future Research Directions

Long-term sustainability assessments and community engagement strategies are recommended to ensure the continued effectiveness of these integrated systems (Section 7).
