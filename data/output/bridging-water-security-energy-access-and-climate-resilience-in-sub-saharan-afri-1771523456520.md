**Bridging Water Security, Energy Access, and Climate Resilience in Sub-Saharan Africa**

## Executive Summary

Sub-Saharan Africa faces a critical juncture in addressing severe challenges in water security, energy access, and climate resilience. With only 32.4% of the population having access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank, 2024), and just 53.3% accessing electricity (World Bank, 2023), the region's development trajectory is severely hampered. To bridge these gaps, we recommend a three-pronged approach: enhancing water security through infrastructure upgrades and community management systems; promoting clean energy access through subsidies and tax incentives for renewable energy sources; and improving climate resilience through sustainable agriculture practices and early warning systems.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is grappling with significant gaps in basic infrastructure, particularly in water supply and energy generation. Only 32.4% of the population has access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank, 2024), compared to 73.7% worldwide (World Bank, 2024). Similarly, only 53.3% of Sub-Saharan Africa's population has electricity access (World Bank, 2023), whereas the global average stands at 91.6% (World Bank, 2023). These disparities exacerbate vulnerability to climate change impacts and hinder socio-economic development.

## Analysis

The challenges in water security and energy access are deeply intertwined with climate resilience. Sub-Saharan Africa is home to a significant portion of the world's population without reliable access to safe drinking water or electricity (World Bank, 2024). The region also lags behind globally in terms of renewable energy consumption (19.7%, World Bank, 2020), limiting its ability to adapt to climate-induced changes and sustain economic growth.
