Bridging Water Security Gaps in Sub-Saharan Africa Through Renewable Energy and Climate Adaptation

Executive Summary

The lack of access to safely managed drinking water is a critical issue in Sub-Saharan Africa, with only 32.4% of the population having access to this essential resource [1]. Climate change exacerbates these challenges by altering rainfall patterns, leading to increased droughts and floods [2]. To address this pressing concern, we recommend integrating renewable energy into national water security plans to ensure sustainable development. This can be achieved through pilot projects that demonstrate the effectiveness of combining renewable energy with water treatment facilities and irrigation systems.

Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is one of the least electrified regions globally, with only 53.3% of its population having access to electricity [3]. This limited energy supply severely hinders efforts to improve water security through treatment facilities and irrigation systems. Climate change intensifies these challenges by affecting rainfall patterns, leading to increased droughts and floods [4].

According to the World Bank Open Data, in 2024, only 32.4% of Sub-Saharan Africa's population has access to safely managed drinking water [1]. This percentage is even lower for rural areas where people often rely on contaminated surface waters.
