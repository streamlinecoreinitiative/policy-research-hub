**Climate-Resilient School Feeding Supply Chains in the Lower Mekong Delta: Supporting Adaptation Targets in NDCs**

## Executive Summary

The Lower Mekong delta faces a triple threat of water scarcity, energy poverty, and climate vulnerability. With only 32.4% of its population having access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank, 2024), and less than half relying on electricity (World Bank, 2023), urgent action is needed. This policy brief outlines actionable strategies for improving water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience in the region while aligning with global climate goals.

## Context & Challenge

The Lower Mekong delta is one of the most vulnerable regions to environmental challenges due to its reliance on natural resources. Water scarcity and inadequate access to clean energy disproportionately affect millions, exacerbating health issues and economic instability (UN-Water & WHO, 2022). Climate change intensifies these vulnerabilities through increased droughts and floods. Without significant improvements in water security, climate resilience, and renewable energy use, the region will struggle to meet the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs).

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Expand Water Infrastructure Investments**:
   - Invest in building and maintaining boreholes, wells, and pipelines to increase access to clean drinking water.
   - Implement community-led total sanitation programs (CLTS) to promote better hygiene practices and reduce waterborne diseases.

2. **Promote Renewable Energy Adoption**:
   - Encourage the installation of solar panels and small hydropower plants in rural areas where grid extension is not viable.
   - Provide subsidies for renewable energy systems and training on their maintenance.

3. **Enhance Climate Resilience Programs**:
   - Invest in early warning systems to alert communities about impending climate events like droughts or floods.
   - Develop disaster preparedness plans, including community-based evacuation routes and shelters.

## Implementation Considerations

- The initial investment for water infrastructure projects can range from $10 million to $50 million per project (World Bank estimates). Renewable energy projects often require smaller upfront costs but may involve higher maintenance fees.
- Potential barriers include limited government funding, lack of skilled labor, and resistance to change from traditional stakeholders. Addressing these through targeted training programs and community engagement can mitigate such challenges.

## Conclusion

Addressing water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience in the Lower Mekong delta is imperative for sustainable development and poverty reduction. By implementing the recommended policies and strategies, governments can significantly improve living conditions while contributing to global environmental goals. Collaboration between national authorities, international organizations, and local communities will be crucial for successful implementation.
