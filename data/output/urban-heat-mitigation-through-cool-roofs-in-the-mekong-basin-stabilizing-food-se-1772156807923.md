## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa is one of the regions most vulnerable to climate change impacts. Water scarcity, exacerbated by unreliable rainfall patterns, limits agricultural productivity and access to safe drinking water. Inadequate renewable energy infrastructure contributes to economic stagnation and environmental degradation. According to World Bank data (2024), only 32.4% of the population in Sub-Saharan Africa has access to safely managed drinking water, compared to the global average of 73.7% [Source: World Bank Open Data, 2024]. Renewable energy consumption is at a mere 9.2%, well below global averages [Source: World Bank Open Data, 2021].

## Analysis

The region's limited access to safe water not only impacts public health but also hinders economic growth. ## Policy Recommendations

To address water scarcity and inadequate renewable energy access in Sub-Saharan Africa, decision-makers should consider the following policy measures:

1. **Implement Community-Based Water Supply Systems**: Invest in decentralized water treatment facilities to ensure safe drinking water for rural populations. Collaborate with local communities to design and manage these systems.
2. **Increase Public-Private Partnerships for Clean Energy Projects**: Encourage partnerships between governments, private sector entities, and international organizations to finance clean energy initiatives. This can include solar-powered desalination plants or community-scale wind turbines.
3. **Strengthen Climate Adaptation Policies**: Develop comprehensive climate adaptation plans that integrate renewable energy solutions into water management strategies. Focus on early warning systems for droughts and floods.
