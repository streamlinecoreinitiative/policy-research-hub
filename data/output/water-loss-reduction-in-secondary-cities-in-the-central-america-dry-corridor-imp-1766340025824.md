# Water Loss Reduction in Secondary Cities in the Central America Dry Corridor: Improving Health Outcomes in Heatwaves

## Executive Summary

This brief highlights the critical issue of water loss reduction in secondary cities within the Central America dry corridor, a region prone to severe heatwaves and droughts. The integration of agroforestry with micro-hydro systems offers a promising solution to address water scarcity and poverty while ensuring economic benefits and sustainability.

## Water Scarcity in the Central America Dry Corridor

The Central America dry corridor is characterized by limited rainfall, high temperatures, and frequent droughts, making water scarcity a significant challenge for secondary cities. According to NOAA climate data, the region has experienced changes in rainfall patterns over the past decade, exacerbating water shortages.

### Current Irrigation Practices and Challenges

Existing irrigation systems in the Mesoamerican Highlands rely heavily on boreholes and canals, resulting in significant water loss due to evaporation and seepage. This inefficiency affects agricultural productivity and perpetuates poverty and food insecurity.

## Agroforestry's Role in Climate Resilience

Agroforestry practices have been shown to improve soil health, biodiversity, and carbon sequestration while reducing erosion. Case studies demonstrate that agroforestry can enhance water retention, allowing for more efficient irrigation methods.

### Micro-Hydro Systems for Efficiency

Micro-hydro technology offers a viable alternative to traditional diesel generators, providing increased energy efficiency and reduced reliance on non-renewable resources.

## Integration of Agroforestry and Micro-Hydro in Climate-Resilient Water Supply

Pilot projects showcasing the combined use of agroforestry and micro-hydro systems have demonstrated significant water savings and improved crop yields. Economic analysis highlights the cost-effectiveness of these integrated solutions, with potential for scaling up.
