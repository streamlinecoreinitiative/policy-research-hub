# watershed co-management with indigenous councils in the Indus watershed: stabilizing food security during droughts

**Watershed Co-management with Indigenous Councils in the Indus Watershed: Stabilizing Food Security during Droughts**

Executive Summary:

Climate change poses significant challenges to food security in lower-income countries, particularly in regions like the Indus watershed where droughts are becoming more frequent and severe. This brief explores the importance of watershed co-management with indigenous councils as a strategy for stabilizing food security during droughts. By examining existing insurance products and proposing a micro-insurance program, this report highlights the potential benefits of such initiatives in reducing climate vulnerability and enhancing food security.

**Climate Stress in Livestock Farming**

The Indus watershed is home to millions of livestock farmers who rely on rain-fed agriculture for their livelihoods. Climate change has led to changes in rainfall patterns, resulting in increased frequency and severity of droughts (UNDP, 2019). Droughts have devastating effects on livestock farming, causing significant economic losses due to reduced productivity and increased mortality rates.

**Current Insurance Market Analysis**

Existing insurance products available for livestock farmers in the Indus watershed are limited in their ability to address the specific needs of this sector. Current coverage rates are low, with only a small percentage of farmers having access to insurance (World Bank, 2018). Existing policies often fail to account for the unique risks faced by livestock farmers in drought-prone areas.

**Design of Micro-insurance Program**

The proposed micro-insurance program aims to provide timely financial support to farmers during extreme weather events. Key features include:

* Coverage areas: Targeting regions with high frequency and severity of droughts
* Premium structures: Affordable premiums based on risk assessment and farmer income
* Payout mechanisms: Quick disbursement of funds in response to drought declarations
* Partnerships: Collaboration with local organizations, government agencies, and indigenous councils

**Economic Impact Assessment**

Estimating the economic benefits of the micro-insurance program is challenging due to data gaps. However, potential outcomes include:

* Reduced livestock mortality rates
* Increased productivity through improved water management practices
* Improved food security for beneficiaries

**Sustainability and Scalability**

The micro-insurance model can be replicated in other regions or adapted to different contexts within the Indus watershed by:

* Building partnerships with local organizations and government agencies
* Conducting thorough risk assessments and premium structures
* Providing training and capacity-building programs for farmers and indigenous councils

**Policy Recommendations**

To facilitate the adoption and expansion of micro-insurance programs, governments should consider:

* Implementing supportive policies and regulations
* Allocating resources for capacity building and infrastructure development
* Encouraging private sector investment in agriculture insurance

Actions/Recommendations:

1. Conduct a thorough risk assessment to inform premium structures and coverage areas.
2. Engage with local organizations, government agencies, and indigenous councils to build partnerships and ensure effective implementation.
3. Provide training and capacity-building programs for farmers and indigenous councils on water management practices and micro-insurance program benefits.

Closing Outlook:

Watershed co-management with indigenous councils holds significant potential for stabilizing food security during droughts in the Indus watershed. By addressing data gaps through further research and analysis, policymakers can make informed decisions about implementing micro-insurance programs that meet the specific needs of livestock farmers in this region.
