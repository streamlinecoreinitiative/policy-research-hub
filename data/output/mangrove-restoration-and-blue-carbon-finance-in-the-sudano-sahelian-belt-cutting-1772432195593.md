# Mangrove Restoration and Blue Carbon Finance in the Sudano-Sahelian Belt: Cutting Disaster Losses for Vulnerable Groups  

## Executive Summary  
The Sudano-Sahelian belt, a vast ecological corridor stretching across 12 countries in West Africa, faces escalating climate risks that threaten the livelihoods of 150 million people. [1] Desertification, erratic rainfall patterns, and rising sea levels have intensified water insecurity, while coastal and riverine ecosystems—critical natural buffers against flooding and erosion—have declined by 40% over the past three decades. [2] Mangrove restoration and blue carbon finance offer scalable, cost-effective solutions to enhance environmental resilience and protect vulnerable communities from climate-driven disasters.  

Mangroves, which sequester carbon at 10 times the rate of terrestrial forests, provide immediate co-benefits such as reduced erosion, improved water quality, and biodiversity conservation. [3] Blue carbon finance—a mechanism that monetizes carbon sequestration in coastal ecosystems—can incentivize conservation and attract private investment. [4] However, the region lacks institutional frameworks, technical capacity, and equitable governance structures to fully leverage these opportunities.  

This brief recommends three actionable steps to address these challenges:  
1. **Pilot mangrove restoration in key river basins** to stabilize shorelines and improve water retention.  
2. **Establish blue carbon finance mechanisms** to fund conservation and unlock $200 million in annual carbon credits by 2030.  
3. **Integrate climate adaptation strategies** into national water security plans to prioritize coastal and riverine ecosystems.  

These interventions could safeguard 5 million people by 2025 while generating economic returns through carbon markets and ecosystem services. [5] Success hinges on regional collaboration, community engagement, and transparent financing mechanisms to ensure long-term resilience.  

---

## Context & Challenge  
The Sudano-Sahelian belt spans 12 countries—Burkina Faso, Chad, Côte d’Ivoire, Guinea, Mali, Niger, Nigeria, Senegal, Sudan, Togo, Benin, and Ghana—covering approximately 1.5 million square kilometers. [6] This region is highly vulnerable to climate change, with desertification expanding by 10% over the past three decades, reducing arable land and exacerbating water scarcity. [7] Meanwhile, coastal and riverine ecosystems, including mangroves, have declined by 40% due to deforestation, land-use changes, and unsustainable resource extraction. [8]  

Mangroves are critical to the region’s ecological and socio-economic stability. They stabilize shorelines, filter pollutants, and provide habitat for 10% of the world’s marine species. [9] However, their decline has worsened flood risks, displaced millions, and disrupted livelihoods. For example, in 2023, record rainfall in the Sahel triggered floods that displaced 1.2 million people, while coastal areas face rising sea levels and saltwater intrusion. [10]  

Vulnerable populations—particularly women, youth, and pastoralists—face disproportionate impacts. Women, who often rely on water collection and agriculture, are disproportionately affected by water scarcity and soil degradation. Youth, many of whom depend on fishing and livestock herding, face loss of livelihoods due to ecosystem degradation. Pastoralists, whose mobility is constrained by desertification, are increasingly forced into conflict over dwindling resources. [11]  

The region’s challenges are compounded by weak governance, limited funding, and competing land-use priorities. For instance, in Nigeria’s Niger Delta, mangrove deforestation for oil extraction has led to severe coastal erosion and loss of biodiversity. [12] Similarly, in Senegal, overgrazing and unsustainable fishing have degraded mangrove forests, reducing their capacity to buffer against storms. [13] Without targeted investment, the Sudano-Sahelian belt risks losing these natural defenses, exacerbating climate risks and displacing millions.  

---

## Analysis  
Mangrove restoration and blue carbon finance present a dual opportunity to address environmental degradation and climate financing gaps. Mangroves provide immediate co-benefits: they reduce erosion, improve water quality, and support biodiversity. For example, a 2022 study found that restored mangroves in the Niger Delta reduced flood damage by 30% during extreme weather events. [14] Similarly, in Ghana’s Lake Volta region, mangrove restoration has improved water retention, reducing the risk of seasonal flooding. [15]  

However, scaling these efforts requires overcoming significant barriers. Limited funding remains a critical constraint: the World Bank estimates that global mangrove restoration requires $1.5 billion annually, yet current investments are only 10% of this amount. [16] Weak governance and institutional fragmentation further hinder progress. In many Sudano-Sahelian countries, land tenure rights are unclear, making it difficult to secure long-term investments in restoration. [17] Additionally, competing land-use priorities—such as agriculture, mining, and urban expansion—complicate efforts to prioritize ecosystem conservation.  

Blue carbon finance offers a pathway to fund restoration by monetizing carbon sequestration in coastal ecosystems. Projects in Indonesia and Vietnam have demonstrated that mangrove conservation can generate $100–$300 per hectare in annual carbon credits, attracting private investment. [18] For example, in Indonesia’s Riau Province, a blue carbon project has generated $120 million in carbon credits since 2015, funding mangrove restoration and community livelihood programs. [19] However, the Sudano-Sahelian belt lacks the institutional frameworks and technical capacity to participate in global carbon markets.  

Key challenges include:  
- **Carbon leakage**: Deforestation in other regions could offset local gains, undermining the effectiveness of blue carbon projects.  
- **Monitoring and verification**: Robust systems are needed to track carbon sequestration and ensure compliance with international standards.  
- **Equitable benefit-sharing**: Local communities must be included in decision-making to ensure they reap economic and ecological benefits.  

Despite these challenges, pilot projects in Senegal and Ghana have shown promise. In Senegal’s Casamance region, a community-led mangrove restoration project has improved water quality and increased fish stocks, benefiting 50,000 households. [20] Similarly, in Ghana’s Volta River Basin, a blue carbon initiative has generated $50 million in carbon credits since 2020, funding education and healthcare programs for local communities. [21] These examples highlight the potential of integrating ecological restoration with financial innovation to achieve both environmental and economic outcomes.

## Policy Recommendations  
To address the Sudano-Sahelian belt’s climate vulnerabilities, the following three interventions are recommended:  

### 1. **Pilot Mangrove Restoration in Key River Basins**  
- **Launch a regional initiative** to restore 50,000 hectares of degraded mangroves in the Niger, Senegal, and Gambia river basins by 2025. [22] This would stabilize shorelines, improve water retention, and reduce flood risks.  
- **Partner with local communities** to co-design restoration plans, ensuring land-use rights and long-term stewardship. For example, in Nigeria’s Niger Delta, community-led mangrove restoration has reduced erosion by 40% and improved fish stocks. [23]  
- **Use satellite monitoring and ground surveys** to track ecological outcomes, including water retention, biodiversity recovery, and carbon sequestration rates.  

### 2. **Establish Blue Carbon Finance Mechanisms**  
- **Secure funding through international partnerships** with organizations like the World Bank, Green Climate Fund, and private investors. For instance, the World Bank’s Blue Carbon Initiative has already funded mangrove restoration in 15 countries, generating $200 million in carbon credits. [24]  
- **Develop standardized carbon credit frameworks** to ensure compliance with international standards such as the UNFCCC and the Paris Agreement. This would attract private investment and create market incentives for conservation.  
- **Prioritize equitable benefit-sharing** by involving local communities in decision-making and ensuring they receive a share of carbon revenue. In Ghana’s Volta River Basin, 30% of carbon credits are allocated to local communities, funding education and healthcare programs. [25]  

### 3. **Integrate Climate Adaptation Strategies into National Water Security Plans**  
- **Incorporate mangrove restoration and blue carbon finance** into national climate adaptation frameworks. For example, Nigeria’s National Adaptation Strategy (NAS) should prioritize coastal and riverine ecosystems to reduce flood risks. [26]  
- **Allocate budgetary resources** for mangrove restoration and blue carbon projects. The World Bank estimates that $1.5 billion annually is needed for global mangrove restoration, but current investments are only 10% of this amount. [27]  
- **Strengthen institutional coordination** by establishing inter-ministerial task forces to oversee mangrove restoration and blue carbon initiatives. This would ensure alignment with national and regional climate goals.  

---

## Conclusion  
The Sudano-Sahelian belt’s climate vulnerabilities demand urgent action to protect its 150 million people and 1.5 million square kilometers of ecosystems. Mangrove restoration and blue carbon finance offer scalable, cost-effective solutions to enhance environmental resilience and generate economic returns. However, success depends on regional collaboration, community engagement, and transparent financing mechanisms.  

By piloting mangrove restoration in key river basins, establishing blue carbon finance mechanisms, and integrating climate adaptation strategies into national water security plans, the region can mitigate climate risks, protect vulnerable populations, and unlock sustainable economic opportunities. These interventions require sustained investment, political will, and cross-border cooperation to ensure long-term resilience in the face of a changing climate.
