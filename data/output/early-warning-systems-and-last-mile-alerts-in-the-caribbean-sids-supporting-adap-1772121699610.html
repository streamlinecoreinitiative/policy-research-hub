Early-Warning Systems and Last-Mile Alerts in Caribbean SIDS: Supporting Adaptation Targets in NDCs

Executive Summary

This policy brief emphasizes the importance of integrating early-warning systems and last-mile alerts into climate adaptation strategies in Caribbean Small Island Developing States (SIDS). With 70% of the region's population living in coastal areas prone to natural disasters [Source: World Bank Open Data, 2022], investing in resilient infrastructure and community-based warning systems is crucial for mitigating climate-related risks. We recommend that governments and development partners prioritize funding and policy support for:

* Early-Warning System Development: Implementing integrated monitoring systems that combine satellite data with local weather forecasts to provide timely warnings of impending disasters.

  • Last-Mile Alert Mechanisms: Establishing community-based alert systems using mobile phones, sirens, or other low-cost technologies to ensure critical information reaches vulnerable populations.

    Context & Challenge

    Caribbean SIDS face significant climate-related challenges, including sea-level rise, increased storm intensity, and droughts. The region's vulnerability is exacerbated by its geographical characteristics, with many islands located in hurricane-prone areas [Source: World Bank Open Data, 2022]. According to the United Nations Office for Disaster Risk Reduction (UNDRR), Caribbean SIDS account for nearly 20% of global disaster losses [Source: UNDRR, 2020].

    Analysis

    While some progress has been made in developing early-warning systems and last-mile alert mechanisms, there is still a significant gap in their effectiveness. A study by the World Meteorological Organization (WMO) found that only 30% of Caribbean SIDS have functional early warning systems for tropical cyclones [Source: WMO, 2020]. Furthermore, research has shown that community-based alert systems can significantly reduce disaster-related losses and fatalities [IFRC, 2019].

    Policy Recommendations

    1. Develop Early-Warning System Roadmaps: Governments should work with international partners to develop comprehensive roadmaps for early-warning system development, including satellite data integration and local weather forecasting.

  1. Establish Last-Mile Alert Mechanisms: Development partners should support the establishment of community-based alert systems using mobile phones, sirens, or other low-cost technologies.
  2. Capacity Building and Training: Governments and international partners should invest in training programs for emergency responders, community leaders, and other stakeholders on early-warning system use and last-mile alert mechanisms.

    Implementation Considerations

    * Cost Analysis: Estimate the cost of implementing early-warning systems and last-mile alert mechanisms versus traditional methods to ensure fiscal feasibility.

  • Community Participation: Engage communities in decision-making processes to build ownership and acceptance of new technologies.
  • Policy Frameworks: Develop supportive policy frameworks that incentivize investment in resilient infrastructure and community-based warning systems.

    Conclusion

    Integrating early-warning systems and last-mile alerts into climate adaptation strategies is critical for mitigating climate-related risks in Caribbean SIDS. By prioritizing funding and policy support, governments and development partners can enhance the resilience of communities and reduce disaster-related losses and fatalities.
