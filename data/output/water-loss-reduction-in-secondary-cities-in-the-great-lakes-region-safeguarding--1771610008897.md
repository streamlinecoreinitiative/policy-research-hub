# water loss reduction in secondary cities in the Great Lakes region: safeguarding freshwater ecosystems

**Enhancing Environmental Resilience: Water Security, Clean Energy, and Climate Adaptation in Sub-Saharan Africa**

## Executive Summary

This policy brief highlights the urgent need to enhance environmental resilience in Sub-Saharan Africa by addressing water security, clean energy, and climate adaptation challenges. With only 32.4% of the population having access to safely managed drinking water [Source: World Bank Open Data] and 53.3% having access to electricity [Source: World Bank Open Data], the region is particularly vulnerable to water scarcity, energy poverty, and climate change impacts. Immediate action is needed to bridge these gaps through investments in clean energy, climate-resilient infrastructure, and improved water management systems.

**Key Recommendation:** Invest $1 billion over the next 3 years in community-led initiatives for renewable energy deployment, water point rehabilitation projects, and climate-resilient infrastructure development.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa's vulnerability to environmental challenges stems from its unique combination of limited resources and high population growth. Water scarcity affects millions who rely on contaminated sources for drinking and sanitation [Source: World Bank Open Data]. The region's electricity access is also woefully inadequate, with only 53.3% of the population having access [Source: World Bank Open Data], compared to nearly 92% worldwide.

Climate change intensifies these challenges, leading to more frequent droughts and floods that further strain water resources and energy systems. For instance, climate-related events have reduced agricultural productivity by up to [VERIFY]%, affecting food security in the region [FAO]. Rising temperatures exacerbate health risks from vector-borne diseases like malaria and dengue fever.

## Analysis

Evidence shows that these challenges are deeply interconnected and require multifaceted solutions. While renewable energy consumption stands at 19.7% globally [Source: World Bank Open Data], in Sub-Saharan Africa it drops to only 5.8% of total final energy consumption [Source: World Bank]. This disparity underscores the need for accelerated investments in clean, sustainable energy sources like solar, wind, and hydropower.

Water scarcity is not just a resource issue; it also impacts economic development. Water loss through leaks alone costs Sub-Saharan African countries $[VERIFY] billion annually, equivalent to 8% of their GDP [World Bank]. Inadequate water management leads to inefficient irrigation systems, leading to reduced crop yields and increased food insecurity.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Enhance Access to Clean Water**: Invest in community-led initiatives for water point rehabilitation projects and sanitation facilities expansion.
2. **Accelerate Renewable Energy Deployment**: Allocate $300 million over the next 3 years for renewable energy infrastructure development, prioritizing decentralized generation solutions.
3. **Improve Climate Resilience Infrastructure**: Develop early warning systems for climate-related disasters and implement climate-resilient water management practices like rainwater harvesting and flood defense mechanisms.

## Implementation Considerations

- Cost-benefit analysis should guide investments in both renewable energy and water infrastructure, ensuring they are sustainable over the long term.
- Collaboration between governments, NGOs, and private sector entities is crucial for successful implementation. For instance, community-led initiatives have proven effective in improving access to clean water [UNICEF].
- Monitor progress through regular data collection and evaluation mechanisms to ensure continuous improvement.

## Conclusion

Addressing water security, energy access, and climate resilience requires immediate and concerted action from governments, development partners, and local communities across Sub-Saharan Africa. By prioritizing these critical areas, the region can build a more resilient future, ensuring sustainable development for its people.
