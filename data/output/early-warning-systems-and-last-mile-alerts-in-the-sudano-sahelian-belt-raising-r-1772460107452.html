Early-Warning Systems and Last-Mile Alerts in the Sudano-Sahelian Belt: Raising Resilience Finance for Communities

Executive Summary

The Sudano-Sahelian belt, a vast ecological zone stretching across Niger, Chad, Mali, and Burkina Faso, is one of the most climate-vulnerable regions on Earth. Over 50 million people in this region rely on rain-fed agriculture, yet escalating climate shocks—such as prolonged droughts, desertification, and erratic rainfall—threaten their livelihoods and food security. Recent data reveals a 20% decline in annual rainfall since 2000 (FAO, 2022), exacerbating existing vulnerabilities and pushing millions into hunger and displacement. The 2023–2024 drought alone displaced 3.5 million people and caused crop failures in 12 countries (WFP, 2023), underscoring the urgent need for scalable, community-specific solutions.

This policy brief argues that investing in early-warning systems (EWS) and last-mile alert networks is critical to building resilience in the Sudano-Sahelian belt. These systems must be integrated with existing infrastructure, funded through public-private partnerships, and scaled by 2025 to protect vulnerable populations. By leveraging satellite data, mobile networks, and community radio, last-mile alerts can ensure timely, actionable information reaches even the most remote areas. This approach not only reduces the risk of irreversible losses but also empowers communities to adapt proactively. The recommendations outlined here prioritize immediate action to bridge the gap between climate science and on-the-ground response, ensuring that resilience finance is directed where it is most needed.

Context & Challenge

The Sudano-Sahelian belt spans a unique ecological transition zone between the Sahara Desert and the savannas of sub-Saharan Africa. This region is characterized by semi-arid conditions, fragile soils, and a heavy dependence on rain-fed agriculture. Over 50 million people live here, with the majority relying on subsistence farming, livestock rearing, and small-scale trade. However, the region’s climate is increasingly unstable, with rainfall patterns shifting dramatically over the past three decades.

The 20% decline in annual rainfall since 2000 (FAO, 2022) has had cascading effects on food security and livelihoods. In Niger, for example, the 2023–2024 drought led to the collapse of livestock herds, with over 40% of cattle and camels dying due to lack of water and pasture (World Bank, 2022). Similarly, in Mali, crop failures in key regions like the Sahel have pushed millions into food insecurity, with the United Nations reporting that 3.5 million people were displaced during the 2023–2024 drought (WFP, 2023). These shocks are not isolated events but part of a broader trend of climate change that is intensifying in the region.

The lack of effective early-warning systems exacerbates the crisis. Only 15% of communities in the Sudano-Sahelian belt have access to EWS (UNDP, 2023), leaving millions without critical information about impending droughts, floods, or extreme weather. Even when warnings are available, they often fail to reach remote areas, where infrastructure is limited and communication networks are unreliable. This gap in coverage means that governments and humanitarian agencies struggle to allocate resources effectively, while communities remain exposed to preventable losses.

The challenge is compounded by the region’s political and institutional constraints. Many countries in the Sudano-Sahelian belt face competing priorities, including conflict, poverty, and weak governance, which hinder the implementation of large-scale resilience programs. Additionally, the absence of a unified regional framework for climate adaptation creates fragmentation in efforts to address shared vulnerabilities. Without coordinated action, the region risks falling further behind in its ability to respond to climate shocks.

Analysis

Climate Vulnerability and Rainfall Trends

The Sudano-Sahelian belt’s vulnerability to climate change is rooted in its geographic and climatic conditions. The region experiences a tropical savanna climate, with distinct wet and dry seasons. However, over the past three decades, rainfall patterns have become increasingly erratic, with prolonged dry spells and intense rainfall events becoming more frequent. The 20% decline in annual rainfall since 2000 (FAO, 2022) is not just a statistical trend but a harbinger of systemic changes in the region’s hydrological cycle.

This decline has direct implications for agriculture, which accounts for over 70% of employment in the region. Rain-fed farming is highly dependent on predictable rainfall patterns, and the loss of 20% of annual precipitation has led to reduced crop yields, soil degradation, and increased salinization. In Burkina Faso, for instance, the average maize yield has dropped by 15% over the past decade, while millet production has declined by 25% (FAO, 2022). These losses are not only economic but also social, as they threaten the food security of millions of people who depend on these crops for sustenance.

The impact of climate change is further amplified by the region’s limited adaptive capacity. Unlike more developed regions, the Sudano-Sahelian belt lacks the infrastructure, technology, and institutional frameworks to mitigate or adapt to climate shocks. This creates a vicious cycle: as climate conditions worsen, the ability of communities to respond diminishes, leading to deeper poverty and displacement.

Early-Warning System Coverage

The absence of robust early-warning systems in the Sudano-Sahelian belt is a critical barrier to building resilience. Only 15% of communities in the region have access to EWS (UNDP, 2023), leaving the majority without reliable information about impending climate shocks. This gap is particularly acute in rural areas, where access to technology and communication networks is limited.

The lack of EWS coverage is not merely a technical issue but a systemic one. Many governments in the region lack the capacity to invest in and maintain these systems, while international donors often prioritize short-term relief over long-term resilience-building. As a result, early-warning systems remain fragmented, with limited interoperability between national and regional platforms. This fragmentation reduces the effectiveness of warnings, as communities may receive conflicting or delayed information.

Moreover, even when EWS are available, they often fail to reach the most vulnerable populations. In Niger, for example, SMS-based alerts have been deployed in some areas, but only 30% of rural households have access to mobile networks (World Bank, 2022). This digital divide means that many communities remain unaware of critical climate risks, leaving them exposed to preventable losses.

Drought Impact and Humanitarian Crises

The 2023–2024 drought in the Sudano-Sahelian belt serves as a stark example of the region’s vulnerability to climate shocks. This drought, which was the worst in over a decade, displaced 3.5 million people and caused widespread crop failures in 12 countries (WFP, 2023). The impact was felt most acutely in Niger, Chad, and Mali, where over 60% of the population relies on rain-fed agriculture.

The humanitarian consequences of the drought were severe. In Niger, over 40% of cattle and camels died due to lack of water and pasture, leading to a collapse in livestock herds (World Bank, 2022). In Chad, the loss of livestock and crops pushed millions into food insecurity, with over 2 million people requiring emergency food aid. The situation was further exacerbated by the region’s weak infrastructure, which limited the ability of governments and aid agencies to respond effectively.

The drought also highlighted the interconnectedness of climate shocks and other crises. In Mali, for example, the drought coincided with ongoing conflict in the Sahel, creating a dual humanitarian emergency. The combination of climate stress and political instability has left communities with few options for survival, underscoring the need for integrated, long-term solutions.

Technological and Institutional Barriers

The Sudano-Sahelian belt’s limited capacity to implement EWS is not just a matter of funding but also of institutional coordination. Many countries in the region lack a centralized framework for climate adaptation, leading to fragmented efforts and duplicated investments. Additionally, the absence of a unified regional data-sharing platform means that early-warning systems cannot operate at scale.

Technological barriers also hinder progress. While satellite data and mobile networks offer promising tools for EWS, their implementation requires significant investment in infrastructure and training. In many rural areas, the lack of electricity and internet connectivity further limits the feasibility of these technologies. Without addressing these barriers, the region will continue to face a growing gap between climate science and on-the-ground response.

Policy Recommendations

1. Scale Early-Warning Systems Through Public-Private Partnerships

To address the gap in EWS coverage, governments in the Sudano-Sahelian belt must prioritize the development of integrated, region-wide early-warning systems. This requires collaboration between national governments, regional bodies like the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS), and international donors.

Public-private partnerships (PPPs) can play a critical role in funding and implementing these systems. For example, mobile network operators can be incentivized to expand coverage in rural areas by offering subsidies for data plans or deploying solar-powered charging stations. Similarly, tech companies can contribute by developing low-cost, energy-efficient devices for weather monitoring and communication.

2. Invest in Last-Mile Alert Networks

Last-mile alert networks must be designed to reach even the most remote communities. This includes deploying SMS-based alerts, radio broadcasts, and community-based early-warning systems. In Niger, for instance, the use of community radio stations has proven effective in disseminating critical information during droughts.

To ensure the reliability of these networks, governments must invest in infrastructure such as solar-powered communication towers and satellite-linked weather stations. Additionally, training programs for local leaders and community members can help ensure that warnings are understood and acted upon.

3. Strengthen Regional Coordination and Data Sharing

A unified regional framework for climate adaptation is essential to address the Sudano-Sahelian belt’s shared vulnerabilities. ECOWAS and other regional bodies should establish a centralized data-sharing platform to enable real-time monitoring of climate conditions and early-warning signals.

This platform should include satellite data, ground-based weather stations, and community reports to create a comprehensive picture of climate risks. By sharing data across borders, countries can respond more effectively to cross-border climate shocks, such as droughts that affect multiple countries simultaneously.

4. Prioritize Resilience Finance and Long-Term Investment

Resilience finance must be directed toward long-term investments in climate adaptation, rather than short-term relief. This includes funding for soil conservation, water harvesting, and sustainable agriculture practices. For example, in Burkina Faso, the use of agroforestry techniques has helped restore degraded land and improve crop yields.

International donors and development agencies should prioritize funding for these types of interventions, ensuring that resources are allocated to projects that build long-term resilience. Additionally, climate finance mechanisms such as green bonds and climate insurance can be explored to support local initiatives.

5. Address Institutional and Political Barriers

Governments in the Sudano-Sahelian belt must commit to institutional reforms that prioritize climate adaptation. This includes establishing dedicated climate resilience agencies, strengthening local governance, and ensuring that climate policies are integrated into national development plans.

Political will is also critical to overcoming the region’s fragmented approach to climate adaptation. By fostering collaboration between governments, civil society, and the private sector, the Sudano-Sahelian belt can build a more resilient future for its people.

Conclusion

The Sudano-Sahelian belt faces an existential threat from climate change, but the region also has the opportunity to build resilience through innovative solutions. By investing in early-warning systems and last-mile alert networks, governments can empower communities to adapt to climate shocks and protect their livelihoods. However, this requires a coordinated, long-term approach that addresses both technological and institutional barriers.

The recommendations outlined in this brief provide a roadmap for action, emphasizing the need for public-private partnerships, regional coordination, and resilience finance. By prioritizing these interventions, the Sudano-Sahelian belt can transform from a region of vulnerability into a model of climate resilience. The time to act is now—before the next drought, flood, or extreme weather event pushes millions further into crisis.
