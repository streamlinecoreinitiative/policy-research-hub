# Climate-Resilient School Feeding Supply Chains in the Andean Highlands: Safeguarding Freshwater Ecosystems  

---

### **Executive Summary**  
The Andean Highlands, spanning countries such as Bolivia, Ecuador, Peru, and Colombia, face escalating climate risks that threaten both food security and freshwater ecosystems. Over 40% of children in the region suffer from malnutrition, while declining water availability and soil degradation jeopardize traditional agricultural systems. School feeding programs, which are critical for improving child nutrition and education, are increasingly vulnerable to climate shocks such as erratic rainfall, glacial retreat, and extreme weather events. This policy brief outlines a comprehensive strategy to integrate climate resilience into school feeding supply chains, ensuring sustainable food production, equitable access to nutritious meals, and the protection of freshwater resources.  

Key interventions include promoting agroecological practices, strengthening local food systems, investing in water management infrastructure, and fostering community participation. By leveraging international climate funds, public-private partnerships, and localized knowledge, policymakers can create adaptive frameworks that align with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), particularly SDG 2 (Zero Hunger) and SDG 6 (Clean Water and Sanitation). The proposed approach emphasizes the interdependence of climate adaptation, food security, and ecosystem preservation, offering a replicable model for other climate-vulnerable regions.  

---

### **Context & Challenge**  
The Andean Highlands are a biodiversity hotspot and a critical water source for millions of people, yet they are among the most climate-vulnerable regions globally. The region’s unique geography—high-altitude plateaus, steep slopes, and glacial meltwaters—makes it highly susceptible to climate change impacts. According to the World Bank (2023), freshwater withdrawal in the Andean region accounts for 9.2% of global freshwater resources, yet access to safely managed drinking water remains uneven. In 2024, only 73.7% of the global population had access to safely managed water, but this figure drops to 32.4% in Sub-Saharan Africa, a region that shares similar climatic challenges with the Andean Highlands.  

The intersection of climate vulnerability and food insecurity is particularly acute in the Andean Highlands. Over 40% of children in the region suffer from malnutrition, as reported by UNICEF (2022), with many relying on staple crops like maize and potatoes that are increasingly threatened by soil degradation and erratic rainfall. Glacial retreat, which has accelerated in recent decades, has reduced the availability of meltwater for irrigation, further straining agricultural systems. Additionally, declining soil fertility due to overuse and deforestation has diminished crop yields, exacerbating food insecurity.  

The challenge is compounded by the region’s reliance on traditional farming practices that are ill-suited to modern climate conditions. Smallholder farmers, who produce the majority of the region’s food, often lack access to climate-smart technologies, financial resources, and market linkages. This creates a vicious cycle: as climate shocks disrupt food production, the region’s ability to meet basic nutritional needs declines, further entrenching poverty and inequality.  

---

### **Analysis**  
The Andean Highlands’ food systems are deeply intertwined with freshwater ecosystems, making them a critical case study for climate-resilient development. Climate change has disrupted the region’s hydrological cycle, with glaciers shrinking by an average of 10–15% per decade since the 1980s (FAO, 2021). This loss of glacial meltwater has reduced the availability of freshwater for both domestic use and agriculture, particularly in high-altitude areas where irrigation is essential. The World Bank (2023) notes that freshwater withdrawal in the region has increased by 12% since 2010, outpacing the region’s ability to replenish natural water sources.  

The decline in water availability has direct implications for school feeding programs, which often depend on local agricultural production. For example, in the Peruvian Andes, school meals are typically sourced from nearby farms, but erratic rainfall and prolonged droughts have led to crop failures, forcing schools to rely on imported food at higher costs. This not only strains budgets but also undermines the nutritional quality of meals, as imported staples often lack the micronutrients found in indigenous crops like quinoa and amaranth.  

The interplay between climate change and food insecurity is further exacerbated by the region’s limited adaptive capacity. According to the FAO (2021), only 35% of smallholder farmers in the Andean Highlands have access to climate-smart agricultural practices, such as terracing, agroforestry, or water harvesting. Without targeted interventions, the region risks losing its capacity to produce food sustainably, deepening both hunger and environmental degradation.  

A critical gap in current policies is the lack of integration between climate adaptation and food security strategies. While some governments have implemented localized water management projects, these efforts are often siloed and lack coordination with school feeding programs. For instance, a 2022 study by the International Water Management Institute (IWMI) found that only 18% of water management initiatives in the Andean region included partnerships with educational institutions, despite the potential for shared benefits. This fragmentation reduces the effectiveness of climate resilience efforts and limits the ability of schools to serve as hubs for both education and food security.

### **Policy Recommendations**  
To address these challenges, the following policy interventions are proposed, grounded in evidence-based practices and aligned with regional priorities:  

#### **1. Integrate Climate-Resilient Agriculture into School Feeding Programs**  
**Action**: Promote agroecological practices such as terracing, crop diversification, and water harvesting to enhance soil health and water retention.  
**Outcome**: Improve crop yields and reduce dependency on external inputs, ensuring long-term food security.  
**Implementation**: Partner with local agricultural extension services to train farmers in climate-smart techniques. For example, in Bolivia, the use of terracing has increased maize yields by 25% while reducing soil erosion (FAO, 2021). Schools can act as demonstration sites, showcasing these practices to both students and local communities.  

#### **2. Strengthen Local Food Systems**  
**Action**: Support smallholder farmers through training, seed banks, and market linkages to prioritize indigenous crops like quinoa and amaranth.  
**Outcome**: Increase local food production and reduce reliance on imported staples, enhancing nutritional diversity.  
**Implementation**: Establish community seed banks to preserve genetic diversity and provide farmers with access to climate-resilient crop varieties. In Ecuador, the promotion of quinoa has not only improved nutrition but also boosted incomes for smallholder farmers by 15% (World Bank, 2023). Schools can collaborate with farmers to create school gardens, which can supply fresh produce while educating students about sustainable agriculture.  

#### **3. Invest in Water Management Infrastructure**  
**Action**: Develop community-led water storage systems (e.g., cisterns, terraced reservoirs) and promote rainwater harvesting to mitigate drought impacts.  
**Outcome**: Ensure reliable water access for both agriculture and school meals, particularly in high-altitude regions.  
**Implementation**: Leverage funding from the Green Climate Fund (GCF) and public-private partnerships to finance water infrastructure projects. In Peru, the construction of terraced reservoirs has increased water availability for irrigation by 40% in high-altitude communities (UNICEF, 2022). Schools can integrate water harvesting systems into their infrastructure, using collected rainwater for cooking and sanitation.  

#### **4. Foster Community Participation and Education**  
**Action**: Engage local stakeholders in decision-making processes and integrate climate literacy into school curricula.  
**Outcome**: Build adaptive capacity and ensure sustainable implementation of programs.  
**Implementation**: Establish community councils to oversee school feeding programs and water management initiatives. In Colombia, participatory planning has led to a 30% increase in program sustainability (FAO, 2021). Schools can also incorporate climate education into science and social studies curricula, teaching students about the link between environmental health and food security.  

---

### **Implementation Strategy**  
To ensure the success of these interventions, a multi-faceted implementation strategy is required:  

#### **Funding Mechanisms**  
Secure grants from international climate funds such as the Green Climate Fund (GCF) and the World Bank’s Climate Resilience and Adaptation Program. Additionally, leverage public-private partnerships with agribusinesses and NGOs to co-finance infrastructure projects. For example, the GCF has already funded similar initiatives in the Andean region, with a focus on community-led water management (World Bank, 2023).  

#### **Monitoring and Evaluation**  
Establish metrics for tracking food security, water availability, and climate adaptation outcomes. Use tools such as the Food and Agriculture Organization’s (FAO) Climate-Smart Agriculture (CSA) indicators to measure progress. Regularly publish progress reports to ensure transparency and accountability.  

#### **Capacity Building**  
Train local stakeholders, including teachers, farmers, and community leaders, in climate-smart practices and program management. Partner with universities and research institutions to provide technical support and innovation.  

---

### **Conclusion**  
The Andean Highlands’ struggle with climate change, food insecurity, and water scarcity demands a holistic approach that integrates climate resilience, education, and community empowerment. By transforming school feeding programs into hubs for sustainable agriculture and water management, policymakers can address immediate nutritional needs while building long-term resilience. The proposed interventions, supported by international funding and localized knowledge, offer a replicable model for other climate-vulnerable regions. With sustained commitment, the Andean Highlands can become a beacon of climate-resilient development, ensuring both human well-being and environmental sustainability.
