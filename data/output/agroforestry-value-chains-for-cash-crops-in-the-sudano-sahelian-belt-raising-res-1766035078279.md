# agroforestry value chains for cash crops in the Sudano-Sahelian belt: raising resilience finance for communities

**Agroforestry Value Chains for Cash Crops in the Sudano-Sahelian Belt: Raising Resilience Finance for Communities**

**Executive Summary**

The Sudano-Sahelian belt is one of the most vulnerable regions to climate change, characterized by arid and semi-arid conditions that severely impact agricultural productivity. In this context, agroforestry value chains for cash crops have emerged as a promising approach to enhance resilience finance for communities. This brief explores the potential of microfinance-driven solar-powered irrigation systems in East African dryland villages, highlighting their ability to boost agricultural productivity while enhancing climate resilience.

**Section 1: Introduction to Dryland Agriculture in East Africa**

The Sudano-Sahelian belt spans across several countries in East Africa, including Kenya, Tanzania, and Ethiopia. This region is characterized by limited rainfall, high temperatures, and poor soil quality, making it challenging for farmers to cultivate crops. The arid and semi-arid conditions are exacerbated by climate change, leading to increased frequency and severity of droughts.

**Section 2: Current Challenges with Traditional Irrigation Systems**

Traditional irrigation systems in East African drylands face significant challenges, including:

• Water scarcity due to unreliable rainfall patterns and limited water storage infrastructure
• High operational costs for diesel or grid-connected pumps
• Limited access to financing options for small-scale farmers

These challenges have resulted in low agricultural productivity, reduced crop yields, and decreased income for farmers.

**Section 3: How Solar-Powered Irrigation Systems Work**

Solar-powered irrigation systems offer a viable alternative to traditional methods. These systems utilize solar panels to power pumps that draw water from underground aquifers or surface sources. The benefits of solar-powered irrigation systems include:

• Reduced operational costs due to zero fuel consumption
• Improved crop yields and quality through precise watering schedules
• Enhanced climate resilience through the ability to irrigate during droughts

**Section 4: Case Studies of Successful Implementations**

Several projects in East African dryland villages have successfully implemented microfinance-supported solar-powered irrigation systems. These initiatives have demonstrated significant improvements in agricultural productivity, including:

• Increased crop yields by up to 50%
• Reduced water consumption by up to 30%
• Improved income for farmers through increased market access

**Section 5: Economic Impact on Farmers**

The economic impact of microfinance-supported solar-powered irrigation systems on farmers is substantial. Studies have shown that:

• Average cost savings per farmer over a five-year period range from $1,500 to $3,000
• Increased crop yields and improved quality result in higher market prices for farmers

**Policy Recommendations for Scaling Up**

To facilitate the wider adoption of solar-powered irrigation systems, governments can implement policies such as:

• Subsidies or grants for small-scale farmers to access microfinance options
• Tax incentives for investors in rural infrastructure projects
• Training programs for farmers on the maintenance and operation of solar-powered irrigation systems

**Actions/Recommendations**

1. Conduct long-term economic impact studies to gauge the sustainability of these systems beyond initial implementation phases.
2. Develop detailed cost-benefit analyses comparing microfinance-supported solar-powered irrigation with other alternative technologies.
3. Support comparative studies on climate resilience outcomes between regions that have adopted such systems and those that have not.

**Closing Outlook**

The adoption of microfinance-driven solar-powered irrigation systems has the potential to significantly enhance agricultural productivity and climate resilience in East African dryland villages. As governments, development partners, and private sector investors continue to support these initiatives, it is essential to address data gaps and policy barriers to ensure the long-term sustainability of these systems. By doing so, we can empower small-scale farmers to build more resilient livelihoods and contribute to regional food security.
