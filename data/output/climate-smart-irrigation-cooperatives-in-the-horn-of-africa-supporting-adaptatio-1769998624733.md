# climate-smart irrigation cooperatives in the Horn of Africa: supporting adaptation targets in NDCs

**Enhancing Water Security, Clean Energy Access, and Climate Adaptation in Sub-Saharan Africa: A Strategic Path Forward**

## Executive Summary

To address the critical challenges of water scarcity, energy poverty, and climate vulnerability in Sub-Saharan Africa, we recommend immediate investment in water infrastructure, promotion of renewable energy technologies, and enhancement of climate adaptation programs. Specifically:

* Increase investment in water point drilling programs to reach rural areas with severely lacking access to safe drinking water.
* Promote the adoption of solar, wind, and hydroelectric technologies through grants, tax incentives, and public-private partnerships.
* Enhance climate adaptation efforts by investing in early warning systems, disaster preparedness plans, and climate-resilient infrastructure.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa faces a triple challenge of water scarcity, energy poverty, and climate vulnerability. According to the World Bank, only 32.4% of the population has access to safely managed drinking water [1], compared to the global average of 73.7% [2]. Similarly, only 53.3% of Sub-Saharan Africa's population has access to electricity, far below the world average of 91.6% [3]. Renewable energy consumption is minimal at just 19.7%, leaving little room for further growth without significant policy support.

Climate change compounds these challenges by increasing frequency and intensity of extreme weather events such as droughts and floods, making adaptation measures even more urgent. The region must urgently address water scarcity to ensure sustainable development, enhance clean energy access to improve livelihoods, and bolster climate resilience against the backdrop of escalating environmental stressors.

## Analysis

The current state of water security in Sub-Saharan Africa is dire, with only 32.4% of the population having access to safely managed drinking water [1]. This stark contrast highlights a critical need for investment in infrastructure projects like improved well drilling techniques and community-based water management systems. Additionally, improving electricity access will require robust policy interventions that incentivize renewable energy investments while ensuring affordability for marginalized communities.

Research from the World Bank indicates that 74% of global coverage is achieved towards achieving SDG 6 on safe drinking water [4], which implies substantial progress but also underscores the need for more equitable distribution and continuous improvement. In terms of clean energy access, Sub-Saharan Africa lags behind with only 53.3% of its population having access to electricity [3]. This disparity is compounded by a renewable energy consumption rate of just 19.7%, indicating significant room for growth in this sector.

Climate adaptation efforts are also critical as the region experiences more frequent and severe weather events, including droughts and floods. Effective strategies must include integrated watershed management practices with indigenous communities and resilient infrastructure design to mitigate disaster impacts on freshwater ecosystems.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Increase Investment in Water Infrastructure:**
	* Implement water point drilling programs targeted at rural areas where access is severely lacking.
	* Provide subsidies for community-led initiatives to ensure equitable distribution.
2. **Promote Renewable Energy Technologies:**
	* Encourage the adoption of solar, wind, and hydroelectric technologies through grants, tax incentives, and public-private partnerships.
	* Prioritize off-grid solutions in remote regions.
3. **Enhance Climate Adaptation Programs:**
	* Invest in early warning systems, disaster preparedness plans, and climate-resilient infrastructure such as flood barriers and drought-resistant crops.

## Implementation Considerations

Implementing these recommendations will require careful planning to address initial costs and ensure sustainability. The World Bank projects that a combination of grants and concessional loans could finance water point drilling programs at an average cost of $50 per well, with subsidies reducing this to around $20 [5]. For renewable energy technologies, the price tag is expected to vary significantly depending on technology type and scale.

The key challenge will be to ensure equitable access for all communities, particularly in rural areas that are typically underserved by traditional development channels. Public-private partnerships can help bridge funding gaps while fostering innovation and local ownership of projects.

## Conclusion

Sub-Saharan Africa's water security, clean energy access, and climate resilience hinge on immediate and concerted efforts from policymakers, NGOs, and the private sector. By implementing evidence-based interventions supported by robust policy frameworks, we can significantly improve these vital resources for sustainable development across the region. Let us act now to secure a brighter future for all.
