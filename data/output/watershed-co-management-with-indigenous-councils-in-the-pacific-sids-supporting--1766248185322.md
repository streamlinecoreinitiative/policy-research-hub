# Watershed Co-management with Indigenous Councils in Pacific SIDS: Enhancing Climate Resilience through Integrated Approaches

## Executive Summary

Climate change poses significant threats to water security, clean energy, and climate adaptation in lower-income countries in the Pacific Small Island Developing States (SIDS). This brief highlights the importance of watershed co-management with indigenous councils as a critical strategy for supporting Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) adaptation targets. By integrating traditional knowledge with modern technologies and practices, SIDS can enhance their resilience to climate-related challenges while promoting sustainable development.

## I. Introduction to Water Scarcity in Pacific SIDS

Pacific SIDS are highly vulnerable to the impacts of climate change due to their geographical location and limited resources. Rising temperatures, sea-level rise, and extreme weather events exacerbate water scarcity, threatening food security, economic stability, and human well-being.

## II. Current Water Management Practices and Challenges

Existing water management practices in Pacific SIDS often rely on traditional systems, which are increasingly under strain due to climate change. Key challenges include:

*   **Water loss:** [Verify] percentages of water are lost through evaporation and seepage, reducing the efficiency of irrigation systems.
*   **Limited access:** Many communities lack access to reliable and safe drinking water, exacerbating health issues and economic burdens.

## III. Agroforestry and Micro-Hydro Systems for Climate Resilience

Agroforestry and micro-hydro systems offer promising solutions for enhancing climate resilience in Pacific SIDS:

*   **Agroforestry:** Integrates trees into agricultural landscapes to improve soil health, biodiversity, and carbon sequestration.
*   **Micro-hydro:** Small-scale hydroelectric systems reduce reliance on diesel generators, increasing energy efficiency while minimizing greenhouse gas emissions.

## IV. Integration of Agroforestry and Micro-Hydro in Watershed Co-management

Pilot projects demonstrate the effectiveness of combining agroforestry with micro-hydro systems:

*   **Increased water retention:** Agroforestry enhances soil moisture, reducing erosion and runoff.
*   **Improved energy efficiency:** Micro-hydro systems reduce energy costs while promoting renewable energy adoption.

Watershed co-management with indigenous councils holds significant potential for enhancing climate resilience in Pacific SIDS while promoting sustainable development.
