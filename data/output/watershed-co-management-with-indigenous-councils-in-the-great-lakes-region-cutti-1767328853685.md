# Watershed Co-management with Indigenous Councils in the Great Lakes Region: Enhancing Climate Resilience and Disaster Risk Reduction

## Executive Summary

The Great Lakes region is home to numerous indigenous communities that have traditionally managed their watersheds sustainably. However, climate change and increasing human activities are putting pressure on these ecosystems, leading to water scarcity, soil erosion, and increased disaster risk. This brief outlines the importance of watershed co-management with indigenous councils in addressing these challenges.

## Introduction to Water Scarcity in the Great Lakes Region

The Great Lakes region is one of the most biodiverse areas globally, with numerous indigenous communities relying heavily on water resources for their livelihoods. Climate change is altering precipitation patterns, leading to increased frequency and severity of droughts and floods (Source: [verify]). This has severe consequences for agriculture, which is a primary source of income for many households in the region.

## Current Irrigation Practices and Challenges

Traditional irrigation systems in the Great Lakes region often rely on boreholes and canals. However, these systems are plagued by inefficiencies, with water loss due to evaporation and seepage reported as significant (Source: [verify]). Diesel generators used for pumping water contribute significantly to greenhouse gas emissions.

## Micro-Hydro Systems for Irrigation Efficiency

Micro-hydro technology offers a sustainable solution for irrigation by harnessing energy from small-scale hydroelectric systems. This approach reduces reliance on diesel generators, decreases greenhouse gas emissions, and increases energy efficiency.

## Integration in Climate-Resilient Water Supply

Pilot projects have successfully integrated agroforestry and micro-hydro systems to provide climate-resilient water supply for agriculture. These initiatives demonstrate the potential for scaling up and reducing disaster losses for vulnerable groups.
