# Clean Cooking Transition for Peri-Urban Households in the Horn of Africa: Supporting Adaptation Targets in NDCs

## Executive Summary

The Horn of Africa is one of the most water-scarce regions globally, facing severe droughts and climate variability. The transition to clean cooking practices is crucial for addressing these issues, but it requires a comprehensive approach that integrates water security, clean energy, and climate adaptation strategies. This brief outlines the importance of agroforestry-based micro-hydro systems in ensuring sustainable water management, economic development, and climate resilience in the region.

## Addressing Water Scarcity in the Horn of Africa

The Horn of Africa is characterized by a semi-arid to arid climate, with limited rainfall and high evaporation rates. This has led to severe water scarcity, exacerbated by historical droughts and current challenges such as limited access to reliable irrigation systems and economic instability due to reduced crop yields.

## Current Practices and Challenges

Peri-urban households in the Horn of Africa rely heavily on traditional methods for cooking, including charcoal and firewood. These practices contribute to water scarcity, deforestation, air pollution, and greenhouse gas emissions.

## Agroforestry-Based Micro-Hydro Systems: A Solution for Climate-Resilient Irrigation

Agroforestry-based micro-hydro systems offer a promising solution for addressing water scarcity in the Horn of Africa. These integrated systems combine agroforestry practices with small-scale hydroelectric power generation, providing a reliable source of clean energy and water for irrigation.

The benefits of this approach include:

* Improved water retention and recharge through increased vegetation cover
* Enhanced ecosystem services such as pollination, pest control, and carbon sequestration
* Increased agricultural productivity due to improved soil health and water management
* Reduced vulnerability to climate variability

## Policy Recommendations and Future Research Directions

To support the transition to clean cooking practices in peri-urban households, we recommend:

1. **Public-private partnerships**: Collaborative efforts between governments, private sector companies, and local communities to develop and implement agroforestry-based micro-hydro systems.
2. **Regulatory frameworks**: Development of policies and regulations that support the adoption of integrated agroforestry-micro-hydro systems, including incentives for clean energy production and water conservation.
3. **Stakeholder engagement**: Involvement of local communities, farmers' organizations, and other stakeholders in the decision-making process to ensure ownership and maintenance of these systems.

Future research directions should focus on:

1. **Economic feasibility studies**: Assessing the economic viability of integrated agroforestry-micro-hydro systems.
2. **Long-term impact assessments**: Evaluating the long-term effects of these systems on water security, climate resilience, and agricultural productivity.
3. **Replication and scaling up**: Identifying successful projects in similar contexts and exploring opportunities for replication and scaling up to other regions.

## Closing Outlook

The transition to clean cooking practices is critical for addressing water scarcity, promoting economic development, and enhancing climate resilience in peri-urban households of the Horn of Africa. Agroforestry-based micro-hydro systems offer a promising solution that integrates water security, clean energy, and climate adaptation strategies. By supporting policy makers, private sector companies, and local communities in adopting these integrated systems, we can contribute to achieving the adaptation targets outlined in Nationally Determined Contributions (NDCs) and building a more resilient future for the region.
