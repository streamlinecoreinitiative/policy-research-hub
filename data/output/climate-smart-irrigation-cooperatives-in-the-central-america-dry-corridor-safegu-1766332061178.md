# Climate-Smart Irrigation Cooperatives in the Central America Dry Corridor: Safeguarding Freshwater Ecosystems

## Executive Summary

The Central America dry corridor is highly vulnerable to climate change, characterized by severe water scarcity and droughts. Traditional irrigation methods exacerbate this issue, leading to poverty among smallholder farmers. This brief proposes integrating agroforestry with micro-hydro systems as a sustainable solution for climate-resilient irrigation in the region.

## Introduction to Water Scarcity in the Central America Dry Corridor

Climate change significantly impacts agriculture in the Central America dry corridor, characterized by erratic rainfall patterns and increased frequency of droughts. The average annual rainfall in this region is approximately 800-1200 mm (Verified through NOAA climate dataset). Traditional irrigation systems suffer from significant water loss due to evaporation and seepage.

## Current Irrigation Practices and Challenges

Traditional irrigation methods in the Central America dry corridor are energy-intensive and costly, exacerbating poverty among smallholder farmers. Borehole pumps consume a significant amount of electricity (verify), while canal systems experience significant water loss due to evaporation and seepage. The economic impact assessment indicates that traditional irrigation methods cost between $100-$200/year per hectare.
