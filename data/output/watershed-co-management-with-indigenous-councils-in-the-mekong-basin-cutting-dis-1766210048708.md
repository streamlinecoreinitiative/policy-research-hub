# Watershed Co-Management with Indigenous Councils in the Mekong Basin: Enhancing Environmental Resilience for Vulnerable Groups

## Executive Summary

The Mekong River basin is home to over 60 million people, many of whom are vulnerable to climate-related disasters due to its unique geography and rapidly changing environmental conditions. This brief explores the potential benefits of watershed co-management with indigenous councils in reducing disaster losses in lower-income countries.

## Environmental Resilience in Lower-Income Countries: Water Security, Clean Energy, and Climate Adaptation

### Overview of the Mekong Basin's Environmental Challenges

*   The Mekong River basin is characterized by:
    *   High population density
    *   Limited access to clean water and sanitation facilities
    *   Frequent flooding and droughts due to climate change
    *   Rapid deforestation and land degradation
*   These challenges exacerbate poverty, food insecurity, and social inequality in the region

### Importance of Watershed Co-Management with Indigenous Councils

*   Indigenous communities have traditional knowledge and practices that can inform modern conservation efforts
*   Collaborative watershed management can:
    *   Improve water quality and quantity
    *   Reduce erosion and landslides
    *   Enhance biodiversity and ecosystem services
    *   Support climate change adaptation and resilience building

### Key Benefits of Watershed Co-Management with Indigenous Councils

*   Improved water security through sustainable management practices
*   Enhanced clean energy production from hydroelectric power
*   Increased climate resilience through adaptive land use planning
*   Empowerment of indigenous communities through participatory decision-making processes
