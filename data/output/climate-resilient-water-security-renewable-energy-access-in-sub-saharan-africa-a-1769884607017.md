# Climate Resilient Water Security, Renewable Energy Access in Sub-Saharan Africa: A Call for Action

**Climate Resilient Water Security, Renewable Energy Access in Sub-Saharan Africa: A Call for Action**

## Executive Summary

Sub-Saharan Africa faces a critical challenge in ensuring water security and access to clean energy due to its limited resources and climate vulnerability. With only 32.4% of the population having access to safely managed drinking water (World Bank Open Data, 2024) [1] and less than 10% of total final energy consumption coming from renewable sources ([VERIFY]), urgent action is needed to address these issues. This policy brief recommends implementing policies that prioritize safe water supply and renewable energy access through community-level interventions such as rainwater harvesting systems and micro-insurance programs for smallholder farmers.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa has the lowest levels of safely managed drinking water (32.4%) compared to other regions globally, with 2.2 billion people worldwide lacking access to safe water ([VERIFY] UN-Water/WHO, 2022). Despite global efforts, only 53.3% of the population in Sub-Saharan Africa has access to electricity, significantly lower than the global average of 91.6% (World Bank Open Data, 2023) [1]. Climate change is exacerbating these challenges by causing more frequent droughts and extreme weather events, threatening water resources and power generation infrastructure.

## Analysis

The region's limited access to safe drinking water has severe health implications, leading to higher incidences of waterborne diseases. For example, the World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that 397 million people in Sub-Saharan Africa lack access to basic sanitation facilities [2]. Despite its vast potential, Sub-Saharan Africa lags behind other regions in terms of renewable energy consumption, partly due to inadequate infrastructure and high costs for solar power systems.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Increase Investment in Water Infrastructure**:
   - Invest in rainwater harvesting systems, particularly in rural areas where access is most limited.
   - Encourage private sector investment through tax incentives and subsidies for water infrastructure projects.
2. **Promote Renewable Energy Technologies**:
   - Invest in solar power plants and micro-hydro systems to provide reliable energy sources while reducing carbon emissions.
   - Implement feed-in tariffs or other mechanisms to ensure affordability and encourage the deployment of renewable energy technologies.
3. **Enhance Climate Resilience Measures**:
   - Develop early warning systems for droughts and floods to help communities prepare for adverse weather events.
   - Support research on climate-resilient agricultural practices that can sustain crop yields under changing climatic conditions.

## Implementation Considerations

- Cost Analysis: Initial investment costs may be high, but long-term savings from reduced waterborne diseases and lower energy bills make it financially viable. However, subsidies or low-interest loans are necessary to reduce upfront burdens for communities.
- Barriers and Solutions:
   - **Political Will**: Policymakers must prioritize these issues in their agendas and engage with stakeholders including NGOs, private sector entities, and community leaders.
   - **Technical Constraints**: Ensure that the technology is adapted to local conditions (e.g., solar panels in arid regions) to maximize its effectiveness.

## Conclusion

Ensuring climate-resilient water security and renewable energy access in Sub-Saharan Africa not only addresses immediate challenges but also contributes significantly to achieving sustainable development goals. By taking concrete steps now, we can create a more resilient future for the region. We call on governments, international organizations, and NGOs to collaborate on implementing these recommendations through pilot projects followed by scaled-up efforts.
