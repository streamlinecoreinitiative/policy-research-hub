# nature-based solutions for landslide risk in the Indus watershed: cutting disaster losses for vulnerable groups

**Nature-Based Solutions for Landslide Risk in the Indus Watershed: Enhancing Environmental Resilience**

**Executive Summary**

The Indus watershed is a highly vulnerable region to landslides, affecting millions of people and causing significant economic losses. Climate change, population growth, and unsustainable land use practices exacerbate these risks. This brief explores nature-based solutions that integrate agroforestry and micro-hydro systems to enhance environmental resilience and reduce landslide risk in the Indus watershed.

**Section 1: Introduction to Water Scarcity and Landslide Risk in the Indus Watershed**

The Indus watershed spans across four countries (Pakistan, India, China, and Afghanistan) and is home to a large population. Climate change has altered precipitation patterns, leading to increased frequency and severity of landslides. The region's fragile ecosystem makes it prone to landslides triggered by heavy rainfall, deforestation, and soil erosion.

**Section 2: Current Practices and Challenges**

Existing irrigation systems in the Indus watershed are inefficient, relying heavily on groundwater extraction, which exacerbates land subsidence and reduces water tables. Limited access to electricity hinders the adoption of micro-hydro systems, while unsustainable agricultural practices contribute to soil degradation and increased landslide risk.

**Section 3: Agroforestry-Based Micro-Hydro Systems for Climate-Resilient Water Supply**

Agroforestry practices have been shown to enhance soil fertility, improve water retention, and reduce erosion. Integrating agroforestry with micro-hydro systems can provide sustainable water management solutions, reducing reliance on groundwater extraction.

**Section 4: Policy Recommendations and Future Research Directions**

To address landslide risk in the Indus watershed:

* Governments should prioritize policy support for integrated agroforestry-micro-hydro projects.
* NGOs and private sector organizations should collaborate to develop and implement these solutions.
* Further research is needed on:
	+ System design and cost-effectiveness
	+ Impact assessment of climate-resilient irrigation systems
	+ Policy frameworks supporting large-scale adoption

**Actions/Recommendations:**

1. Governments should allocate funds for integrated agroforestry-micro-hydro projects in the Indus watershed.
2. NGOs and private sector organizations should collaborate to develop and implement these solutions.
3. Research institutions should conduct further studies on system design, cost-effectiveness, and impact assessment.

**Closing Outlook**

The integration of agroforestry and micro-hydro systems offers a promising approach to reducing landslide risk in the Indus watershed. By promoting climate-resilient irrigation and sustainable water management practices, we can mitigate disaster losses and support vulnerable communities. Collaboration between governments, NGOs, private sector organizations, and research institutions is essential for scaling up these nature-based solutions.
