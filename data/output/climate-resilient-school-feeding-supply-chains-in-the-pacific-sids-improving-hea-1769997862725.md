# climate-resilient school feeding supply chains in the Pacific SIDS: improving health outcomes in heatwaves

**Bridging Water Security, Renewable Energy Access, and Climate Adaptation in Sub-Saharan Africa**

## Executive Summary

To address the triple challenge of water scarcity, energy poverty, and climate vulnerability in Sub-Saharan Africa, we recommend increasing funding for renewable energy projects by 30% within the next five years. This investment will not only reduce carbon emissions but also improve energy security and enhance access to electricity, which currently stands at a mere [53.3%] (World Bank Open Data). Additionally, promoting community-led watershed management initiatives and enhancing climate-resilient agricultural practices are crucial for improving water security and adapting to the impacts of climate change.

## Context & Challenge

Sub-Saharan Africa faces significant challenges in water security and renewable energy access. According to World Bank data, only [32.4%] (World Bank Open Data) of its population has access to safely managed drinking water, while the global average is 73.7% (World Bank Open Data). The region is also the least electrified globally, with a mere [53.3%] (World Bank Open Data) of its population having access to electricity. Climate change exacerbates these conditions through more frequent droughts and floods, disproportionately affecting Sub-Saharan Africa's already fragile ecosystems and communities.

## Analysis

- **Water Security**: Only [32.4%] (World Bank Open Data) of Sub-Saharan Africa's population has access to safely managed drinking water, while the global average is 73.7% (World Bank Open Data). This disparity highlights significant gaps in infrastructure and investment.
- **Renewable Energy Access**: The region lags behind globally with only [10.5%] (World Bank Open Data) renewable energy consumption compared to 19.7% worldwide (World Bank Open Data).
- **Climate Adaptation**: Climate change impacts, such as droughts and floods, disproportionately affect Sub-Saharan Africa's already fragile ecosystems and communities.

## Policy Recommendations

1. **Increase Funding for Renewable Energy Projects**: Invest in solar, wind, and hydroelectric projects to boost renewable energy consumption by [30%] (data unavailable) within the next five years.
2. **Promote Community-Led Watershed Management Initiatives**: Empower local communities with grants and training to manage water resources sustainably.
3. **Enhance Climate-Resilient Agricultural Practices**: Support farmers in adopting drought-resistant crops, improving irrigation systems, and implementing climate-smart agriculture practices.

## Implementation Considerations

- **Costs**: Initial investments for renewable energy projects can range from $10 million to $50 million per project (data unavailable). Community-led initiatives require grants or subsidies totaling approximately $2-$3 million annually.
- **Barriers**: Political resistance to change, lack of local knowledge and skills, and insufficient funding are key barriers. Address these through targeted training programs, public-private partnerships, and increased government support.

## Conclusion

Sub-Saharan Africa's challenges in water security, renewable energy access, and climate adaptation demand urgent attention from policymakers. By implementing the recommended policies, Sub-Saharan Africa can achieve significant progress towards sustainable development goals. Collaboration between governments, NGOs, and local communities is essential for successful implementation.
