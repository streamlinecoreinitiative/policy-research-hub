# Water Loss Reduction in Secondary Cities of the Great Lakes Region: Safeguarding Freshwater Ecosystems

## Executive Summary

This brief focuses on reducing water loss in secondary cities of the Great Lakes region by integrating agroforestry with micro-hydro systems, emphasizing the importance of safeguarding freshwater ecosystems. The proposal aims to address severe water scarcity and poverty while ensuring economic benefits and sustainability.

### Introduction

The Great Lakes region is home to approximately [verify] 40% of the world's surface freshwater. Secondary cities in this area face significant challenges related to water security, clean energy, and climate adaptation. Climate change has led to increased evaporation rates, reduced precipitation, and altered temperature patterns, exacerbating existing water scarcity issues.

### Current Irrigation Practices and Challenges

Existing irrigation systems in the Great Lakes region include boreholes, canals, and open channels. However, these methods are often inefficient, with reported water loss percentages ranging from 30% to [verify] 50%. The primary causes of water loss include:

• Evaporation: significant losses due to direct evaporation from water surfaces
• Seepage: substantial losses through infiltration into the soil
• Leaks and breaks: frequent occurrences in aging infrastructure

Micro-hydro technology involves the use of small-scale hydroelectric systems, which offer several benefits:

• Reduced reliance on diesel generators

• Lower operating costs compared to traditional irrigation methods
