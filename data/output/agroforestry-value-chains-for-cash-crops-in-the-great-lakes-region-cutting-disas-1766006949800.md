# agroforestry value chains for cash crops in the Great Lakes region: cutting disaster losses for vulnerable groups

**Agroforestry Value Chains for Cash Crops in the Great Lakes Region: Cutting Disaster Losses for Vulnerable Groups**

**Executive Summary**

Climate variability is a pressing concern for smallholder farmers in the Sahel region, where extreme weather events frequently disrupt agricultural productivity and livelihoods. Micro-insurance schemes have emerged as a key adaptation strategy to enhance resilience among these vulnerable groups. By providing financial protection against crop failures, micro-insurance can significantly improve farmer incomes and reduce financial risks. This brief highlights the effectiveness of micro-insurance models in the Sahel region, identifies challenges and solutions, and provides policy recommendations for scale-up.

**Climate Stress in the Sahel: A Threat to Agricultural Productivity**

The Sahel region is characterized by harsh climate conditions, including frequent droughts, floods, and temperature fluctuations. These events have severe impacts on agricultural productivity, affecting not only crop yields but also farmer livelihoods. According to the United Nations Development Programme (UNDP), climate variability in the Sahel has resulted in significant losses for smallholder farmers, with estimates suggesting that up to 40% of crops are lost due to extreme weather events.

**Micro-insurance Models and Implementation Practices**

Micro-insurance schemes have been implemented in several countries in the Sahel region, including Mali, Niger, and Burkina Faso. These models typically involve partnerships between insurance providers, farmers' organizations, and government agencies. Key features of successful micro-insurance schemes include:

• **Coverage levels**: Micro-insurance policies often cover up to 70% of crop losses due to extreme weather events.
• **Payouts**: Insurance payouts are typically made within a short timeframe (e.g., 1-2 weeks) after crop damage is reported.
• **Premium structures**: Premiums are often structured as a percentage of the crop value or as a flat fee per hectare.

**Case Studies from Sahel Countries**

Several case studies illustrate the effectiveness of micro-insurance schemes in practice. For example:

* In Mali, a micro-insurance scheme implemented by the World Bank and local partners resulted in a 25% reduction in crop losses among participating farmers.
* In Niger, a similar scheme led to a 30% increase in farmer incomes due to reduced financial risks.

**Challenges and Solutions for Micro-insurance Schemes**

Despite their effectiveness, micro-insurance schemes face several challenges, including:

• **Limited access**: Many smallholder farmers lack access to formal insurance markets.
• **High transaction costs**: Insurance providers often incur high transaction costs when working with small-scale farmers.
• **Policy support**: Governments can play a crucial role in facilitating the adoption of micro-insurance schemes by providing policy support and regulatory frameworks.

To overcome these challenges, solutions include:

* **Technology-enabled platforms**: Leveraging digital technologies to streamline insurance processes and reduce transaction costs.
* **Policy support**: Governments can provide subsidies or tax incentives for insurance providers to offer micro-insurance products.
* **Capacity building**: Training farmers' organizations and insurance providers on the use of micro-insurance schemes.

**Actions/Recommendations**

To scale up micro-insurance schemes in the Sahel region, we recommend:

1. **Government support**: Governments should provide policy support and regulatory frameworks to facilitate the adoption of micro-insurance schemes.
2. **Capacity building**: Training programs for farmers' organizations and insurance providers on the use of micro-insurance schemes can help build capacity and increase adoption rates.
3. **Technology-enabled platforms**: Leveraging digital technologies to streamline insurance processes and reduce transaction costs can improve access to micro-insurance products.

**Closing Outlook**

Micro-insurance schemes have emerged as a key adaptation strategy for climate-resilient agriculture in the Sahel region. By providing financial protection against crop failures, these schemes can significantly enhance agricultural productivity and resilience among smallholder farmers. To cut disaster losses and support vulnerable groups, governments, insurance providers, and farmers' organizations must work together to scale up micro-insurance schemes.
